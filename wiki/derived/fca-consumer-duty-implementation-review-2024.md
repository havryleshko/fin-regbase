# FCA Consumer Duty Implementation Review — Good Practice and Areas for Improvement (February 2024)

## What This Document Is

Published February 2024 — seven months after the July 2023 go-live for open products — this is the FCA's first post-implementation review of Consumer Duty compliance across the regulated population. It builds on earlier reviews of implementation plans and fair value frameworks.

It is empirical: the FCA is reporting what it has actually observed in firms, not reiterating what guidance says. Good practice examples are things firms have done; poor practice examples are failures the FCA has found in the field. Both sets are directly usable as compliance benchmarks.

The FCA states explicitly: firms should aim to **continuously address issues** that risk causing consumer harm, and should be **proactive** rather than waiting for the FCA to intervene.

---

## Culture and Governance

### Good Practice Observed

- Firms altering their stated company purpose to signal to staff that actions and behaviour should focus on customer outcomes.
- Accelerating business changes to deliver better outcomes — including greater attention to call abandonment rates, root-cause analysis of complaints (see [[complaints-handling-framework]] for the DISP 1.3.3R hard rule on root cause analysis), and customer satisfaction surveys, with action taken to improve standards.
- Ensuring responsibility for good customer outcomes is understood across the whole business — not just risk and compliance, but product design through to post-sale support.
- Increased board-level focus on the customer, with senior leadership giving serious consideration to what the Duty means in practice and culturally.
- Updating staff bonus structures to align incentivisation with the aims of the Duty.
- Developing new data and metrics to better understand customers, with governance to ensure action is taken where problems are identified.

### Poor Practice Observed

- **Duty driven only by compliance teams:** Where the Duty is primarily owned by programme or compliance teams and is not discussed at board level. Firms must ensure focus on good customer outcomes is embedded in strategies, leadership and people policies at all levels.
- **"Wait and see" behaviour:** Firms waiting to see if the FCA will intervene rather than tackling issues themselves. The FCA states this is likely to cause greater complexity in the long run, particularly if consumer redress becomes due. The Duty requires proactive identification and remediation of risks of harm.
- **Inadequate data and monitoring:** Firms that assume they can repackage existing data without thinking seriously about what information they genuinely need to understand customer outcomes and issues. The FCA explicitly warned against complacency here.

## Vulnerable Customers

### Good Practice Observed

- Considering vulnerability needs at product and service design stage — for example, using 'selfie' identity verification to help elderly customers avoid scams.
- Centralising vulnerability operations, resulting in more consistent handling by specialist staff, better data capture, and improved quality assurance.
- Reviewing communications to support vulnerable customers — alternative formats (braille, audio, enlarged text), typographical changes, adaptations for customers with lower financial literacy or for whom English is not a first language.
- Using customer data (e.g. problematic trading patterns) to identify potential vulnerabilities, routing those customers to trained staff and sending appropriate communications.
- Introducing data flags to identify and monitor outcomes for customers who have requested alternative formats.
- Turning off productivity targets for customer service staff when a customer is identified as vulnerable, to allow time for bespoke support.
- One mortgage firm allowing brokers to pass on details about customers who may need help managing home loans.

### Poor Practice Observed

- **Failing to track vulnerable customers across multiple product sets,** leaving gaps in data and servicing capabilities. Firms must be able to identify where vulnerable groups receive poorer outcomes than others, and act.
- **Wealth management and stockbroking: near-zero vulnerability identification.** The FCA's wealth data survey found 49% of portfolio managers and 69% of stockbrokers identified no vulnerable customers in their customer base. The FCA issued a Dear CEO letter to wealth and stockbroking firms, noting that statistically 50% of people will be classed as vulnerable at some point and it is implausible that every firm in this sector has no vulnerable customers.
- **Blanket age-based vulnerability assessment:** Automatically classifying all customers above a certain age as vulnerable. Age is relevant to vulnerability, but a generalised approach fails to tailor support to individual needs — which the Duty requires.
- **Demanding evidence of vulnerability:** Asking customers who self-identify as vulnerable to provide evidence. This creates a barrier to disclosure and to receiving appropriate support.
- **Warning customers that vulnerability disclosure may affect their service:** Telling customers who identify as vulnerable that it might affect their ability to receive the service. This is a barrier to firms identifying vulnerability and providing the support the Duty requires.
- **Repeated re-disclosure:** Requiring customers to repeatedly disclose their additional needs or personal circumstances when passed between teams. The FCA frames this as foreseeable harm — the impact on a customer's mental wellbeing of repeated disclosure is itself harm.

## Products and Services Outcome

### Good Practice Observed

- Carefully and precisely defining target markets for products that could cause harm if sold to the wrong consumers, then redesigning sales journeys to make suitability clear.
- Refining data and monitoring to track customers who bought a product despite falling outside the target market, enabling proactive contact.
- Adapting products to introduce controls that limit exposure to financial loss.
- Making products previously available only through limited channels available more widely where they meet the needs of a broader customer base.
- Improving product development processes with greater emphasis on consumer research and testing. One firm introduced "11 golden rules" for designing new products linked to Duty requirements.
- Simplifying product ranges to eliminate products with similar or overlapping features, making it easier for customers to identify what they need.
- Investment firm identifying it has institutional clients with retail customers in the chain and offering alternative investment options more suited to those end customers.

### Poor Practice Observed

- **Ineffective information sharing across distribution chains.** Manufacturers not informing distributors of product characteristics, target market and intended value; distributors not providing manufacturers with information needed to review products.
- **Insufficient attention to distribution strategy outcomes.** Where a manufacturer finds third-party brokers are distributing its products outside the intended target market, the manufacturer must consider appropriate action — for example, providing additional information to brokers — even though it is not responsible for the broker's activities.
- **Failure to recognise distribution chain role.** Firms that have material influence over retail customer outcomes but do not have a direct customer relationship failing to recognise that they are in scope of the Duty.

## Price and Value Outcome

### Good Practice Observed

- Examining total costs to consumers (fees, charges, all-in costs) and reducing them where they do not represent fair value — including reducing credit rates for certain customers, removing charges where benefits don't justify them, and capping fees for long-term clients.
- Enhancing product benefits to improve value — increasing savings/investment interest rates, widening insurance policy definitions, increasing cover limits at no additional cost.
- Considering value across the distribution chain — ending agreements with distributors whose charges are too high, capping commission payments, restricting distributor mark-ups.
- Waiving fees entirely where the firm cannot justify the product's cost.

### Poor Practice Observed

- **Benchmarking alone is not a value assessment.** Some firms have relied solely on comparing their product to similar offerings in the market. The FCA states this alone does not prove the customer is getting a good deal. Value assessments must address the nature and benefits of the product, its limitations, and the expected total price paid — not just market comparability.
- **Value statements without qualitative reasoning.** Firms making assertions that their product offers fair value without reasoning that explains *why*. The FCA expects qualitative explanation, not conclusions.
- **Inability to justify remuneration relative to services provided.** Firms unable to explain their remuneration practices or demonstrate proportionality to the work they do. The FCA expects this to be articulable.
- **Distribution chain fee stacking.** Multiple parties in long or complex chains adding fees that cumulatively result in the end consumer not receiving fair value. This is specifically flagged as a risk in complex chains.
- **Charging for undelivered services.** Firms providing — and charging for — ongoing services (such as annual reviews) that customers are not actually receiving. The FCA describes the worst examples as customers paying for a service and not receiving it at all.
- **Firm-focused value assessments.** Value assessments structured to justify the firm's economics rather than viewed through the customer lens. Some firms failing to consider amounts customers invest and the value they receive relative to risks they are taking.

## Consumer Understanding Outcome

### Good Practice Observed

- Working with experts to improve communications — simplifying language (one firm created a 'jargon buster' library), changing layout and presentation, improving website accessibility.
- Developing customer understanding frameworks that consider how marketing could exploit behavioural biases and then moderating those approaches for higher-risk customer profiles.
- Redesigning customer journeys based on how consumers actually behave — adding 'in the moment' prompts and push notifications where customers could incur unexpected fees or charges; an insurer highlighting policy exclusions before customers start applications.
- Proactive communications using data to identify customers not earning enough interest/cashback to cover product fees, making them aware of their options and monitoring the impact on behaviour.
- Testing customer understanding through surveys, experiments, and interviews — one funeral plan provider tested 14 documents with focus groups before finalising them.

### Poor Practice Observed

- **Exploiting long-term client relationships to downplay portfolio risk.** Investment portfolio managers taking advantage of established relationships to play down risks in portfolios not aligned to client risk profiles. The FCA states all firms, including execution-only, must promote products in a way that supports good outcomes.
- **Unclear or absent charge disclosure.** Firms unclear with customers about what charges apply and when. The FCA suggests providing worked examples of product and service costs as one approach.
- **Financial promotion approvers not referencing the Duty.** Firms that approve financial promotions — including promotions for qualifying cryptoassets — making little reference to the Duty. Firms must consider Duty requirements when approving financial promotions.

## Consumer Support Outcome

### Good Practice Observed

- Ensuring the same level of support offered to new customers is available to existing customers.
- Reviewing customer journeys and removing sludge practices — one consumer credit firm removed certain retention incentives and changed how customers take out credit online. Firms also enabling exit through a wider range of channels.
- Introducing positive interventions — a consumer credit firm now directs online customers to speak with an agent where there are signs they may struggle with repayments. Other firms have introduced flexible repayment plans.
- Creating additional engagement touchpoints, including one firm committing in its terms and conditions to notify customers if a savings account with a better interest rate becomes available.
- Taking steps to ensure outsourced support meets the Duty standard — including switching providers where service challenges were identified.
- One firm identifying that Power of Attorney holders could not service all products the same way customers could, and making changes to give them the same options as account holders.

### Poor Practice Observed

- **Insufficient staff training for complex conversations.** Firms not training staff well enough to handle complex conversations with customers — particularly those in financial difficulty — meaning customers may not receive appropriate forbearance or signposting to other support.
- **Gamification on online trading platforms.** Business practices akin to gamification on trading applications that encourage risky short-term trading, including brokerage deals that may not deliver fair value. The FCA states firms must design and deliver customer journeys that support good outcomes — not practices that exploit customers' behavioural tendencies for commercial gain.
- **Failure to understand individual circumstances of customers in financial difficulty.** Firms not taking time to understand customer circumstances, leading to forbearance solutions that are inappropriate for the individual or failure to signpost to relevant support — including support for vulnerable customers. This can cause non-financial harm (stress, unnecessary delays).
- **Inadequate fraud and cyber protection.** Firms without sufficiently robust systems to protect customers from loss of investments, savings or personal data due to fraud or cyber-attacks. The FCA frames this as both a foreseeable harm failure and a consumer support failure: firms must have robust controls *and* provide prompt and appropriate support for fraud victims.

---

## Key Points for Agents

- This is the FCA's first empirical review of post-go-live Consumer Duty compliance (February 2024). Good/poor practice examples reflect what the regulator has actually observed — not theoretical guidance.
- The FCA explicitly called out "wait and see" behaviour as unacceptable: proactive identification and remediation is a Duty requirement, not an optional quality.
- Five named vulnerability poor practices are new: blanket age classification; demanding evidence of vulnerability; warning customers disclosure may affect their service; repeated re-disclosure between teams; and wealth management firms identifying implausibly low or zero vulnerable customers.
- Gamification on trading platforms is named as a consumer support failure — a new harm category not in PS22/9 or FG22/5.
- Charging for services not delivered (e.g. annual reviews that don't happen) is a named price and value breach.
- Fraud victim support is framed as a consumer support obligation, not only a foreseeable harm one.

# Consumer Duty — Product Lifecycle Checklist

## Purpose

This page provides a structured checklist of manufacturer obligations across the full product lifecycle under the Consumer Duty (PS22/9) and PROD 3. Distributor obligations are noted where they interact with or depend on manufacturer actions.

The checklist follows the lifecycle chronologically: design → approval → distribution → ongoing review → material change → withdrawal. Obligations within each stage are grouped by the four Consumer Duty outcomes where relevant.

An agent assessing a firm's Consumer Duty compliance posture, or working through a specific product's lifecycle, should use this checklist as a traversal template. All items marked **[HARD]** are binding rules. Items marked **[EXPECTED]** reflect FCA supervisory expectations articulated in FG22/5 or PS22/9 and will be evaluated in supervisory review.

---

## Stage 1 — Product Design

### 1.1 Products & Services Outcome

**[HARD] Define the target market** **(PROD 3.2.10R; PS22/9 §4.2)**
- Identify the positive target market: the group of end clients for whose needs, characteristics and objectives the product is compatible.
- Identify the **negative target market**: who the product is **not** compatible with. This is mandatory and separate from the positive target market definition. A target market assessment without a negative target market is incomplete.
- Document: client type, risk/reward compatibility, intended distribution strategy.

**[HARD] Assess product/service suitability for the target market** **(PS22/9 §4.3)**
- The product must meet the needs of customers in the target market, not merely avoid causing harm.
- Firms must consider whether the product is genuinely designed to serve those customers' needs, characteristics and objectives.

**[EXPECTED] Conduct conflicts of interest review** **(SYSC 10; PRIN 8)**
- Assess whether product characteristics (charges, structure, investment objectives) benefit the firm at the expense of the investor.
- Assess whether any product feature creates incentives to favour one group of investors over another (e.g. different share classes with different fee structures).

**[EXPECTED] Vulnerability integration in target market definition** **(PS22/9 §6.9; FG22/5)**
- Consider whether any vulnerable customers are within or near the target market.
- Where vulnerable customers are in the target market, the product must be designed to meet their needs throughout the lifecycle — not only at point of sale.

### 1.2 Price & Value Outcome

**[HARD] Conduct manufacturer value assessment** **(PS22/9 §5.2)**
- Assess whether the price charged for the product or service is reasonable relative to the benefits it provides.
- The assessment is at the **product level**, not at the level of individual customers or transactions.
- Document the assessment and the methodology.

**[HARD] Assess cumulative charges through the distribution chain** **(PS22/9 §5.4)**
- Where the product will be distributed through intermediaries, the manufacturer must consider the total cost to the end customer, including distributor charges layered on top of manufacturing charges.
- The manufacturer value assessment must take into account known or expected distribution charges.

**[EXPECTED] Identify named poor-value practices** and confirm they do not apply:
- Benchmarking alone (comparing charges to market average) does not constitute a value assessment.
- Charging for services not delivered.
- Fee stacking — layering charges that individually appear reasonable but produce an unreasonable total.

### 1.3 Consumer Understanding Outcome

**[HARD] Ensure product communications are clear, fair and not misleading** **(COBS 4; PRIN 7)**
- Pre-sale materials must not contradict or understate figures in regulated disclosure documents (KID, KIID, SIPP illustrations, etc.).
- Portfolio transaction costs must be included in cost disclosures — omission is a named persistent failure across FCA reviews.

### 1.4 Governance Gate

**[EXPECTED] Product approval by governance committee** **(PROD 3; PS22/9 §4.8)**
- The product must pass a governance gate before launch.
- The second line of defence (compliance/risk) must have a defined role in the approval process — a governance committee where the second-line role is poorly defined does not meet expectations.
- Decision records from the approval gate must be retained. Undocumented challenge creates SM&CR personal liability risk. **(SYSC 9.1.1R)**

## Stage 2 — Product Testing (Pre-Launch)

### 2.1 Scenario and Stress Testing

**[HARD] Conduct product-level scenario testing** **(PROD 3.2.13R)**
- Testing must assess the risk of poor outcomes for consumers and the circumstances in which they may occur.
- Testing is **instrument-specific** — generic market scenarios applied without regard to the specific product's asset class, structure, or investor base are insufficient.

Required coverage:
| Stress dimension | What to test |
|---|---|
| Market volatility | Product performance during periods of market stress |
| Asset-specific stress | Stresses specific to the underlying assets (credit stress, liquidity stress, etc.) |
| Investor concentration | Risk that concentrated redemptions by a few investors harm remaining investors |
| Commercial viability | Whether the product remains commercially viable under stressed conditions |

**Named failures (FCA 2021 review):**
- Scenarios not tailored to the specific product's asset class.
- Over-reliance on backward-looking scenarios — forward-looking risks must be included.
- Failure to consider the firm's own financial strength as a factor in product resilience.

### 2.2 Distribution Strategy Validation

**[EXPECTED] Validate distributor arrangements pre-launch**
- Confirm that identified distributors have client bases consistent with the manufacturer's target market.
- Conduct due diligence on distributors' onboarding processes and AML/suitability procedures.
- Claiming that distributor due diligence adds little value is not acceptable — the FCA has explicitly rejected this position.

## Stage 3 — Launch and Initial Distribution

### 3.1 Manufacturer → Distributor Information Flow

**[HARD] Provide distributors with target market information** **(PROD 3.2.10R; PS22/9 §4.6)**
- Manufacturers must provide distributors with sufficient information to understand the target market — including the negative target market.
- This information must be kept current — stale target market information given to a distributor does not discharge the obligation.

**[HARD] Provide distributors with product cost and value information** **(PS22/9 §5.4)**
- Share the results of the manufacturer value assessment with distributors.
- Manufacturers who identify that a distribution channel is generating poor value for end customers must act — sharing information and then taking no action when poor value is identified is not compliant.

### 3.2 Distributor Obligations (Interacting with Manufacturer)

**[HARD] Distributors must not distribute outside the target market** **(PS22/9 §4.6)**
- Distributors are not permitted to sell a product to customers who fall within the negative target market.
- Distributors must consider their own customer base against the manufacturer's target market definition before distribution.

**[HARD] Distributors must consider cumulative charges** **(PS22/9 §5.4)**
- Before adding fees to a product already assessed by the manufacturer, the distributor must assess whether the cumulative charges remain consistent with the product delivering fair value to the end customer.

### 3.3 Consumer Understanding at Point of Sale

**[HARD] Communications must support informed decisions** **(PS22/9 §6.2)**
- Customers must be given information at the right time, in the right format, and in a way that reflects their communication needs.
- Customers must be able to make informed decisions — information that is technically accurate but presented in a way that prevents understanding does not satisfy the outcome.

**[EXPECTED] Tailor to vulnerable customers within the target market** **(PS22/9 §6.9; FG22/5)**
- Where vulnerable customers are in the target market, ensure point-of-sale communications and processes meet their needs.
- This includes digital accessibility, non-digital alternatives, and time and support to make decisions.

## Stage 4 — Ongoing Monitoring

### 4.1 Distributor → Manufacturer Information Flow

**[HARD] Manufacturers must obtain end-client data from distributors** **(PS22/9 §4.7; PROD 3)**
- Manufacturers need end-client data to monitor whether the product is reaching the intended target market.
- Commercial sensitivity between manufacturers and distributors does not excuse failure to obtain this data.
- Where a distributor does not cooperate, the manufacturer must **document the challenge** — the attempt itself is evidence of compliance. Silence in the face of non-cooperation is not.
- The most problematic channel is **pooled nominee accounts for execution-only clients** — manufacturers must have a strategy for obtaining MI through these structures.

**[EXPECTED] Maintain management information (MI) systems** **(FCA 2015 TCF-MI guide)**
- Systems and procedures for monitoring internal distribution data must be maintained.
- MI should allow the firm to identify whether the product is reaching the target market and whether customers are experiencing the expected outcomes.

### 4.2 Ongoing Product Review

**[HARD] Periodic product review — active products** **(PS22/9 §4.8)**
- Manufacturers must regularly review products to assess whether they continue to meet the needs of the target market.
- Review must consider: whether product performance is consistent with what was expected at design; whether harm is occurring or at risk of occurring; whether the distribution channel remains appropriate.

**[HARD] Periodic value reassessment** **(PS22/9 §5.6)**
- The manufacturer value assessment is not a one-time exercise at launch. It must be repeated periodically.
- Triggers for reassessment: material changes in market conditions, cost structure, distribution channels, or evidence of poor outcomes.

**[EXPECTED] AFM Board oversight** **(PROD 3; FCA 2021 review)**
- The AFM Board must receive and interrogate the output of product governance committees and second-line review functions.
- Independent NEDs must challenge across all areas of product governance — challenge limited to a NED's area of personal expertise is a named FCA failure.

### 4.3 Consumer Support Outcome — Ongoing

**[HARD] Support must be proportionate and accessible** **(PS22/9 §7.2)**
- Customers must be able to access support that meets their needs.
- Support processes that deter customers from seeking help (e.g. lengthy hold times, unclear escalation paths, digital-only for customers who need other channels) breach this outcome.

**[EXPECTED] Identify vulnerability signals post-sale** **(PS22/9 §7.4)**
- Firms must have processes to identify customers who have become vulnerable after the point of sale.
- A customer who was not vulnerable at onboarding may develop vulnerabilities — the firm's processes must be capable of identifying and responding to these.

## Stage 5 — Material Change

### 5.1 Trigger Assessment

A material change to a product, its price, or its distribution requires a reassessment of all applicable obligations:

| Change type | Obligations triggered |
|---|---|
| Fee increase / charge restructure | Manufacturer value reassessment; distributor notification of updated charges; distributor cumulative charge review |
| Change to target market | Full target market redefinition (positive + negative); distributor notification; communication to existing customers if impacted |
| Change to distribution channel | Distributor due diligence on new channel; assessment of whether target market information is adequate for the new channel |
| Material change to product terms | Customer communication assessment (understanding outcome); value reassessment if terms affect benefit/price relationship |

**[EXPECTED] Consider customer impact before acting unilaterally** **(PS22/9 §6.10)**
- Where a firm proposes to withdraw or materially change a product, it must consider the impact on existing customers before acting.
- Withdrawing a product without considering the impact on existing customers who hold it — particularly where switching is difficult — is a named FCA concern.

## Stage 6 — Product Withdrawal

### 6.1 Closed Product Obligations

When a product is closed to new business, it does not exit the Consumer Duty framework entirely.

**[HARD] High-level cross-cutting rules continue to apply to closed products** **(PS22/9 §4.9)**
- The firm must act to deliver good outcomes for customers who hold the closed product.
- The four outcomes (Products & Services, Price & Value, Consumer Understanding, Consumer Support) continue to apply at a level appropriate to the firm's ability to affect outcomes in a closed product.
- Note: the Products & Services outcome obligation on manufacturers to design and review is necessarily reduced for closed products — but the obligation is not zero.

**[EXPECTED] Closed book value review** **(PS22/9 §5.7)**
- The price/value obligation applies to closed products. Where customers in a closed book are paying charges that no longer represent fair value (e.g. because the product has become significantly less competitive or the service has degraded), this must be addressed.
- Named FCA poor practice: continuing to charge customers in a closed book for services that are no longer being delivered.

### 6.2 Withdrawal Process

**[EXPECTED] Notify customers with adequate notice** **(PS22/9 §6.10)**
- Customers must be given adequate notice of withdrawal and practical information about alternatives.
- The notice period and information provided must be proportionate to the difficulty customers would face in switching.

**[EXPECTED] Consider vulnerable customers specifically** **(PS22/9 §7.5)**
- Vulnerable customers who hold a product being withdrawn may need additional support to transition.
- The withdrawal process must have a plan for identifying and supporting these customers.

## SM&CR Accountability

Under SM&CR, accountability for Consumer Duty product governance sits with the Senior Manager allocated responsibility for the Products & Services outcome in the firm's Statement of Responsibilities. **(FSMA 2000 s.66A)**

The Duty of Responsibility applies: where a firm breaches Consumer Duty, the relevant SM must demonstrate they took reasonable steps to avoid the breach. Evidence required:
- Governance committee records showing challenge was made at design and review stages.
- Records of the value assessment and its periodic updates.
- Records of distributor due diligence and information-flow challenge.
- Records of scenario testing and its conclusions.

Undocumented challenge is treated as no challenge under SYSC 9.1.1R. SM personal liability attaches where documentation is absent. See [[sysc9-record-keeping]] and [[smcr-senior-managers-regime]].

## PROD 3 / Consumer Duty Interaction

For investment products, PROD 3 (product governance for MiFID/non-MiFID investment manufacturers) and the Consumer Duty Products & Services outcome are treated as **equivalent** — compliance with one satisfies the other; they are not cumulative. **(PS22/9 §6.5, §6.7)**

For AFMs: PROD 3 applies as **guidance**; binding obligations come from COBS, COLL, and the Principles. Acting in line with PROD 3 demonstrates compliance with those binding obligations.

## Key Points for Agents

- The negative target market (who the product is NOT for) is a mandatory, separate element of the target market definition under PROD 3.2.10R. Near-universal non-compliance was found in the FCA's 2021 review. A target market assessment without it is incomplete.
- The manufacturer value assessment (PS22/9 §5.2) is at the product level and must be updated periodically — it is not a one-time pre-launch exercise. Benchmarking against market average is explicitly insufficient.
- Manufacturer–distributor information flow is bidirectional: manufacturers must provide target market and value information to distributors (→); distributors must provide end-client MI back to manufacturers (←). Commercial sensitivity does not excuse failure in either direction; document all challenge attempts.
- Closed products are not outside Consumer Duty. The high-level cross-cutting rules and Price & Value obligation continue; firms cannot treat a closed book as a compliance-free zone.
- Product withdrawal must consider customer impact before acting unilaterally (PS22/9 §6.10). Vulnerable customers need specific support plans for transition.
- All product governance decisions must be documented. Undocumented challenge at any lifecycle stage creates SM&CR personal liability under SYSC 9.1.1R — the individual accountable for the outcome, not only the firm, is at risk.

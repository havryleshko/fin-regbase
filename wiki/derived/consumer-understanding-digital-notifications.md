# Consumer Understanding Outcome — Digital Investment Platform In-App Notifications

## Scope: Notifications Are Communications

In-app notifications are communications within the scope of the Consumer Understanding outcome. The outcome applies to **all** communications with consumers — verbal, written, online, and in-product. **(PS22/9 §8.7)** There is no carve-out for digital or automated formats.

---

## The Operative Standard

The test is not whether a notification *is* clear, but whether it **supports effective decision-making in practice**. **(PS22/9 §8.11)** The FCA's self-test, directly applicable here: is the platform applying **the same standards and testing capabilities** to its risk-disclosure and information notifications as it applies to notifications designed to drive engagement, trading activity, or product uptake? **(PS22/9 §8.11; FCA implementation review, February 2024)**

---

## The "Customers Intended to Receive" Standard

The FCA removed the "average customer" concept. The platform must assess whether notifications are likely to be understood by **the actual range of customers** in its target market — not a hypothetical typical investor. **(PS22/9 §8.10)**

For a mass-market digital investment platform, this means accounting for customers with lower financial literacy or numeracy. Population baseline: 1 in 7 UK adults have literacy at or below that of a 9–11 year old; 34% have poor numeracy for financial concepts. **(PS22/9 §8.10)** A notification that assumes investment literacy across the entire user base does not satisfy this standard.

Where it becomes apparent in a one-to-one in-app interaction that an individual customer has a characteristic of vulnerability, the platform must adapt its communications. **(PS22/9 §8.10)**

---

## Timing

The outcome requires information to reach customers **at the right time**. **(PS22/9 §8.1)** For in-app notifications, the moment of delivery must correspond to a point at which the customer can act on or process the information. The implementation review cited adding **"in the moment prompts and push notifications during the customer journey to advise of costs"** as good practice. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**

The corollary: a notification disclosing fees or risks that is delivered after the customer has already committed, or that is buried between marketing messages, fails the timing requirement.

---

## Gamification and Behavioural Bias Exploitation

This is the highest-risk area for a digital investment platform.

**Gamification:** The February 2024 implementation review explicitly named **business practices akin to gamification on online trading platforms** as a failure — specifically, designs that encourage risky short-term trading or deals that may not deliver fair value. Push notifications that create urgency around market movements, highlight short-term gains, or use streak or reward mechanics to stimulate trading activity fall directly into this category. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**

**Exploiting behavioural biases:** The good faith cross-cutting rule prohibits firms from designing communications that inappropriately exploit consumers' behavioural biases to create demand or drive activity. **(FG22/5 §5.13)** Loss-aversion triggers ("Your portfolio is down X%"), FOMO messaging ("Trending stocks right now"), or recency-bias amplification are examples of notification design that could constitute a good faith breach.

**What good practice looks like:** One firm in the implementation review identified that its marketing could exploit behavioural biases, then took steps to provide more balanced information and moderate incentivisation for higher-risk customer profiles. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**

---

## Incomplete or Skewed Information

A notification presenting only upside — portfolio performance highlights, opportunity alerts — without corresponding risk or cost information presents customers with **incomplete or skewed information**: a named bad-faith pattern under the cross-cutting rules. **(FG22/5 §5.13)**

**Execution-only status provides no carve-out.** The FCA confirmed in the implementation review that all firms, including execution-only platforms, must promote and present products in a way that supports good outcomes. Non-advisory status does not reduce the Consumer Understanding obligation. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**

---

## Testing Obligation

Testing is a formal obligation, not a best practice. **(PS22/9 §8.11)** The platform must have a coherent approach to testing whether its notification system achieves its purpose — that customers are making better-informed decisions, not just clicking through.

Testing approaches validated by the implementation review: surveys, experiments, interviews, focus groups, and monitoring the impact of communications on customer behaviour. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)** The FCA will ask specifically: how is the firm testing notification effectiveness, and what action is it taking on the results?

Not every notification must be tested before sending — the rule includes a "where appropriate" qualifier **(PS22/9 §8.12)** — but the platform must be able to demonstrate a coherent testing strategy that covers the range of notification types.

---

## Vulnerable Customers

The platform must consider how its notification system serves customers with characteristics of vulnerability — including those with cognitive impairment, low financial literacy, or who are under financial stress. **(PS22/9 §8.10, §10.1)**

This does not require individual tailoring of every notification, but it does require that notification design accounts for the range of needs in the target market and that the platform can demonstrate how it accommodates users at the edges of that distribution. **(PS22/9 §8.10)**

---

## FCA Supervisory Questions for This Outcome

The FCA will ask the following in supervisory interactions. **(FG22/5 Ch.8)**

- Is the platform applying the same standards and testing capabilities to notifications that ensure consumer understanding as to notifications that drive engagement or sales?
- What insights is the platform using to ensure customers receive the right information at the right time to make decisions?
- How is the platform testing notification effectiveness? What action is taken on the results?
- How does the platform adapt notifications for customers with characteristics of vulnerability, and how does it know those adaptations are effective?
- Are notifications equally effective across all channels and device types? How is this tested?
- What data and MI is the platform using to monitor the impact of its notification system on customer outcomes?

---

## Compliance Assessment Checklist

The notification system is consistent with the Consumer Understanding outcome if:

- [ ] Notifications are designed for the actual range of customers, not a typical investor profile
- [ ] Risk, cost and limitation information is as prominent and timely as opportunity or performance information
- [ ] The system is tested for its effect on decision-making quality, not only for engagement or conversion metrics
- [ ] Notifications do not exploit loss aversion, FOMO, or other behavioural biases to drive trading activity
- [ ] Vulnerable customer needs are reflected in notification design and any channel adaptations
- [ ] The platform can evidence all of the above to the FCA on request
- [ ] Financial promotions delivered via notification have been approved with the Duty requirements in mind

## Key Points for Agents

- In-app notifications are communications in scope of the Consumer Understanding outcome — no digital carve-out exists.
- The operative test is whether notifications support effective decision-making, not whether they are technically accurate.
- Gamification in trading apps is a named FCA concern from the February 2024 implementation review — urgency-triggering and activity-stimulating notifications are the primary risk vector.
- Execution-only status does not reduce the obligation: the platform must support good outcomes regardless of whether advice is given.
- Testing is mandatory; the FCA will specifically ask what the platform does with the results of its communications testing.

# FCA FG21/1 — Guidance for Firms on the Fair Treatment of Vulnerable Customers

## What This Document Is

FG21/1 is the FCA's finalised guidance on the fair treatment of vulnerable customers, published February 2021. It predates the Consumer Duty (July 2022) and was issued under the FCA's existing Principles framework — primarily Principle 6 (customers' interests) but also Principles 2, 3, 7, and 9.

Despite predating the Duty, FG21/1 remains fully in force. FG22/5 (Consumer Duty operational guidance) explicitly cross-references FG21/1 for vulnerability obligations, treating it as the baseline vulnerability standard that the Duty builds upon. **(FG22/5 §11.40; PS22/9 §10.8)**

The Guidance applies to all firms where the Principles apply, regardless of sector, in respect of retail customers who are natural persons (individuals, sole traders, unincorporated partnerships — not incorporated bodies). **(FG21/1 §1.11–§1.12)**

---

## Regulatory Basis

The Guidance derives from Principle 6 — Customers' interests: firms must pay due regard to the interests of customers and treat them fairly. **(FG21/1 §1.22)**

Key Principles engaged:

| Principle | Requirement activated |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Principle 2** (Skill, care and diligence) | Firms must have knowledge and skills to treat vulnerable customers fairly |
| **Principle 3** (Management and control) | Adequate processes and controls to manage risk of harm to vulnerable customers |
| **Principle 6** (Customers' interests) | The primary principle — due regard to customers' interests including vulnerable customers |
| **Principle 7** (Communications) | Communications must be clear, fair and not misleading including for vulnerable customers |
| **Principle 9** (Customers: relationships of trust) | Relevant to advisory contexts with vulnerable consumers |

Firms may be asked to demonstrate how their business model, actions and culture ensure fair treatment of all customers, including vulnerable customers. **(FG21/1 §1.26)**

## Key Additions Not Present in PS22/9 or FG22/5

### 1. HLRC Four-Driver Taxonomy and Table 1

FG21/1 Chapter 2 provides the canonical definition of vulnerability as a spectrum of risk driven by four categories — Health, Life events, Resilience, Capability — each with specific characteristics listed in Table 1. **(FG21/1 §2.5–§2.6)**

This is the primary source for what vulnerability looks like in practice. PS22/9 and FG22/5 reference vulnerability as a concept without providing this level of characterisation. See `concepts/vulnerability-drivers-and-taxonomy.md`.

### 2. Scale Data

As at February 2020, 46% of UK adults (24.1 million people) had characteristics of vulnerability. By October 2020 (Covid-19 panel survey) this had risen to 53%. **(FG21/1 §2.7)** These figures are the primary FCA-published statistics on vulnerability prevalence and are used in supervisory assessments.

### 3. TEXAS Model — Staff Disclosure Protocol

FG21/1 Chapter 3 describes the TEXAS model for handling vulnerability disclosures — a five-step protocol built into frontline staff interactions that simultaneously serves the firm's data collection needs and the GDPR requirements for processing special category data. **(FG21/1 §3.18–§3.20)** This model is not described in PS22/9 or FG22/5.

### 4. Policy/Practice Gap — Named Failure Mode

FG21/1 §3.13 names the "policy/practice gap" as a distinct vulnerability failure: firms that have high-level vulnerability policies but whose frontline staff lack the knowledge or skills to implement them. This gap is a specific supervisory finding type distinct from firms that have no policy at all. **(FG21/1 §3.13)**

### 5. Vulnerability MI Catalogue

FG21/1 Chapter 5 provides the most detailed list of MI types firms should collect to monitor outcomes for vulnerable consumers — 10 named types, from cancellation rates to complaint root cause analysis. PS22/9 requires firms to monitor outcomes for distinct customer groups; FG21/1 specifies the mechanism. **(FG21/1 §5.9–§5.12)**

## Relationship to the Consumer Duty

FG21/1 was written under the pre-Duty Principles framework and does not reference Consumer Duty terminology. However:

- PS22/9 §10.8 states that FCA guidance on vulnerable customers (i.e., FG21/1) remains relevant under the Duty despite the disapplication of Principle 6 — because the standards it sets for fair treatment are preserved in the Duty's cross-cutting rules and outcomes.
- FG22/5 Chapter 11 cross-references FG21/1 on data protection obligations for vulnerability-related data processing.
- The four-driver culture model in FG22/5 §10.6 builds on the four-driver vulnerability model in FG21/1 §2.5.

In practice: FG21/1 provides the operational depth on *identifying* and *responding to* vulnerability; PS22/9 and FG22/5 provide the Duty framework within which that response must occur.

---

## Supervisory Approach

Firms should note that supervision of vulnerable customer treatment is integrated into regular supervision — not a one-off exercise. The FCA may ask for information about the treatment of vulnerable customers in any supervisory work relevant to fair treatment of consumers. **(FG21/1 §1.29)**

The Guidance may also be relevant to enforcement proceedings. The FCA may use FG21/1 to assess whether a firm reasonably understood or should have predicted that its conduct fell below the Principles standard. **(FG21/1 §1.31)**

## Key Points for Agents

- FG21/1 is the primary source for the HLRC four-driver taxonomy (Health, Life events, Resilience, Capability) and Table 1 characteristics — PS22/9 and FG22/5 do not contain this taxonomy.
- Scale data: 46% of UK adults had characteristics of vulnerability (February 2020); rose to 53% by October 2020 — the primary FCA-published prevalence figures.
- TEXAS model (FG21/1 §3.18): the endorsed staff protocol for recording vulnerability disclosures while meeting GDPR requirements for special category data.
- Policy/practice gap (FG21/1 §3.13): a named supervisory failure mode — high-level policies that frontline staff cannot implement.
- Vulnerability MI catalogue (FG21/1 Ch.5): the most detailed list of what MI firms should collect — 10 named types from cancellation rates to complaint root cause analysis.
- FG21/1 remains in force under the Consumer Duty; FG22/5 and PS22/9 explicitly cross-reference it.

# MiFID II: Product Governance Review (February 2021)

## Overview

The FCA reviewed product governance practices at **8 asset management firms** (manufacturers) in 2021 to assess how they apply MiFID II's PROD regime throughout the product lifecycle. Firms ranged from £2bn to over £100bn AUM. All case-study products were UK-authorised collective investment schemes (UCITS and NURS), available to retail investors via platforms on both an advised and execution-only basis. Total fund value assessed: approximately £7bn.

The FCA's conclusion: significant scope for improvement across all four areas assessed. The review identified systemic weaknesses in negative target market identification, distributor information flow, product-level stress testing, and governance quality.

---

## The Four Areas

### 1. Product Design

**Negative target market:** Only 1 of 8 firms had attempted to define a negative target market, and that firm could not identify the specific consumer group or drew the boundary incorrectly — defining a negative target market that overlapped with its existing investor base. PROD 3.2.10R requires manufacturers to specify not only who a product is compatible with, but who it is *not* compatible with.

**Conflicts of interest:** All firms had a conflicts framework, but not all were operating effectively. The FCA found that having a framework in place is insufficient — outcomes matter. Firms must consider whether product characteristics (charges, objectives, structure) benefit the firm at the expense of the investor, and whether conflicts create incentives to favour one investor group over another. **(SYSC 10; PRIN 8)**

### 2. Product Testing

**Scenario and stress testing:** All firms provided some evidence of scenario testing, but quality varied significantly. Weaknesses included: insufficient analysis of product-specific characteristics (underlying assets, investor concentration, market volatility impact); over-reliance on backward-looking scenarios; inadequate coverage of commercial viability under stress. PROD 3.2.13R requires manufacturers to assess the risk of poor outcomes and the circumstances in which they may occur, including stressed conditions. **(PROD 3.2.13R; BIPRU 12.4.1R; COLL 6.12; FUND 3.7)**

**Costs and charges disclosure:** A recurrent problem (also identified in the FCA's February 2019 costs disclosure review): costs shown in marketing documents did not match regulatory documents such as UCITS KIIDs; portfolio transaction costs were commonly omitted. **(PRIN 7)**

### 3. Distributors

**Due diligence quality was variable.** Some firms assessed distributor arrangements robustly; others claimed that distributor due diligence adds little value — a position the FCA rejected. Due diligence is necessary to establish whether a distributor's intended product recipient matches the target market.

**Manufacturer–distributor information flow:** All 8 manufacturers faced challenges obtaining end-client data from distributors. The most problematic channel: pooled nominee accounts for execution-only clients. The FCA found that commercial sensitivities — and in some cases the manufacturer's smaller size relative to the distributor — were allowed to take precedence over regulatory obligations. The FCA's view: manufacturers must challenge their distributors for this information and document that challenge; they are not absolved by distributor non-cooperation.

**Management information:** Systems for monitoring internal distribution data varied. The FCA directed firms to its 2015 TCF–MI guide as still-applicable good practice.

### 4. Governance and Oversight

**Product governance committees:** All firms had them; some fell short of expectations. The second line of defence role was often poorly defined, limiting meaningful challenge.

**AFM Board obligations:** The AFM Board has specific PROD oversight obligations. The FCA found variable quality of independent NED contribution — some NEDs limited their challenge to their area of personal expertise, leaving gaps. Reasonable challenge must extend across all areas of product governance, not only where an individual director has domain knowledge.

**Record keeping:** Most firms had poor record keeping. Where decisions, challenge, and oversight activities were undocumented, firms could not recall what had taken place. The FCA explicitly linked undocumented challenge to SM&CR personal liability: individuals accountable for product governance who cannot evidence their oversight are in potential breach of SYSC 9.1.1R.

**Training:** Training quality and focus varied. Not all training adequately covered the needs and outcomes of the end investor — a gap the FCA identified as undermining the effectiveness of product governance.

## Regulatory Context

PROD rules apply to AFMs as **guidance**, not binding rules. The binding product governance obligations for AFMs arise from COBS, COLL, and the Principles (particularly PRIN 6 — customer best interests, PRIN 7 — fair communications, PRIN 8 — conflicts of interest). Acting in line with PROD enables AFMs to demonstrate compliance with these binding obligations. AFMs should also consider the RPPD (Responsibilities of Providers and Distributors for the Fair Treatment of Customers).

Under Consumer Duty, firms complying with PROD 3 for investments satisfy the Products & Services outcome — they are not subject to both regimes simultaneously.

## Key Points for Agents

- Negative target market identification is a PROD 3.2.10R requirement; the 2021 review found near-universal non-compliance. A firm that cannot identify who its product is *not* suitable for has an incomplete target market assessment.
- PROD scenario testing (PROD 3.2.13R) is instrument-level, not firm-level — it assesses how the specific product performs under stress, including asset-specific and investor concentration stresses. It is distinct from firm-level prudential stress testing.
- Manufacturer–distributor information flow failure is systemic: all 8 firms faced challenges. Commercial sensitivity is not a defence. Manufacturers must document their attempts to obtain end-client data from distributors.
- AFM Board NEDs have oversight obligations across all product governance areas — not only in their area of personal expertise. Selective challenge is a named FCA failure.
- Poor record keeping creates direct SM&CR individual liability risk: individuals who cannot evidence challenge or oversight of product governance decisions may be in breach of SYSC 9.1.1R.
- PROD applies to AFMs as guidance; binding obligations come from COBS, COLL, and the Principles. Consumer Duty compliance satisfies PROD obligations and vice versa — not both simultaneously.

# FCA Payment for Order Flow (PFOF) Supervisory Report (April 2019)

## Overview

The FCA reviewed how brokers manage conflicts of interest when they charge commissions from both the client whose order they execute and the liquidity provider on the other side of the trade (Payment for Order Flow). The supervisory work covered 2018/2019: information requested from 15 firms; 12 firms visited. Focus: listed derivatives on the former LIFFE exchange (now traded on Eurex, ICE Futures Europe, and other regulated markets). The report updates preliminary findings from Market Watch 56 (September 2018).

Context: PFOF for retail and professional client business had largely ceased following FCA guidance published in 2012 and thematic findings in TR 14/13 ("Best execution and payment for order flow"). The remaining problem area was Eligible Counterparty (ECP) client business. MiFID II (in force January 2018) tightened the requirements — moving from "reasonable" to "all appropriate" steps to identify, prevent, or manage conflicts of interest, and requiring prevention in the first instance rather than reliance on disclosure.

---

## Key Findings

### Capacity Identification (Transaction-by-Transaction)

Most firms had adopted a general approach of not charging PFOF when sourcing exclusive liquidity for a specific client (Steps 1–4 of the broker transaction lifecycle — see [[payment-for-order-flow]]). However, most brokers did **not** consistently identify the capacity in which they acted on a transaction-by-transaction basis. As a result, they could not reliably identify the conflicts of interest that arose, or confirm they were managing them through their policies and procedures. **(§2.26)**

### Broad Dissemination of Non-Exclusive Liquidity

Some firms described their activity as broadly disseminating non-exclusive liquidity to their full client base — an approach that, with adequate controls, may allow dual-sided charging. These firms relied on "unwritten rules and protocols" to govern order handling, price dissemination, and allocation. **(§2.27)**

The FCA's finding: these protocols were not codified in firms' written conflicts of interest policies, as required under SYSC 10.1 and Article 34(1) of the MiFID Organisational Requirements Regulation. Firms also could not demonstrate that their controls were effective at managing the conflicts. **(§2.36–2.37)**

### Systems and Controls Deficiencies

Compliance functions relied almost exclusively on risk-sensitive manual sampling — typically sampling less than 1% of total transactions. No broker's manual sampling identified any instance of a breach of internal PFOF policies or failures to manage conflicts, inducements, or best execution requirements. The FCA was not satisfied that this level of sampling was sufficient to give confidence that brokers were complying with their own policies. **(§2.32–2.40)**

Firms also failed to demonstrate systems to assess whether brokers had actually disseminated trading interests broadly. For example, no processes existed to compare the timing of broad communications with the timing of executions — a basic test for whether a transaction genuinely involved non-exclusive liquidity. **(§2.42)**

### Named Pretextual Structures

Five arrangements were identified where firms argued PFOF did not apply but where the FCA concluded it did — see [[payment-for-order-flow]] for the full analysis of each. **(§2.17)**

### Overseas Affiliate Routing

Some firms routed UK client orders to overseas affiliates that charged PFOF to liquidity providers. In one case, a firm received payments from its affiliate for directing order flow to it, regardless of client classification. The FCA's position: UK conflicts of interest rules apply at every stage where a UK broker is involved in handling client orders — including when passed to affiliated overseas entities. The FCA had taken, or was considering, enforcement action. **(§2.43–2.48)**

---

## Conclusions and Next Steps

The FCA's summary conclusions **(§3.1)**:
- Nearly all firms have stopped charging liquidity providers when sourcing exclusive liquidity for a specific client, regardless of client classification — this is the correct position
- Firms found it difficult to consistently determine whether their activity legitimately constitutes non-exclusive liquidity broad dissemination
- Firms could take further steps to improve systems and controls for monitoring conflicts in specific business areas
- Some firms routed client orders to overseas affiliates which charged liquidity providers PFOF

The FCA stated it would continue to monitor PFOF compliance as an ongoing supervisory priority, use all available tools including enforcement action for serious breaches, and engage with EU regulators to ensure a consistent international approach. **(§3.2–3.5)**

## Regulatory Framework

| Rule | Obligation |
|------|-----------|
| SYSC 10.1.3R | Identify conflicts of interest that arise in the course of business |
| SYSC 10.1.7R | Prevent or manage conflicts; rely on disclosure only as last resort |
| MiFID Org Regs Article 34(1) | Conflicts of interest policy must be in writing |
| COBS 2.1.1R / 2.1.4R | Act in clients' best interests |
| MiFID II best execution | Take all sufficient steps to achieve best possible result for clients |

## Key Points for Agents

- PFOF is incompatible with MiFID II conflicts of interest rules when a broker sources exclusive liquidity for a specific client — charging that liquidity provider is prohibited regardless of the client's MiFID II classification (retail, professional, or ECP).
- Most firms had stopped PFOF for retail and professional clients before this review; ECP business was the remaining compliance problem.
- Capacity must be assessed transaction-by-transaction — blanket firm-level or market-level categorisations are rejected as insufficient.
- Broad dissemination of non-exclusive liquidity may allow dual-sided charging, but only if all four cumulative conditions are met and the protocols are codified in the firm's written conflicts of interest policy.
- Manual sampling of less than 1% of transactions is insufficient as a control — no firm's sampling identified any breach, which the FCA treated as a red flag about control effectiveness rather than evidence of compliance.
- UK conflicts rules apply when a UK broker is involved at any stage, including routing to overseas affiliates. Group structures do not shift the regulatory obligation.

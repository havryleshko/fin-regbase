# FCA Multi-Firm Review — Firms' CDD Processes and Controls: Our Findings

## What This Document Is

This is the FCA's published summary of a multi-firm review of CDD, EDD and ongoing due diligence controls conducted in 2025. The FCA assessed firms through a questionnaire, desk-based policy/procedure review, customer file reviews, and staff interviews. Evaluation was against the MLR 2017, FCG, SYSC, JMLSG guidance, and FATF guidance.

**Sectors covered:** Asset management, crowdfunding, wholesale banking, contracts for difference, and non-bank lenders — but the FCA explicitly states findings **apply to all firms undertaking CDD (including EDD)**.

**Audience:** Authorised and registered firms; MLROs; senior managers with oversight of financial crime controls; AML practitioners responsible for CDD.

**Note on publication date:** The document carries a published date of 2026-08-04. The review describes work conducted in 2025. Treat the effective date as prospective guidance until the publication date passes.

## Theme 1 — Policies and Procedures

### Findings

**Good practice:**
- Several firms distinguished clearly between standard CDD and EDD in their written policies, specifying when EDD is required for higher-risk customers such as PEPs.
- These same firms had incorporated the changes to domestic PEP treatment introduced on **10 January 2024**. See the FCA's PEP treatment review (July 2024) — flagged for future ingest.

**Named gaps (poor practice):**
- Most firms had documented identity verification procedures, but **few had enough detail or practical guidance for staff** — procedures described the obligation but not how to fulfil it.
- Some policies **did not explain what alternative evidence** can be used and should be obtained when customers lack standard forms of identification. This is a named gap: policies must include alternative ID procedures.
- Some firms lacked sufficient detail on **how often periodic reviews should take place**, and lacked clarity on what steps to take if an event-driven review is triggered.
- Some firms had approval matrices and governance tools, but others **lacked detail on when senior management sign-off was required** or failed to maintain document version control.

### FCA Expectation

CDD policies must contain: (a) criteria distinguishing standard CDD from EDD; (b) alternative ID procedures for customers who lack standard documentation; (c) explicit periodic review frequencies and event-driven review triggers; (d) clear senior management sign-off matrix for high-risk relationships.

## Theme 2 — CDD and EDD Processes

### Findings

**Good practice:**
- Most firms tailored their CDD approach to the risk profile of each customer, ensuring higher-risk customers were subject to enhanced checks and more frequent reviews.
- Stronger firms **documented each stage of the EDD process**, including clear requirements for senior management approval and oversight (e.g. compliance committee involvement).

**Named failures:**
- Some firms **did not gather or record** relevant information, such as the purpose and intended nature of the business relationship — a mandatory element of standard CDD under MLR Reg 27.
- Some firms **failed to evidence and document EDD measures** taken for high-risk customers. In particular, there was limited documentation showing how the approach differed between low and high-risk customers.
- Some firms were **not conducting periodic reviews as required**.

### Failure Pattern — EDD Documentation

The FCA's specific concern is not merely that EDD was inadequate, but that firms could not **demonstrate** how their EDD approach differed from standard CDD. Documentation must evidence:
1. What additional information was obtained
2. Why it was proportionate to the risk
3. That senior management approval was obtained where required

## Theme 3 — Compliance Monitoring and Audit

### Findings

**Good practice:**
- Several firms conducted regular thematic reviews of their CDD framework, including **reviews by external parties or internal audit functions**, with clear cycles for ongoing assessment.
- Some firms used **sample-based compliance monitoring** with proportionate review cycles, ensuring CDD processes remained effective.
- Stronger firms operated **independent third-line testing** that assessed controls across customer onboarding and due diligence, then documented and acted on findings.

**Named failure — Independence Gap:**
- In some cases there was **no independent second-line assurance**: the same staff were responsible for both onboarding customers and reviewing those same customers. The FCA explicitly stated this "raises questions about impartiality and effectiveness of testing."

### Three-Lines Model for CDD

| Line | Role | Failure Mode |
|------|------|-------------|
| First line (onboarding) | Applies CDD controls during onboarding and relationship management | Reduces CDD rigour under commercial pressure |
| Second line (compliance) | Independent assurance of onboarding controls; separate from onboarding staff | Same staff doing onboarding and second-line review — named failure |
| Third line (internal audit / external review) | Independent testing of CDD framework; documents and tracks findings | Absent or unresponsive to findings |

## Key Points for Agents

- CDD policies must document alternative identification procedures for customers who lack standard ID — absence of this is a named regulatory gap.
- Periodic review frequency and event-driven review triggers must be explicit in CDD policy — vague or absent language is a named failure.
- EDD documentation must evidence **how the approach differed from standard CDD** — not merely that EDD was conducted. Undocumented EDD is treated as absent EDD.
- The January 2024 domestic PEP regime change must be reflected in CDD policies. Firms that had not incorporated this at time of review were observed as a gap.
- Second-line compliance assurance must be independent of first-line onboarding staff. Same-staff review is a named failure mode that the FCA flagged as undermining impartiality.
- Third-line testing (internal audit or external review) is an observed good practice; firms should document findings and demonstrate action taken.

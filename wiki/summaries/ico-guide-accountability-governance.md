# ICO Guide to Accountability and Governance

> **Citation type note**
>
> This source is **ICO guidance** — the supervisory authority's authoritative interpretation of UK GDPR accountability obligations, not primary legislation. It carries significant weight in enforcement and litigation but is not itself law.
>
> **DUA Act review status:** This guidance is under review and may be subject to change following the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 coming into law on 19 June 2025. The **Data Protection by Design and by Default** sub-section was updated **5 February 2026** to reflect DUAA changes; all other sections remain under review as of April 2026.
>
> **Scope of this ingest:** This summary covers the **top-level overview page only**. Linked sub-pages (DPbD detailed guidance, Documentation, Contracts, DPIAs, DPO) have not yet been ingested and may contain more granular obligations.

## Overview

Accountability is the seventh and overarching principle of the UK GDPR (Art. 5(2)). It has two elements:

1. **Responsibility** — the controller is responsible for complying with the UK GDPR
2. **Demonstration** — the controller must be able to demonstrate that compliance

Article 24(1) operationalises this: controllers must implement **appropriate technical and organisational measures** to ensure and demonstrate compliance. These measures must be:
- **Risk-based and proportionate** to the nature, scope, context, and purposes of processing
- **Reviewed and updated** as necessary — accountability obligations are ongoing, not one-time

---

## The Eight Accountability Measures

The UK GDPR identifies eight measures that help demonstrate accountability. Some are **mandatory** (depending on the organisation), others are **voluntary best practice**.

| Measure | Article(s) | Mandatory? |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Data protection policies | Art. 24(2) | Where proportionate |
| Data protection by design and by default | Art. 25 | Yes — legal requirement |
| Written contracts with processors | Art. 28 | Yes — whenever a processor is used |
| Records of processing activities (RoPA) | Art. 30 | Yes — most organisations |
| Security measures | Arts. 5(1)(f), 32 | Yes |
| Recording and reporting personal data breaches | Arts. 33–34, 33(5) | Yes |
| Data protection impact assessments (DPIAs) | Arts. 35–36 | Yes — before high-risk processing |
| Data protection officer (DPO) | Arts. 37–39 | Some organisations |

Voluntary additional measures:
- Adherence to **codes of conduct** (Arts. 40–42)
- **Certification schemes** (Arts. 42–43)

---

## Privacy Management Framework

Larger organisations may implement a **privacy management framework** to embed accountability systematically. Required elements:
- Robust programme controls informed by UK GDPR requirements
- Appropriate reporting structures
- Assessment and evaluation procedures

Smaller organisations should take a proportionate approach:
- Good staff understanding and awareness
- Comprehensive but proportionate policies and procedures
- Records of what they do and why

---

## Data Protection Policies (Art. 24(2))

Where proportionate, implementing data protection policies is a named accountability measure. What is needed depends on what the organisation does with personal data. Organisations handling **large volumes** or **special category data** should have robust, comprehensive policies.

Policies alone are insufficient — organisations must also demonstrate **implementation and adherence**: awareness raising, training, monitoring, and audits. These tasks can be allocated to the DPO.

---

## Data Protection by Design and by Default (Art. 25)

DPbD is a **legal requirement**, not merely good practice. It means embedding data protection throughout **all processing operations and the entire lifecycle**.

Named example measures: minimising data collected, pseudonymisation, improving security features.

Decisions made in applying DPbD should be documented — often through DPIAs.

> Updated **5 February 2026** to reflect DUAA changes. See `[[uk-gdpr-data-protection-by-design]]` for full detail.

---

## Written Contracts with Processors (Art. 28)

Every time a controller uses a processor to handle personal data on its behalf, a **written contract is mandatory**. Contracts must include certain **minimum terms**, including:
- The processor must take **appropriate security measures**
- The processor must **assist the controller** in meeting individual rights obligations

See `[[uk-gdpr-processor-contracts]]` for full contractual requirements.

---

## Records of Processing Activities (Art. 30)

Most organisations must maintain a **RoPA** covering: processing purposes, data sharing arrangements, and retention. Documenting this helps compliance with other obligations — knowing what data is held and where makes accuracy and security obligations easier to meet.

Beyond the RoPA, organisations must also maintain:
- **Consent records** (Art. 7(1))
- **Personal data breach records** (Art. 33(5)) — all breaches, regardless of whether reported

See `[[uk-gdpr-records-of-processing]]`.

---

## Data Protection Impact Assessments (Arts. 35–36)

A DPIA is a **legal requirement** before undertaking any processing **likely to result in high risk** to individuals' interests. It is also an essential accountability tool and a core element of the DPbD approach.

A properly conducted DPIA:
- Identifies and minimises data protection risks
- Assesses how to comply with UK GDPR requirements
- Acts as documented evidence of the decision-making process and safeguards applied

See `[[uk-gdpr-dpia]]`.

---

## Data Protection Officer (Arts. 37–39)

Some organisations are **required** to appoint a DPO; for others it is voluntary but strongly advisable.

DPO tasks include:
- Advising on UK GDPR compliance
- Monitoring compliance
- Training staff

Governance requirements:
- Must **report to the highest level of management**
- Must **operate independently**
- Must have **adequate resources** to carry out tasks

Even organisations not required to appoint a DPO must ensure they have sufficient staff, skills, and reporting structures to meet their UK GDPR obligations.

See `[[uk-gdpr-dpo]]`.

---

## Codes of Conduct and Certification (Arts. 40–43)

**Codes of conduct** may be drawn up by trade associations and representative bodies, covering topics such as fair and transparent processing, pseudonymisation, and exercise of individual rights. These are voluntary.

**Certification** can be issued by the ICO or accredited certification bodies, verifying the data protection compliance of products and services. Also voluntary.

Both mechanisms are an **excellent way to verify and demonstrate UK GDPR compliance** and may assist in mitigating enforcement action.

---

## Accountability as an Ongoing Obligation

Accountability is not a box-ticking exercise. Key implications:
- Organisations must be able to **prove the steps taken** to comply — including justifying decisions that the UK GDPR does not explicitly require to be documented (example: compatibility assessments under Art. 6(4))
- Accountability extends to **individuals as well as the regulator** — individuals have rights to be informed, and to contest automated decisions (Art. 22)
- Measures must be **reviewed at appropriate intervals** and updated where no longer fit for purpose

---

## Key Points for Agents

- Accountability (Art. 5(2)) has two elements: **responsibility** (being the one accountable) and **demonstration** (proving it with evidence). Passive compliance is insufficient.
- Art. 24(1) requires measures to be **risk-based, proportionate, and reviewed** — not a fixed checklist.
- **DPbD (Art. 25) is a legal requirement**, not best practice. It was updated 5 February 2026 for the DUAA. See `[[uk-gdpr-data-protection-by-design]]`.
- **Written processor contracts (Art. 28) are mandatory** whenever a processor is used. Missing or inadequate contracts = direct Art. 28 breach, not just a governance gap.
- **DPIAs (Arts. 35–36) are legally required** before high-risk processing — not optional project hygiene.
- DPO appointment is **mandatory for some organisations**; for others, voluntary. In both cases the DPO must report to the highest level of management and operate independently.
- This guidance is under ICO review following the DUAA (June 2025). Only the DPbD sub-section has been updated (Feb 2026) as of the date of this ingest.

# Money Laundering Regulations 2017 — Primary Legislation Reference

## What MLR 2017 Does

MLR 2017 (SI 2017/692) is the primary domestic legislation implementing the Fourth Money Laundering Directive (2015/849/EU) and the Funds Transfer Regulation (2015/847/EU) in the United Kingdom. It came into force on 26 June 2017, replacing the Money Laundering Regulations 2007 (SI 2007/2157) and the Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2007.

The Regulations impose obligations on "relevant persons" (the 8 categories of firm in scope) covering: firm-level risk assessment; policies, controls and procedures; customer due diligence; record-keeping; reliance on third parties; beneficial ownership disclosure; registration with supervisory authorities; and enforcement through civil penalties and criminal offences.

**Statutory definitions:** "money laundering" has the meaning in POCA 2002 s.340(11). "Terrorist financing" is defined by cross-reference to the Terrorism Act 2000, the Anti-Terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001, and the Counter-Terrorism Act 2008. These are the primary legal anchors for the FCA Financial Crime Guide (FCG) and JMLSG guidance citations to "MLR 2017."

## Application — Relevant Persons (Regs 8–15)

### Eight Categories of Relevant Person (Reg 8)

MLR 2017 applies to:

1. **Credit institutions** **(reg 8(1)(a))**
2. **Financial institutions** — entities carrying on one or more Annex I activities (defined by reference to the Capital Requirements Directive, listed in Schedule 2), excluding where undertaken only on an occasional or very limited basis **(reg 8(1)(b); reg 10)**
3. **Auditors, insolvency practitioners, external accountants, and tax advisers** **(reg 8(1)(c))**
4. **Independent legal professionals** when carrying out certain transactions (purchase/sale of real property or business entities; client money management; opening/managing bank/savings/securities accounts; organising contributions for company creation/operation; creating/operating/managing trusts, companies, foundations, or similar) **(reg 8(1)(d))**
5. **Trust or company service providers (TCSPs)** **(reg 8(1)(e))**
6. **Estate agents** **(reg 8(1)(f))**
7. **High value dealers (HVDs)** — accepting (or making) cash payments of €10,000 or more in respect of any single transaction or linked transactions **(reg 8(1)(g); reg 14)**
8. **Casinos** **(reg 8(1)(h))**

The relevant person category determines which supervisory authority applies and whether HMRC registration is required. **FCA authorisation does not automatically confer AML supervision** — a firm may be FCA-authorised for regulated activities but HMRC-supervised for AML if it falls within categories (c), (e), (f) or (g) and is not authorised in a way that makes it an FCA-supervised entity under reg 7.

### UK Business Test (Reg 9)

Three cases trigger UK scope: (a) the relevant person's registered office is in the UK; (b) registered/head office in the UK and day-to-day management carried on in the UK; (c) for casinos, gambling equipment used in Great Britain **(reg 9)**.

### Occasional/Very Limited Basis Exclusion (Reg 15)

A person whose financial activity is on an occasional or very limited basis is excluded if all seven of the following conditions are met:
1. The person's total annual turnover from financial activity does not exceed **£100,000**;
2. The financial activity is limited to transactions that do not exceed **€1,000** per customer per transaction;
3. The financial activity does not exceed **5%** of the person's total annual turnover;
4. The financial activity is **ancillary** to the person's main activity and directly related to it;
5. The financial activity is **not remittance** (money transmission/payment services);
6. The person is not within **reg 8(2)(a)–(f) or (h)** (i.e. not a credit/financial institution, auditor/IP/accountant/tax adviser, ILP, TCSP, estate agent, or casino);
7. The financial activity is provided **only to customers of the main activity** and not offered to the public generally **(reg 15)**.

This exclusion is significant for embedded finance structures and agent arrangements. **All seven conditions must be satisfied simultaneously.**

## Beneficial Owner Definitions (Regs 5–6)

### Bodies Corporate and Partnerships (Reg 5)

A **beneficial owner** of a body corporate (other than one listed on a regulated market) is any individual who:
- Holds, directly or indirectly, **more than 25%** of the shares in the body corporate **(reg 5(1)(b)(i))**;
- Holds, directly or indirectly, **more than 25% of the voting rights** in the body corporate **(reg 5(1)(b)(ii))**;
- Holds the right to appoint or remove the majority of the board of directors **(reg 5(1)(b)(iii))**; or
- **Otherwise exercises ultimate control** over the management of the body corporate **(reg 5(1)(b)(iv))** — including control exercised by holding a controlling interest (within the meaning of Companies Act 2006 s.415B(4)).

For **partnerships** (other than limited liability partnerships): an individual who directly or indirectly holds more than 25% of the capital or profits; more than 25% of the voting rights; or otherwise exercises control. For **Scottish partnerships**: additional tests apply based on the Partnership Act **(reg 5(2)–(4))**.

Where no individual qualifies under the >25% tests, the beneficial owner is the individual who exercises ultimate control through other means, or — where exhausted — the senior person responsible for management **(reg 5(6)–(7))**.

**Statutory note (reg 28(9)):** Relevant persons cannot satisfy their beneficial owner verification requirements solely by relying on the PSC register (Companies Act 2006 s.790M), the LLP PSC register, or the European Public Limited Liability Company register. These registers inform but do not discharge the verification obligation.

### Trusts (Reg 6)

The following are **beneficial owners of a trust**:
- The **settlor** **(reg 6(1)(a))**;
- The **trustees** **(reg 6(1)(b))**;
- The **beneficiaries** **(reg 6(1)(c))**;
- Where individual beneficiaries have not been determined — the **class of persons** in whose main interest the trust is set up or operates **(reg 6(1)(d))**;
- Any individual who **exercises control** over the trust **(reg 6(1)(e))**.

**"Control" over a trust** means a power (whether exercisable alone, jointly, or with the consent of another) to:
- Dispose of, advance, lend, invest, pay, or apply trust property;
- Vary or terminate the trust;
- Add or remove beneficiaries, protectors, or trustees;
- Direct, withhold consent to, or veto the exercise of any of the above powers **(reg 6(2))**.

This is a deliberately broad definition. Any person who holds a veto over trustee decisions is a beneficial owner regardless of whether they hold a formal title.

## Supervisory Architecture (Reg 7)

MLR 2017 divides AML supervisory responsibility between four supervisory authorities:

| Supervisory Authority | Relevant Persons Supervised |
|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **FCA** | Credit institutions; financial institutions that are authorised persons; MSBs that are authorised persons; TCSPs that are authorised persons; Annex I financial institutions; EMIs; auction platforms **(reg 7(1)(a))** |
| **Professional bodies (Schedule 1)** | Members of the 22 listed professional bodies **(reg 7(1)(b))** |
| **HMRC (Commissioners)** | HVDs; MSBs not supervised by FCA or professional body; TCSPs not supervised by FCA or professional body; auditors/external accountants/tax advisers not supervised by professional body; estate agents not supervised by professional body; bill payment service providers; telecom/digital/IT payment service providers not supervised by FCA **(reg 7(1)(c))** |
| **Gambling Commission** | Casinos **(reg 7(1)(d))** |

**Supervisory authority does not follow authorisation status alone.** An FCA-authorised firm that is also an HVD or estate agent may have activities supervised by HMRC for those non-authorised-person activities.

**Schedule 1** lists 22 professional bodies including ACCA, AIA, ATT, CIOT, Council for Licensed Conveyancers, Faculty of Advocates, General Council of the Bar (and Northern Ireland), Insolvency Practitioners Association, ICAEW, ICAS, Institute of Chartered Accountants in Ireland, Law Society (England and Wales, Northern Ireland, Scotland), and others.

## National and Sectoral Risk Assessment (Regs 16–17)

### National Risk Assessment (Reg 16)

The Treasury and Home Office must prepare and maintain a joint national risk assessment (NRA) of ML/TF risks. The completed NRA must be laid before Parliament and sent to the PRA, supervisory authorities, European Commission, European Supervisory Authorities, and each EEA state **(reg 16(6)–(7))**. The NRA must be kept up to date **(reg 16(9))**.

### Supervisory Authority Risk Assessment (Reg 17)

Each supervisory authority must:
- Identify and assess ML/TF risks for the relevant persons it supervises ("its own sector") **(reg 17(1))**;
- Take into account the European Commission reports, ESA guidelines under 4MLD Arts. 17/18.4/48.10, the Treasury/Home Office report, and NRA information **(reg 17(2))**;
- Keep an up-to-date written record of all steps taken **(reg 17(3))**;
- Develop and record in writing risk profiles for each relevant person (or a permitted cluster profile where persons share similar characteristics and risks do not differ significantly) **(reg 17(4)–(6))**; and
- Make information from the risk assessment available to relevant persons in its sector where this would assist their own risk assessment **(reg 17(9))**.

**Five triggers for review of risk profiles (reg 17(8)):** significant external events changing the nature of ML/TF risks; emerging ML/TF risks; findings from other supervisory authorities; changes in the way the sector is operated; significant changes in regulation.

## Firm-Level Obligations (Regs 18–24)

### Firm Risk Assessment (Reg 18)

A relevant person must **take appropriate steps to identify and assess** the risks of ML/TF to which its business is subject **(reg 18(1))**. The risk assessment must take into account risk factors relating to **(reg 18(2))**:
- Customers
- Countries or geographic areas
- Products or services
- Transactions
- Delivery channels

The firm must **keep an up-to-date written record** of all steps taken, unless its supervisory authority notifies it in writing that such a record is not required **(reg 18(4))**. A supervisory authority may only give that notification if the risks applicable to the sector are clear and understood **(reg 18(5))**. The firm must provide the risk assessment to its supervisory authority **on request** **(reg 18(6))**.

### Policies, Controls and Procedures (Regs 19–20)

A relevant person must:
- **Establish and maintain** policies, controls and procedures (PCPs) to mitigate and manage effectively the ML/TF risks identified in any risk assessment **(reg 19(1)(a))**;
- **Regularly review and update** those PCPs **(reg 19(1)(b))**;
- **Maintain a written record** of the PCPs and any changes made on review **(reg 19(1)(c))**;

PCPs must be **proportionate to the size and nature** of the business and **approved by senior management** **(reg 19(2))**.

PCPs must include (reg 19(3)):
- Risk management practices;
- Internal controls (regs 21–24);
- Customer due diligence (regs 27–38);
- Reliance and record-keeping (regs 39–40);
- Monitoring and internal communication of those PCPs.

PCPs must specifically include procedures for **(reg 19(4))**:
- Identification and scrutiny of complex/unusually large transactions, unusual patterns, and transactions with no apparent economic/legal purpose;
- Additional measures to prevent use of products/transactions favouring anonymity;
- Assessment of ML/TF risks from new technology (before and during adoption);
- Internal disclosure obligations consistent with Terrorism Act 2000 Pt 3 and POCA 2002 Pt 7;
- For **MSBs using agents**: assessment of whether each agent satisfies the fit and proper test (reg 58) and the extent of ML/TF risk posed by that agent **(reg 19(4)(e))**.

**Group-level obligations (reg 20):** A relevant parent undertaking must ensure PCPs apply to all UK subsidiary undertakings and branches carrying on MLR-regulated activities. Group-wide data protection and information-sharing policies for AML purposes must be maintained. Where a subsidiary/branch is in a third country that does not impose equivalent requirements and local law prevents application, the parent must inform its supervisory authority and take additional measures to handle ML/TF risk effectively **(reg 20(3)–(4))**.

### Internal Controls (Reg 21)

Where appropriate for the size and nature of its business, a relevant person must:
- **Appoint a board member** (or, if no board, a member of senior management) as the officer responsible for compliance with the Regulations **(reg 21(1)(a))**;
- **Screen relevant employees** — both before appointment and during the course of employment — assessing their skills, knowledge, expertise, conduct and integrity **(reg 21(1)(b))**;
- **Establish an independent audit function** with responsibility to examine and evaluate the adequacy and effectiveness of PCPs; make recommendations; and monitor compliance with those recommendations **(reg 21(1)(c))**; and
- **Nominate a person as the nominated officer** (MLRO) **(reg 21(3))**.

**Notification obligation:** The firm must, within **14 days** of appointment, inform its supervisory authority of the identity of the board compliance officer (reg 21(1)(a)) and the MLRO (reg 21(3)), and of any subsequent appointments to either position **(reg 21(4))**.

**Authorised person MSBs and TCSPs (reg 23):** An authorised person whose supervisory authority is the FCA must, before acting as an MSB or TCSP (or within 28 days of beginning to do so), inform the FCA **(reg 23(1))**. Ceasing to act as an MSB or TCSP requires notification within 28 days **(reg 23(3))**.

**EMI/PSP additional monitoring function (reg 21(7)):** An electronic money issuer or payment service provider must appoint an individual to monitor and manage compliance with, and internal communication of, the PCPs — specifically to identify situations of higher ML/TF risk; maintain a record of PCPs and their operation; apply measures across new products, new customers, and changed business activities; and provide regular information (at least annually) to senior management on effectiveness.

**Central contact points for EEA-headed EMIs/PSPs (reg 22):** An EMI or PSP established in the UK otherwise than by a branch, and with its head office in another EEA state, must appoint a central contact point in the UK when required by its supervisory authority.

### Training (Reg 24)

A relevant person must take appropriate measures to ensure relevant employees are:
- Made **aware of the law** relating to ML/TF and relevant data protection requirements **(reg 24(1)(a)(i))**; and
- **Regularly given training** in how to recognise and deal with transactions and situations potentially related to ML/TF **(reg 24(1)(a)(ii))**.

A **written record** of training measures must be maintained **(reg 24(1)(b))**. In determining appropriate measures, the firm must consider its business nature, size, and the nature and extent of its ML/TF risks; and may take into account FCA or other supervisory authority guidance approved by the Treasury **(reg 24(3))**.

## Customer Due Diligence (Regs 27–38)

### CDD Triggers (Reg 27)

A relevant person **must** apply CDD when it:
- **Establishes a business relationship** **(reg 27(1)(a))**;
- Carries out an **occasional transaction** that amounts to a funds transfer exceeding **€1,000** **(reg 27(1)(b))**;
- **Suspects ML/TF** **(reg 27(1)(c))**; or
- **Doubts the veracity or adequacy** of documents or information previously obtained **(reg 27(1)(d))**.

Additionally:
- Non-HVD/non-casino relevant persons: occasional transactions ≥**€15,000** (whether in a single operation or linked operations) **(reg 27(2))**;
- **HVDs**: occasional transactions in **cash** amounting to ≥**€10,000** **(reg 27(3))**;
- **Casinos**: transactions ≥**€2,000** (including wagering of stakes and collection of winnings) **(reg 27(5))**.

CDD must also be applied at **other appropriate times to existing customers** on a risk-based approach and whenever circumstances relevant to the risk assessment change **(reg 27(8)–(9))**.

### CDD Measures (Reg 28)

Where CDD is required, the relevant person must:
- **Identify the customer** unless already known and verified **(reg 28(2)(a))**;
- **Verify the customer's identity** unless already verified **(reg 28(2)(b))**; and
- **Assess** (and where appropriate obtain information on) the **purpose and intended nature** of the business relationship or occasional transaction **(reg 28(2)(c))**.

**Bodies corporate:** Must obtain and verify:
- Name, company number or other registration number, and registered office address (and principal place of business if different) **(reg 28(3)(a))**;
- The law to which the body corporate is subject, its constitution **(reg 28(3)(b)(i))**;
- The full names of the board of directors (or equivalent management body) and the senior persons responsible for operations **(reg 28(3)(b)(ii))**;
- The identity of the **beneficial owner** and (where the BO is a legal entity) take reasonable measures to understand the ownership and control structure **(reg 28(4))**.

**PSC register limitation (reg 28(9)):** A relevant person does not satisfy its reg 28(4) obligations solely by relying on the PSC register, LLP PSC register, or European Public Limited Liability Company register. These may inform but do not discharge the verification obligation.

**Listed company exception (reg 28(5)):** Where the customer is a company listed on a regulated market, the requirements to verify the law/constitution and identify/verify the beneficial owner do not apply.

**Senior person fallback (regs 28(6)–(8)):** Where the relevant person has exhausted all possible means of identifying the beneficial owner and is not satisfied that the identified individual is in fact the beneficial owner, the relevant person may treat the senior person in the body corporate responsible for managing it as its beneficial owner — but must keep a written record of all actions taken to identify the beneficial owner **(reg 28(8))**.

**Agents and representatives (reg 28(10)):** Where a person purports to act on behalf of the customer, verify that person is authorised to act, identify them, and verify their identity on the basis of documents or information from a reliable source independent of both the person and the customer.

**Ongoing monitoring (reg 28(11)):** The relevant person must conduct ongoing monitoring of the business relationship including:
- Scrutiny of transactions to ensure they are **consistent with the relevant person's knowledge** of the customer, its business and risk profile, including (where necessary) the **source of funds**; and
- **Keeping documents and information up to date** for the purpose of applying CDD.

**Risk calibration (reg 28(12)–(13)):** The extent of CDD measures and their timing must reflect the firm's reg 18(1) risk assessment, and its assessment of the level of risk in the particular case. Relevant factors include: the purpose of the account/transaction/business relationship; the level of assets to be deposited or the size of transactions; and the regularity and duration of the business relationship.

**CDD and tipping-off exception (reg 28(15)–(16)):** Where a relevant person has taken CDD measures in relation to a customer, has made a Part 7 POCA 2002 or Part 3 Terrorism Act 2000 disclosure, and continuing CDD would constitute a tipping-off offence, the relevant person is not required to continue applying CDD in relation to that customer.

**Verification standard (reg 28(18)):** "Verify" means verify on the basis of documents or information from a reliable source which is independent of the person whose identity is being verified. Documents issued or made available by an official body are regarded as independent even if provided to or made available by the relevant person by or on behalf of the customer.

### Additional CDD for Credit/Financial Institutions (Reg 29)

Credit institutions and financial institutions providing long-term insurance contracts must:
- Identify and (where relevant) verify beneficiaries of insurance policies — by name if specified, or by obtaining sufficient information to establish identity before payment **(reg 29(3)–(4))**;
- On any assignment of the policy, identify the new beneficiary **(reg 29(5))**;
- Not set up **anonymous accounts or anonymous passbooks** for any new or existing customer **(reg 29(6))**; and
- Apply CDD to all anonymous accounts and passbooks in existence at commencement **(reg 29(7))**.

An open-ended investment company authorised on or after the date these Regulations came into force may not issue shares evidenced by a share certificate **(reg 29(8))**.

### Timing of Verification (Reg 30)

Verification of customer identity, person acting on behalf of customer, and beneficial owner must normally be completed **before** the establishment of a business relationship or the carrying out of a transaction **(reg 30(2))**.

**Exception (reg 30(3)):** Where necessary not to interrupt the normal conduct of business and where there is little risk of ML/TF, verification may be completed during establishment of the relationship, provided it is completed as soon as practicable after first contact.

**Credit/financial institutions opening accounts (reg 30(4)):** Verification may take place after the account is opened if there are adequate safeguards ensuring no transactions are carried out before verification is complete.

**Trusts and class beneficiaries (reg 30(6)–(7)):** Where CDD applies to a trust, legal entity, or legal arrangement with a class of beneficiaries, the relevant person must establish and verify the identity of any beneficiary before: (a) any payment is made to the beneficiary; or (b) the beneficiary exercises its vested rights in the trust, entity, or arrangement.

### Requirement to Cease Transactions (Reg 31)

Where a relevant person is unable to apply CDD as required by reg 28, it:
- Must not carry out any transaction through a bank account with the customer **(reg 31(1)(a))**;
- Must not establish a business relationship or carry out a transaction otherwise than through a bank account **(reg 31(1)(b))**;
- Must terminate any existing business relationship with the customer **(reg 31(1)(c))**; and
- Must consider whether the inability to complete CDD is a ground for making a disclosure under Part 3 of the Terrorism Act 2000 or Part 7 of POCA 2002 **(reg 31(1)(d))**.

**Exceptions:** The requirement does not apply where a professional legal adviser or other professional adviser is in the course of ascertaining the legal position for a client or defending/representing a client in legal proceedings **(reg 31(3)–(4))**, or where an insolvency practitioner has been appointed by the court and resignation would be prejudicial to creditors **(reg 31(5))**.

### Obligation to Apply Enhanced Due Diligence (Reg 33)

A relevant person **must** apply EDD and enhanced ongoing monitoring, **in addition to** standard CDD, where **(reg 33(1))**:

- **(a)** The case is identified as one where there is a **high risk of ML/TF** — either by the firm's own reg 18(1) risk assessment or by supervisory/NRA information;
- **(b)** The business relationship or transaction is with a person established in a **high-risk third country** (identified by European Commission delegated act under 4MLD Art. 9.2);
- **(c)** In relation to **correspondent relationships** between credit/financial institutions (reg 34);
- **(d)** The customer or BO is determined to be a **PEP**, family member of a PEP, or known close associate of a PEP (reg 35);
- **(e)** The customer has provided **false or stolen identification** documentation and the firm proposes to continue dealing with that customer;
- **(f)** The transaction is **complex and unusually large**, or there is an unusual pattern of transactions, with no apparent economic or legal purpose; or
- **(g)** Any other case which by its nature can present a higher risk of ML/TF.

**Exception for EEA subsidiaries/branches in high-risk third country (reg 33(2)):** Where the customer is a branch or majority-owned subsidiary of an EEA-supervised obliged entity that complies with group policies under 4MLD Art. 45, and the relevant person does not assess EDD as necessary, reg 33(1)(b) does not apply.

**EDD risk factors (reg 33(6)):** When assessing whether high risk exists and the extent of EDD measures, relevant persons must consider risk factors including:

*Customer risk factors:*
- Business relationship conducted in unusual circumstances;
- Customer resident in a geographical area of high risk;
- Customer is a legal person or arrangement that is a vehicle for holding personal assets;
- Customer has nominee shareholders or shares in bearer form;
- Customer is a cash-intensive business;
- Corporate structure of the customer is unusual or excessively complex given the nature of the business.

*Product, service, transaction or delivery channel risk factors:*
- Product involves private banking;
- Product/transaction might favour anonymity;
- Non-face-to-face business relationships or transactions without certain safeguards (e.g. electronic signatures);
- Payments will be received from unknown or unassociated third parties;
- New products, new business practices, or new delivery mechanisms including use of new or developing technology;
- Service involves nominee directors, nominee shareholders, shadow directors, or third-country company formation.

*Geographical risk factors:*
- Countries identified by credible sources (FATF evaluations, follow-up reports) as not having effective AML/CTF systems;
- Countries with significant corruption or other criminal activity (including terrorism, money laundering, illicit drugs);
- Countries subject to sanctions, embargos, or similar measures (EU, UN, UK);
- Countries providing funding or support for terrorism;
- Countries with proscribed organisations (UK government or international organisations);
- Countries identified by the FATF, IMF, World Bank, OECD or other credible sources as having requirements consistent with FATF Recommendations.

### Correspondent Relationships (Reg 34)

A credit institution or financial institution proposing to have a **correspondent relationship** with a respondent from a third country must (in addition to standard CDD):
- Gather sufficient information about the respondent to understand fully the nature of its business **(reg 34(1)(a))**;
- Determine from publicly available information the reputation of the respondent and quality of supervision to which it is subject **(reg 34(1)(b))**;
- Assess the respondent's controls to counter ML/TF **(reg 34(1)(c))**;
- Obtain **senior management approval** before establishing a new correspondent relationship **(reg 34(1)(d))**;
- **Document** the responsibilities of the respondent and correspondent in the relationship **(reg 34(1)(e))**;
- Be satisfied that the respondent has verified the identity of, and conducts ongoing CDD on, customers with direct access to accounts with the correspondent **(reg 34(1)(f))**.

Credit institutions and financial institutions must **not enter into, or continue, a correspondent relationship with a shell bank** **(reg 34(2))**. They must also take appropriate enhanced measures to ensure they do not enter into or continue a correspondent relationship with a credit/financial institution which is known to allow its accounts to be used by a shell bank **(reg 34(3))**.

### Enhanced CDD for PEPs (Reg 35)

A relevant person must have appropriate **risk-management systems and procedures** to determine whether a customer or BO is a PEP, a family member of a PEP, or a known close associate of a PEP **(reg 35(1))**.

Where a customer or BO (or potential customer/BO) is determined to be a PEP or related person, the relevant person proposing to have (or to continue) a business relationship must:
- Have **senior management approval** for establishing or continuing the business relationship **(reg 35(5)(a))**;
- Take **adequate measures** to establish the **source of wealth and source of funds** involved in the proposed business relationship or transactions **(reg 35(5)(b))**;
- Conduct **enhanced ongoing monitoring** of the business relationship **(reg 35(5)(c))**.

**Continued EDD post-PEP status (reg 35(9)):** Where a person who was a PEP is no longer entrusted with a prominent public function, the relevant person must continue to apply regs 35(5) and (8) for at least **12 months** after the date on which that person ceased to be entrusted with the public function, or for such longer period as the relevant person considers appropriate to address the risks.

**Knowledge standard for known close associates (reg 35(15)):** A relevant person need only have regard to information **in its possession**, or to credible information **which is publicly available**, in deciding whether a person is a known close associate of a PEP.

**PEP definitions (reg 35(12)):**
- **"Politically exposed person" (PEP):** An individual entrusted with prominent public functions, other than as a middle-ranking or more junior official.
- **"Family member"** of a PEP: a spouse or civil partner; children and their spouses/civil partners; and parents of the PEP.
- **"Known close associate":** An individual known to have joint beneficial ownership of a legal entity/arrangement, or any other close business relations, with a PEP; or an individual who has sole beneficial ownership of a legal entity/arrangement known to have been set up for the benefit of a PEP.

**"Prominent public functions" (reg 35(14))** include: heads of state, heads of government, ministers and deputy/assistant ministers; members of parliament or of similar legislative bodies; members of the governing bodies of political parties; members of supreme courts, constitutional courts, or any judicial body whose decisions are not subject to further appeal (except in exceptional circumstances); members of courts of auditors or of the boards of central banks; ambassadors, chargés d'affaires, and high-ranking officers in the armed forces; members of the administrative, management, or supervisory bodies of state-owned enterprises; and directors, deputy directors, and members of the board or equivalent function of an international organisation.

**FCA guidance duty (reg 48):** The FCA must give guidance (under FSMA s.139A) to relevant persons that are subject to FCA rules, in relation to the enhanced CDD required by reg 35 for PEPs, their family members and known close associates. The guidance must cover: what functions constitute "prominent public functions"; who falls within family member/known close associate definitions; what constitutes appropriate risk-management systems; how to assess jurisdiction risk; how to assess risk for individual PEPs; what "senior management" means for approval purposes; what sort of monitoring is required; measures for persons who have ceased to be PEPs; and PEPs who are beneficial owners of insurance beneficiaries **(reg 48(2))**.

### Simplified Due Diligence (Reg 37)

A relevant person **may** apply SDD in relation to a particular business relationship or transaction if it determines that the relationship or transaction presents a **low degree of ML/TF risk**, having taken into account:
- Its own reg 18(1) risk assessment;
- Information from supervisory authorities (regs 17(9) and 47); and
- The risk factors in reg 37(3) **(reg 37(1))**.

Where SDD applies, the relevant person must still comply with reg 28 but **may adjust the extent, timing, or type** of CDD measures to reflect the low risk determination **(reg 37(2)(a))**. It must carry out **sufficient monitoring** to detect unusual or suspicious transactions **(reg 37(2)(b))**.

**SDD cannot be applied if (reg 37(8)):**
- The relevant person doubts the veracity or accuracy of previously obtained documents/information;
- Its risk assessment changes and it no longer considers there is a low degree of risk;
- It suspects ML/TF; or
- Any of the conditions in reg 33(1) (EDD obligation) apply.

**Low risk factors for SDD (reg 37(3))** include:
- Customer risk: public administration or publicly owned enterprise; individual resident in lower-risk EEA geography; supervised credit/financial institution; listed company on regulated market.
- Product/service risk: low-premium life insurance; pension scheme without early surrender and not usable as collateral; payroll pension meeting specific conditions; financial inclusion product; product with purse limits or ownership transparency.
- Geographic risk: EEA state; third country with effective AML systems; low corruption country; third country meeting FATF requirements.

### Electronic Money Carve-Out (Reg 38)

A relevant person is not required to apply CDD measures in relation to **electronic money** if all four conditions are met **(reg 38(1))**:
- Maximum electronically stored amount: **€250** (or **€500** if the instrument can only be used in the United Kingdom);
- The payment instrument is either **not reloadable**, or is subject to a maximum monthly transactions limit of **€250** (UK-only instruments only);
- The instrument is used **exclusively to purchase goods or services**; and
- **Anonymous e-money cannot be used** to fund the payment instrument.

**Exception for cash redemption (reg 38(2)):** The carve-out does not apply to any redemption in cash or cash withdrawal where the amount redeemed exceeds **€100**. The issuer must carry out sufficient monitoring to detect unusual or suspicious transactions **(reg 38(3))**.

## Reliance and Record-Keeping (Regs 39–41)

### Reliance (Reg 39)

A relevant person **may rely** on a third party to apply some or all of the CDD measures in reg 28(2)–(6) and (10), but **the relevant person remains liable** for any failure to apply such measures **(reg 39(1))**.

When relying on a third party, the relevant person must:
- **Immediately obtain** from the third party all information needed to satisfy the CDD requirements **(reg 39(2)(a))**; and
- Have arrangements with the third party enabling the relevant person to **obtain immediately**, on request, copies of any identification and verification data **(reg 39(2)(b)(i))**, and requiring the third party to **retain copies** for the period required by reg 40 **(reg 39(2)(b)(ii))**.

**Who can be relied upon (reg 39(3)):**
- Another relevant person subject to these Regulations **(reg 39(3)(a))**;
- A person who carries on business in an EEA state, is subject to 4MLD obliged entity requirements, and is supervised for compliance **(reg 39(3)(b))**;
- A person in a third country subject to equivalent CDD and record-keeping requirements, supervised for compliance in a manner equivalent to 4MLD Section 2 of Chapter VI **(reg 39(3)(c))**; or
- Organisations whose members consist of persons in categories (a), (b), or (c) **(reg 39(3)(d))**.

**High-risk third country bar (reg 39(4)):** A relevant person may not rely on a third party established in a country identified by the Commission as high-risk under 4MLD Art. 9.2. This bar does not apply to branches or majority-owned subsidiaries of EEA-supervised obliged entities where the branch/subsidiary complies with group-level policies **(reg 39(5))**.

**Group reliance (reg 39(6)):** A relevant person is treated as complying with reg 39(2) if it is relying on information provided by a third party that is a **member of the same group**, the group applies CDD, record-keeping, and AML programmes in accordance with these Regulations (or 4MLD-equivalent rules), and effective implementation of those requirements is supervised at group level by an EEA authority or equivalent third-country authority.

### Record-Keeping (Reg 40)

A relevant person must keep:
- A copy of any documents and information obtained to satisfy CDD requirements in regs 28, 29, and 33 to 37 **(reg 40(2)(a))**; and
- Sufficient supporting records (original documents or copies) in respect of any transaction subject to CDD or ongoing monitoring, to enable the transaction to be **reconstructed** **(reg 40(2)(b))**.

**Retention period:** Records must be kept for **5 years** beginning on:
- For occasional transactions: the date on which the relevant person knows or has reasonable grounds to believe that the transaction is complete **(reg 40(3)(a))**;
- For business relationships: the date the business relationship comes to an end, for any transaction occurring as part of the relationship, and for CDD measures taken in connection with the relationship **(reg 40(3)(b))**.

**Maximum 10-year limit (reg 40(4)):** A relevant person is not required to keep records referred to in reg 40(3)(b)(i) for more than **10 years**.

**Deletion obligation on expiry (reg 40(5)):** On expiry of the applicable retention period, the relevant person must **delete any personal data** obtained for the purposes of these Regulations unless: (a) retention is required by enactment or for court proceedings; (b) the data subject has consented; or (c) there are reasonable grounds to believe the data needs to be retained for legal proceedings.

**Inter-firm transfer (reg 40(7)):** Where firm A is relied upon by firm B, A must, if requested by B within the retention period, immediately make available to B all information about the customer, persons purporting to act on behalf of the customer, and beneficial owners that A obtained when applying CDD.

### Data Protection (Reg 41)

Personal data obtained by relevant persons for the purposes of these Regulations **may only be processed** for the purposes of preventing ML/TF **(reg 41(1))**. No other use may be made of that data unless: (a) use is permitted by another enactment; or (b) the data subject has consented **(reg 41(3))**.

Relevant persons must provide new customers with: the standard data controller information required by the Data Protection Act 1998; and a statement that personal data received from the customer will be processed only for ML/TF prevention purposes **(reg 41(4))**.

## Beneficial Ownership Information (Regs 42–45)

### Application (Reg 42)

Part 5 applies to **UK bodies corporate** (incorporated under UK law, including eligible Scottish partnerships) and **relevant trusts** (UK trusts that are express trusts, and non-UK express trusts that have UK income or assets and are liable to UK taxes).

### Corporate Bodies: Obligations (Reg 43)

When a UK body corporate (not listed on a regulated market) enters into a relevant transaction with, or forms a business relationship with, a relevant person, it must **provide on request**:
- Name, registered number, registered office, and principal place of business;
- Its board of directors (or equivalent management body);
- The senior persons responsible for its operations;
- The law to which it is subject;
- Its **legal owners**;
- Its **beneficial owners** (as defined in reg 5); and
- Its articles of association or other governing documents **(reg 43(1))**.

The body corporate must notify the relevant person of **any change** to the above information within **14 days** of becoming aware of the change **(reg 43(4))**. It must also provide all or part of the above information to a **law enforcement authority** on request **(reg 43(5))**.

### Trustee Obligations (Reg 44)

The trustees of a relevant trust must:
- Maintain **accurate and up-to-date written records** of all beneficial owners of the trust and any potential beneficiaries **(reg 44(1))**;
- When entering into a relevant transaction or forming a business relationship, inform the relevant person that they are acting as trustee, and provide on request the identity of all beneficial owners **(reg 44(2))**;
- Notify the relevant person of changes within **14 days** **(reg 44(3))**; and
- Provide beneficial ownership information to **any law enforcement authority** on request **(reg 44(5))**.

Professional trustees must retain these records for **5 years** after the final distribution is made under the trust **(reg 44(9)(a))**.

### Register of Beneficial Ownership of Trusts (Reg 45)

The Commissioners (HMRC) must maintain a register of beneficial owners (and potential beneficiaries) of **taxable relevant trusts** **(reg 45(1))**. Trustees of taxable relevant trusts must provide the required information to the Commissioners. Law enforcement authorities (FCA, NCA, police forces, SFO, and others listed at reg 44(10)) must have access to the register **(reg 45(12))**.

## Supervision and Registration (Regs 46–64)

### Supervisory Authority Duties (Reg 46)

Each supervisory authority must:
- **Effectively monitor** relevant persons in its sector and take necessary measures to secure compliance **(reg 46(1))**;
- Adopt a **risk-based approach** to supervisory functions informed by its reg 17 risk assessment **(reg 46(2)(a))**;
- **Base frequency and intensity** of on-site and off-site supervision on the risk profiles prepared under reg 17(4) **(reg 46(2)(c))**;
- Keep a **written record** of actions taken and reasons for deciding not to act **(reg 46(2)(d))**; and
- Inform the **NCA as soon as practicable** if, in the course of supervisory functions, it knows, suspects, or has reasonable grounds to suspect that a person is or has engaged in ML/TF **(reg 46(5))**.

### Registration (Regs 54–60)

**Mandatory register (reg 54):** The FCA must maintain a register of relevant persons who are authorised persons and have notified the FCA under reg 23 that they act as an MSB or TCSP. The Commissioners must maintain a register of HVDs, MSBs (not FCA-registered), TCSPs (not FCA-registered), bill payment service providers, and telecom/digital/IT PSPs **(reg 54(2))**.

**Requirement to register (reg 56):** An HVD, MSB, TCSP, bill payment service provider, or telecom/digital/IT PSP must not carry on business unless included in the appropriate register (or an application is pending) **(reg 56(1))**.

**Fit and proper test (reg 58):** The registering authority must **refuse** registration if it is satisfied that the applicant, or any officer/manager/beneficial owner of the applicant (or, for MSBs, any agent of the applicant or any officer/manager/beneficial owner of the agent) **is not a fit and proper person** to carry on the business **(reg 58(1))**. A person convicted of a Schedule 3 relevant offence is automatically treated as not fit and proper **(reg 58(3))**. Where no Schedule 3 offence applies, the registering authority must consider: consistent failure to comply with these Regulations (or prior MLRs); and the risk that the applicant's business may be used for ML/TF **(reg 58(4))**.

**Schedule 3 relevant offences** include: Perjury Act offences; false statements (CJA 1967); falsification of documents (Taxes Management Act 1970); EU offences (European Communities Act 1972); Computer Misuse Act 1990 offences; VAT Act 1994 offences; Data Protection Act 1998; Terrorism Act 2000; POCA 2002 Pts 7 and 8; Commissioners for Revenue and Customs Act 2005; Terrorism Act 2006; Bribery Act 2010 (ss. 1, 2, 6, 7); Serious Crime Act 2015 s.45 (organised crime participation); Investigatory Powers Act 2016 Pts 1–3, 5–7; Criminal Finances Act 2017 ss.45/46 (failure to prevent facilitation of tax evasion); fraud, cheating the revenue, tax evasion; conspiracy to defraud; perverting the course of justice; and any equivalent foreign offence **(Schedule 3)**.

**Transfer of funds supervisory authority (reg 62):** The FCA is the transfer of funds supervisory authority for: authorised persons; authorised payment institutions (not Commissioners-registered); registered small payment institutions (not Commissioners-registered); authorised electronic money institutions; and registered small electronic money institutions. The Commissioners are the transfer of funds supervisory authority for all other PSPs.

## Enforcement (Regs 76–92)

### Civil Penalties — Fines and Statements (Reg 76)

A **designated supervisory authority** (the FCA or the Commissioners) may, if satisfied that a relevant person has contravened a relevant requirement (as defined in Schedule 6), impose a penalty of such amount as it considers appropriate and/or publish a statement censuring the person **(reg 76(1)–(2))**. Officers of the relevant person who were knowingly concerned in a contravention may also be penalised **(reg 76(3))**.

**Due diligence defence:** A designated supervisory authority must not impose a penalty if satisfied that the relevant person took **all reasonable steps** and exercised **all due diligence** to ensure that the requirement would be complied with **(reg 76(4))**.

In deciding whether a contravention occurred, the authority must consider whether the relevant person followed: ESA guidelines under 4MLD Arts. 17, 18.4, or 48.10 or Funds Transfer Regulation Art. 25; and relevant FCA or supervisory authority guidance approved by the Treasury **(reg 76(6))**.

**FCA procedural requirements (reg 81):** The FCA must issue a warning notice before imposing any sanction, specifying the proposed sanction, the reasons, and the right to make representations **(reg 81(2))**. After the representations period, the FCA must issue a decision notice **(reg 81(6))**. The FCA must consider eight factors in setting penalty level: gravity and duration; degree of responsibility; financial strength; profits gained or losses avoided; losses caused to third parties; co-operation; previous contraventions; and potential systemic consequences **(reg 81(1))**.

### Civil Penalties — Suspension and Removal of Authorisation (Reg 77)

The FCA may suspend or cancel a firm's FCA permission, PI authorisation, PI registration (small PI), EMI authorisation, or small EMI registration — for a period not exceeding **12 months** — where the firm has repeatedly or systematically failed to include required funds transfer information, failed to implement risk-based procedures under the funds transfer regulation, failed to comply with Articles 11/12/16 of the funds transfer regulation (serious failure), repeatedly or systematically failed to retain records, or failed to comply with a relevant requirement **(reg 77(1))**.

### Prohibition on Management (Reg 78)

A designated supervisory authority may impose a **temporary or permanent prohibition** on an individual who was knowingly concerned in a contravention, from holding any management role (a role involving responsibility for taking decisions about the management of a relevant person or PSP) **(reg 78(1)–(2))**.

### Criminal Offences (Regs 86–88)

**General criminal offence (reg 86):** A person who contravenes a relevant requirement is guilty of a criminal offence. On **summary conviction**: up to 3 months imprisonment and/or a fine. On **conviction on indictment**: up to **2 years imprisonment** and/or a fine **(reg 86(1))**.

**Due diligence defence:** A person is not guilty if they took all reasonable steps and exercised all due diligence to avoid committing the offence **(reg 86(3))**.

**Conviction excludes civil penalty (reg 86(4)):** A person convicted under reg 86 cannot also be subject to a civil penalty under Chapter 2 of Part 9 in respect of the same contravention.

**Offence of prejudicing investigations (reg 87):** It is a criminal offence for a person who knows or suspects that an officer is acting (or proposing to act) in connection with an investigation into a potential contravention to: make a disclosure likely to prejudice the investigation; or falsify, conceal, destroy, or dispose of documents relevant to the investigation. Summary conviction: 3 months/fine. Indictment: **2 years/fine** **(reg 87(2), (9))**.

**Information offences (reg 88):** It is a criminal offence to provide, in purported compliance with a requirement under these Regulations, information that is false or misleading in a material particular where the person knows or is reckless as to the falsity. Also an offence to disclose information in contravention of a relevant requirement. Summary conviction: 3 months/fine. Indictment: **2 years/fine** **(reg 88)**.

**Body corporate liability (reg 92):** Where an offence under Part 9 is committed by a body corporate with the **consent or connivance** of, or is attributable to any **neglect** on the part of, an officer of the body corporate, the officer (as well as the body corporate) is guilty of the offence.

## Key Points for Agents

- **Reg 5(1)(b): the >25% beneficial owner threshold is statutory** — not guidance. Ownership of more than 25% of shares, voting rights, or the right to appoint/remove the majority board; or ultimate management control. The PSC register alone does not discharge verification obligations (reg 28(9)).
- **Reg 6: trust beneficial owner definition is exhaustive and broad** — settlor, all trustees, all identified beneficiaries, the class of persons in whose main interest the trust operates, and any person with "control" (including veto powers over trustee decisions, power to add/remove beneficiaries, or power to appoint/remove trustees) are all beneficial owners.
- **Reg 7: supervisory authority allocation follows firm type, not FCA authorisation alone** — HVDs, estate agents, unregulated MSBs/TCSPs, and certain accountants/auditors are HMRC-supervised even if the firm also holds FCA authorisation for other activities. The four supervisory authorities are FCA, Schedule 1 professional bodies, HMRC, and the Gambling Commission.
- **Reg 15: the occasional/very limited basis exclusion requires all seven conditions simultaneously** — including the <£100k financial activity turnover cap, the ≤€1,000 per customer per transaction limit, the ≤5% of total turnover threshold, and that the financial activity is only offered to customers of the main activity, not the public. Relevant for embedded finance and agent structures.
- **Reg 18: the firm risk assessment is mandatory and must be in writing** — covering five risk factor categories (customers; countries/geographic areas; products/services; transactions; delivery channels). It must be provided to the supervisory authority on request. It cannot be replaced by sole reliance on JMLSG guidance or FCG.
- **Reg 21: MLRO appointment and board-member compliance officer appointment must be notified to the supervisory authority within 14 days** — not merely recorded internally. Any subsequent appointment must also be notified.
- **Reg 33: EDD is mandatory (not discretionary) in seven named circumstances** — including all high-risk third country relationships, all PEP relationships, correspondent banking relationships, and any case where a customer has provided false/stolen ID and the firm proposes to continue.
- **Reg 35: PEP enhanced EDD minimum period post-status is 12 months** — the firm must continue applying PEP-level EDD for at least 12 months after a PEP ceases to hold prominent public functions. The firm may apply it for longer.
- **Reg 40: the standard record-keeping period is 5 years; the maximum for business relationship records is 10 years** — and personal data must be deleted on expiry unless a separate legal basis for retention exists (legal obligation, court proceedings, or data subject consent). Data obtained under MLR 2017 cannot be processed for other purposes.
- **Reg 86: criminal liability requires only contravention of a relevant requirement** — the maximum sentence on indictment is 2 years. The due diligence defence requires all reasonable steps AND all due diligence. Reg 92 extends criminal liability to consenting/conniving officers of a body corporate.

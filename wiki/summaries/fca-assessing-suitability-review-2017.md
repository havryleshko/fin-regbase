# FCA Assessing Suitability Review — Results (May 2017)

> **PRE-IMPLEMENTATION CAVEAT**
>
> This review assessed advice delivered during **2015** and was published in **May 2017** — before MiFID II, PRIIPs, and the Insurance Distribution Directive (IDD) came into force (January 2018). The FCA explicitly flagged these regimes as introducing increased requirements for financial advisers. As of August 2025, the FCA has marked this document as **historical**.
>
> The three-part disclosure framework assessed here — (1) initial disclosure of charges and services (COBS 6.1A / COBS 6.2A), (2) product disclosure (COBS 14), and (3) the suitability report (COBS 9.4) — should be **cross-referenced against current COBS rules** before relying on specific disclosure standards. Suitability obligations under COBS 9 have been updated by MiFID II transposition and subsequent FCA rule changes. The high-level failure patterns (risk profiling, replacement business, initial charges disclosure) remain directionally relevant for supervisory risk assessment, but specific rule references must be verified against the current handbook.

---

## Overview

The FCA initiated the Assessing Suitability Review in April 2016 to assess, on a statistically robust basis, the suitability of personal recommendations and quality of disclosure delivered by the financial advisory sector. The review focused on pension and investment advice given to retail customers during **2015** — the first full year after the pension freedoms reforms came into force (April 2015).

**Scope:** 1,142 individual advice files from 656 advisory firms, assessed against COBS suitability and disclosure rules. An external statistician designed the sample to achieve 95% confidence intervals of ±3% for sector-wide results and ±5% for each of three advice sub-areas: investments, pension accumulation, and retirement income.

**Methodology:** Each file assessed on a single personal recommendation. Suitability of advice was rated Suitable / Unsuitable / Unclear. Disclosure was rated Acceptable / Unacceptable / Uncertain across the three disclosure components.

---

## Suitability Results

### Sector-wide headline

| Rating | Observed | 95% CI lower | 95% CI upper |
|--------|----------|--------------|--------------|
| Suitable | **93.1%** | 91.2% | 94.7% |
| Unsuitable | **4.3%** | 3.1% | 6.0% |
| Unclear | **2.5%** | 1.7% | 3.8% |

*An "Unclear" rating indicates insufficient information on file to determine suitability — treated as a breach of COBS 9.2 (failure to take reasonable steps to ensure the recommendation is suitable).*

The FCA attributed the positive overall result to successful adoption of the Retail Distribution Review (RDR) requirements by advisers, reinforced by prior supervisory and enforcement activity.

### By advice area

| Advice area | Suitable | Unsuitable |
|-------------|----------|------------|
| Investment advice | 94.8% | 3.8% |
| Pension accumulation | 89.6% | 5.5% |
| Retirement income | 90.9% | 5.1% |

Investment advice performed materially better than pension-related advice — a statistically significant difference. Retirement income advice was assessed in the context of the April 2015 pension freedoms changes.

### By firm type (statistically significant differences)

| Comparison | Higher suitability | Lower suitability |
|------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Network vs. directly authorised | 97.2% (networks) | 91.4% (DA) |
| Restricted vs. independent | 97.0% (restricted) | 90.8% (independent) |
| 25+ advisers vs. 3–24 advisers | 96.2% (large) | 89.3% (mid-size) |
| 25+ advisers vs. 1–2 advisers | 96.2% (large) | 91.8% (small) |

Despite being statistically significant, the FCA considered the absolute gaps small enough that it did not plan to redirect supervisory resource to firm-type segmentation. Instead it focused on root causes across all firm types.

### Two root causes of unsuitable advice

**1. Risk profiling failures.** Firms not considering or mitigating limitations of the risk profiling tool used, or recommending solutions that did not match the risk the customer was willing or able to take. This is the primary driver of unsuitable advice across all file types.

**2. Replacement business failures.** Firms recommending that customers give up valuable guarantees (e.g. guaranteed annuity rates, defined benefit transfer values) without adequate justification, or where the additional costs of the recommended solution appeared to outweigh the benefits. **(COBS 9.2)**

## Disclosure Results

### Sector-wide headline

| Rating | Observed | 95% CI lower | 95% CI upper |
|--------|----------|--------------|--------------|
| Acceptable (all three components) | **52.9%** | 49.0% | 56.8% |
| Unacceptable (at least one component) | **41.7%** | 37.9% | 45.6% |
| Uncertain | **5.4%** | 3.9% | 7.5% |

The overall unacceptable rate of 41.7% is the combined result across all three disclosure components. A file receives "Unacceptable" overall if any one of the three components fails.

### Three-part disclosure framework (component breakdown)

> **Note:** This three-part structure reflects COBS rules as they stood in 2015. MiFID II and subsequent FCA rules have modified these requirements. Cross-reference current COBS before applying.

| Component | Rule basis | Acceptable | Unacceptable | Uncertain |
|-----------|-----------|------------|--------------|-----------|
| Initial disclosure — generic | COBS 6.1A, COBS 6.2A | 60.5% | **34.0%** | 5.5% |
| Initial disclosure — customer specific | COBS 6.1A, COBS 6.2A | 82.6% | 14.6% | 2.9% |
| Product disclosure | COBS 14 | 94.7% | 3.2% | 2.1% |
| Suitability report | COBS 9.4 | 92.8% | 5.6% | 1.6% |

**The problem is concentrated in initial generic disclosure.** Product disclosure and suitability reports were broadly compliant. The FCA's concerns focused on two recurring failures in firms' initial disclosure of charges and services:

1. **Hourly charging without time estimates (COBS 6.1A.20G(2)):** Firms disclosing an hourly charging structure but not providing an approximation of how long each service is likely to take. Without a time estimate, hourly rates cannot be evaluated by consumers.

2. **Wide-range charging structures (COBS 6.1A.20G(1)):** Firms using charging structures with a range so wide (e.g. 0.5%–3.0%) that the disclosure communicates little meaningful information about the likely cost. The FCA noted this was a persistent issue previously identified in a 2014 RDR thematic review.

### By firm type (disclosure — statistically significant differences)

| Comparison | Higher acceptable | Lower acceptable |
|------------|------------------|------------------|
| Network vs. directly authorised | 69.0% (networks) | 46.0% (DA) |
| Restricted vs. independent | 75.0% (restricted) | 39.5% (independent) |
| 25+ advisers vs. 3–24 advisers | 63.9% (large) | 41.8% (mid-size) |
| 25+ advisers vs. 1–2 advisers | 63.9% (large) | 42.2% (small) |

The FCA noted that smaller, directly authorised, and independent firms showed the worst disclosure outcomes — and that these firms may have fewer compliance resources. The communications programme that followed the review was designed to reach this segment specifically.

## Key Points for Agents

- **Pre-MiFID II baseline only.** This review assessed 2015 advice before MiFID II, PRIIPs, or IDD came into force. Specific COBS rule references need verification against the current handbook before operational use.
- The sector-wide suitability rate was **93.1% suitable** (95% CI: 91.2%–94.7%) across 1,142 files from 656 firms. This is the only statistically robust sector-wide benchmark for advisory suitability in the wiki.
- **Two root causes of unsuitable advice:** risk profiling failures (tool limitations unmitigated; solution mismatched to risk tolerance/capacity) and replacement business failures (surrendering guarantees without adequate justification or where costs outweigh benefits).
- **Disclosure failure is concentrated in initial charges disclosure (generic), not in product disclosure or suitability reports.** 34% of generic initial disclosures were unacceptable; product disclosure (3.2%) and suitability reports (5.6%) were broadly compliant.
- The two specific initial disclosure failure modes are: (a) hourly charging rates without time estimates; (b) charging structures with ranges so wide they are uninformative. Both are failures under COBS 6.1A.20G.
- Pension accumulation and retirement income advice had materially lower suitability rates than investment advice — consistent with the complexity introduced by the April 2015 pension freedoms reforms.
- Network firms and restricted-scope advisers outperformed on both suitability and disclosure; mid-size (3–24 adviser) directly authorised independent firms performed worst on both dimensions.

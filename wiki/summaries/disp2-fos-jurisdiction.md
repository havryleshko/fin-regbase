# DISP 2 — Jurisdiction of the Financial Ombudsman Service

DISP 2 defines when the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) has jurisdiction to consider a complaint. It is the gateway rule: a complaint that fails any of the four jurisdiction tests cannot be determined by the FOS regardless of its merits.

> **Regulatory basis:** DISP 2 applies to firms subject to the FCA's Compulsory Jurisdiction (CJ) and to VJ participants who have opted into the FOS Voluntary Jurisdiction.

---

## DISP 2.2 — The Four-Gate Jurisdiction Test

The FOS can only consider a complaint if **all four** of the following conditions are satisfied simultaneously: **(DISP 2.2.1G)**

| Gate | Question | Governing rule |
|------|----------|----------------|
| 1 — Activity type | Does the complaint relate to an activity covered by CJ or VJ? | DISP 2.3 / DISP 2.5 |
| 2 — Territorial scope | Was the activity carried on from a UK establishment? | DISP 2.6 |
| 3 — Eligible complainant | Is the complainant an eligible complainant? | DISP 2.7 |
| 4 — Timeliness | Was the complaint referred to the FOS in time? | DISP 2.8 |

Failure at any gate ends the FOS's jurisdiction — the other three gates are irrelevant.

---

## DISP 2.3 — Compulsory Jurisdiction

The CJ applies automatically and cannot be opted out of. The following must participate: **(DISP 2.3)**

- **FCA-authorised firms** — in relation to all regulated activities (other than auction regulation bidding, administering a benchmark, and dealing with unwanted asset money)
- **Payment service providers** (PSPs) — in relation to payment services and credit-related regulated activities, and ancillary activities
- **Electronic money issuers** (EMIs) — in relation to issuance of electronic money, credit-related regulated activities, and ancillary activities
- **CBTL firms** — in relation to CBTL business and ancillary activities
- **Designated credit reference agencies** — in relation to providing credit information under SME regulations
- **Designated finance platforms** — in relation to specified information under SME finance regulations
- **Consumer redress scheme firms** — where the FCA has established a scheme under FSMA s.404

**Key point for PSPs/EMIs:** Payment service providers and electronic money issuers are in CJ for payment services and ancillary activities even if they are not FSMA-authorised. **(DISP 2.3; DISP 2 Annex 1)**

---

## DISP 2.5 — Voluntary Jurisdiction

VJ participants are firms that have chosen to submit to FOS jurisdiction for activities not covered by CJ — for example, consumer credit activities carried on by firms authorised under consumer credit legislation before the FCA credit regime. VJ is governed by a separate Scheme. **(DISP 2.5)**

---

## DISP 2.6 — Territorial Scope

FOS jurisdiction under the CJ is triggered when the activity was **carried on from an establishment in the UK**. **(DISP 2.6)**

- The complainant does **not** need to be UK-based or UK-resident — the territorial test applies to the location of the firm's establishment, not the customer.
- A complaint about an activity carried on from a UK branch or head office falls within CJ even if the customer is overseas.

---

## DISP 2.7 — Eligible Complainants

A complainant is only eligible if they fall within one of the categories in DISP 2.7.3R and are not excluded by DISP 2.7.9R.

See [[fos-eligible-complainants]] for the full framework, including the seven inclusion categories, the exclusions for regulated firms and professional clients, and the two special-case extensions (PEP identification; APP fraud).

**Key summary:**
- Seven categories of eligible complainant — not only consumers: includes micro-enterprises, charities (< £6.5m annual income), trustees (trust net assets < £5m), small businesses (at time of referral), and guarantors. **(DISP 2.7.3R)**
- If a respondent is in **doubt** about whether a business, charity, or trust is eligible, it must treat the complainant as eligible and let the FOS determine. **(DISP 2.7.5G)**
- A person identified as a PEP, family member, or known close associate **is** an eligible complainant if their complaint concerns that identification or acts done as a consequence of it. **(DISP 2.7.7AR)**
- An **APP fraud victim** who transferred funds via the respondent is an eligible complainant even if the complaint is not a PSD complaint. **(DISP 2.7.7G(2B))**
- Firms, PSPs, EMIs, and professional clients/ECPs are **not** eligible complainants for complaints about activities they are themselves authorised or regulated to carry on. **(DISP 2.7.9R)**

## DISP 2.8 — FOS Time Limits

### When the FOS Can Hear a Complaint (DISP 2.8.1R)

The FOS can only begin to consider a complaint once: **(DISP 2.8.1R)**

- The respondent has sent its **final response** or **summary resolution communication**; OR
- For **non-EMD/PSD complaints**: 8 weeks have elapsed since the respondent received the complaint; OR
- For **EMD complaints or PSD complaints**: (a) 15 business days have elapsed without a holding response, OR (b) 35 business days have elapsed (where a holding response was given).

This mirrors the DISP 1 response timeline (see [[complaints-handling-framework]]) — the FOS referral gateway opens at the same moment the firm's deadline expires.

### When the FOS Cannot Hear a Complaint (DISP 2.8.2R)

The FOS cannot consider a complaint if it is referred: **(DISP 2.8.2R)**

1. **More than 6 months** after the respondent sent the complainant its final response, redress determination, or summary resolution communication; OR
2. **More than 6 years** after the event complained of; OR (if later) **more than 3 years** from the date on which the complainant became aware (or ought reasonably to have become aware) that they had cause for complaint.

Both the 6-month and 6-year/3-year limits apply — a complainant must satisfy both. The 6-year and 3-year limits do not apply in specified pension review and FSAVC review contexts. **(DISP 2.8.5)**

**Exceptions:** The FOS may still consider a complaint outside these limits where: (3) exceptional circumstances caused the delay (incapacity is given as an example); (4) the Ombudsman Transitional Order requires it; or (5) the respondent has consented to the FOS considering a time-expired complaint. **(DISP 2.8.2R(3)–(5))**

### The 6-Month Clock Requires Notice (DISP 2.8.3G)

**Critical operational rule:** The 6-month referral time limit is only triggered by a final response, redress determination, or summary resolution communication that **specifically informs the complainant** that they have six months to refer their complaint to the FOS. **(DISP 2.8.3G)**

If the firm sends a final response without including this referral notice, the 6-month clock does not start to run. This is a mandatory element of every valid final response — not merely a best-practice point.

---

## Key Points for Agents

- All four jurisdiction gates must be satisfied simultaneously — activity type, territorial scope, eligible complainant, and timeliness. Failure at any gate ends FOS jurisdiction.
- PSPs and EMIs are in Compulsory Jurisdiction for payment services even if they are not FSMA-authorised.
- The territorial test is on the firm's UK establishment — the complainant need not be UK-based.
- The eligible complainant definition has seven categories, not just consumers. Small businesses (at time of referral) and guarantors are included; professional clients and ECPs are excluded.
- The 6-month FOS referral time limit only starts running when the firm's final response **tells the complainant** about the 6-month deadline. Omitting this notice means the clock does not start.
- The absolute backstop is 6 years from the event OR 3 years from awareness — whichever is later. Both limits run independently of the 6-month window.

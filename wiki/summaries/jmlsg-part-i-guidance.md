# JMLSG Guidance Part I — Generic AML/CTF Guidance for the UK Financial Sector

## What This Document Is

Part I of the JMLSG guidance provides generic AML/CTF guidance applicable across the UK financial sector. It is authored by the Joint Money Laundering Steering Group, whose membership includes UK Finance, the Investment Association, PIMFA, the Association of British Insurers, and thirteen other trade bodies. It is updated periodically; this version is dated June 2023 with an August 2025 update.

**Legal status: Treasury-approved industry guidance.** Courts are required to take account of it when considering whether a person committed the offence of failing to report under POCA or the Terrorism Act. Courts must also consider whether it was followed when assessing compliance with the ML Regulations. The FCA will have regard to it when considering action against a firm under SYSC or when prosecuting an MLR breach. Departures must be documented and the firm must be prepared to justify them to the FCA. **(JMLSG Preface §27–29)**

**Structure:** 8 chapters (senior management, internal controls, MLRO/nominated officer, risk-based approach, CDD, suspicious activity reporting, staff training, record keeping), plus a Glossary and Appendices. Supplemented by Part II (sector-specific) and Part III (specialist areas). The sectoral guidance in Parts II and III is incomplete without Part I — they must be read together. **(JMLSG Preface §23–26)**

**Terminology convention:** "Must" = mandatory statutory or regulatory requirement. "Should" = way in which requirements may be satisfied, allowing alternative means. Departures from "should" provisions must be documented. **(JMLSG Preface §19)**

## Relationship to FCG

The FCA Financial Crime Guide (FCG) and JMLSG guidance are distinct but complementary:

- FCG is FCA sourcebook guidance on firm systems and controls; it focuses on what the regulator expects.
- JMLSG Part I is Treasury-approved industry guidance; courts must take account of it in criminal and regulatory proceedings; it focuses on how obligations should be met operationally.
- FCG 3.1.7 explicitly states that FCG is not intended to replace or compete with JMLSG guidance; firms should use both.

JMLSG provides operational depth not found in FCG: risk factor weighting methodology, simplified due diligence criteria, the consent/DAML regime, tipping-off offences and permitted disclosures, and third-party reliance pro-forma confirmations.

## Key Additions vs FCG 3 (What JMLSG Part I Adds)

### Simplified Due Diligence (SDD) — A Third CDD Tier

JMLSG §5.4 establishes SDD as a distinct lower CDD tier for low-risk situations. FCG 3 covers only CDD and EDD. See [[simplified-due-diligence]] for the full framework.

### Suspicion Definitions, the DAML Consent Regime, and Tipping Off

JMLSG Chapter 6 provides:
- Court-derived definitions of "knowledge" and "suspicion" and the objective "reasonable grounds" test
- The full DAML consent procedure — what to do when suspicion arises *before* a transaction
- Two distinct tipping-off criminal offences and their permitted disclosure carve-outs

See [[sar-consent-tipping-off]] for the full framework.

### Third-Party Reliance — Full Operational Framework

JMLSG §5.6 provides the complete framework for relying on another firm's CDD, including conditions, immediate disclosure obligations, pro-forma confirmations, and group introduction rules. See the Reliance section in [[customer-due-diligence]].

### Risk Factor Weighting Methodology

JMLSG §4.43–4.52 provides explicit guidance on how firms should weight risk factors. Key principles: weighting must not be unduly influenced by a single factor; economic or profit considerations must not influence risk ratings; situations identified in national legislation as always high-risk cannot be overruled by firm weighting; automated risk scores can be overridden but the rationale must be documented. **(JMLSG §4.49–4.51)**

### PEP Continuum and Source of Wealth Detail

JMLSG §5.5.13–5.5.36 provides the fuller PEP framework:
- Explicit PEP continuum concept: the higher the PEP on the risk scale, the more extensive the EDD
- Domestic PEPs have inherently lower risk than non-domestic PEPs; reduced EDD may apply absent enhanced risk factors **(MLR Reg 35(12)(a), (d)–(e))**
- PEP status ceases for EDD purposes after **one year** of leaving office, or longer at firm's discretion **(MLR Reg 35(9))**
- PEP family members and close associates: obligation ceases when the PEP leaves office (regardless of the one-year rule)
- Source of wealth verification for higher-risk PEPs should use reliable, independent data — e.g. asset/income declarations, conveyancing evidence for property proceeds, copy of will for inheritance

## Chapter Summaries

### Chapter 1 — Senior Management Responsibility

Senior management must identify, assess, and manage ML/TF/PF risks. Policy statement must be prepared and kept under review. Annual MLRO report must be commissioned and deficiencies remedied. Senior management approval required for high-risk business relationship decisions. **(JMLSG §1.1–1.8)**

### Chapter 2 — Internal Controls

Controls framework must cover CDD procedures, staff training, nominated officer procedures, and record keeping. Outsourced functions must meet UK AML/CTF standards; outsourcing to weaker jurisdictions increases rather than reduces risk. Intragroup outsourcing requires SLAs covering KPIs, escalation protocols, and training provision. **(JMLSG §2.1–2.24)**

### Chapter 3 — MLRO and Nominated Officer

All firms (other than sole traders) must appoint a nominated officer. FCA-regulated firms must additionally appoint an MLRO (SMF 17 — requires individual FCA approval). The MLRO must have sufficient seniority, independence, resources, and direct access to the FCA and NCA. Temporary cover of up to 12 weeks requires no FCA pre-approval; longer periods require FCA approval. Annual Financial Crime Report must be submitted to the FCA within 60 business days of accounting reference date. **(JMLSG §3.1–3.49; SUP 16.23)**

### Chapter 4 — Risk-Based Approach

Five-step RBA: identify ML/TF/PF risks → assess customer/product/channel/geography risks → design controls → monitor → record. Risk categories are country, customer, and product/service risk. Risk factor weighting is the firm's own judgment but must not allow economics to override. Automated systems must be understood by the firm. **(JMLSG §4.1–4.55)**

### Chapter 5 — Customer Due Diligence

Full CDD framework: identity verification timing rules and exceptions; beneficial ownership; SDD criteria; EDD triggers (seven specified scenarios); PEPs (domestic vs non-domestic, continuum, one-year rule); high-risk third countries; correspondent banking; third-party reliance; group introductions; monitoring. **(JMLSG §5.1–5.7)**

### Chapter 6 — Suspicious Activity Reporting

Legal definitions of knowledge, suspicion, and reasonable grounds. Internal reporting obligations. Nominated officer evaluation duty. External reporting (SAR Online). The DAML consent regime. Tipping-off offences and permitted disclosures. Transactions following a disclosure (the £3,000 deposit-taker threshold). **(JMLSG §6.1–6.99)**

### Chapter 7 — Staff Training

All staff must receive AML/CTF training appropriate to their role and exposure. Training must cover the firm's own policies and procedures, recognition of suspicious activity, and the personal obligation to report. Training effectiveness must be assessed. **(JMLSG §7.1–7.45)**

### Chapter 8 — Record Keeping

CDD documents and information: five years after the business relationship ends (or five years after an occasional transaction). Transaction records: up to ten years. Records of internal and external reports: five years. Records must be retrievable promptly and must be deleted after the retention period (GDPR compliance). **(JMLSG §8.1–8.34; MLR Reg 40)**

## Key Points for Agents

- JMLSG Part I is Treasury-approved industry guidance. Courts must take account of it. The FCA will have regard to it in enforcement. Departures must be documented and justifiable.
- "Must" = mandatory statutory/regulatory requirement. "Should" = recommended but firm may use an alternative approach, if documented.
- SDD is a permitted third CDD tier for genuinely low-risk situations — it adjusts (but does not exempt from) CDD obligations. Six specified qualifying scenarios. See [[simplified-due-diligence]].
- When suspicion arises before a transaction, the nominated officer must seek NCA consent (DAML) before proceeding — not just file a SAR. NCA has 7 working days to refuse; 31-day moratorium if refused. See [[sar-consent-tipping-off]].
- Tipping off is a criminal offence with two variants: disclosing that a SAR was made; disclosing that an investigation is underway. Permitted disclosures include: within the same firm; within the same group; between two institutions sharing a common customer/transaction.
- Third-party reliance is expressly permitted under MLR Reg 39 but responsibility cannot be delegated. Reliance on SDD-verified identity is not permitted. See [[customer-due-diligence]].
- Domestic PEPs have inherently lower risk than non-domestic PEPs. PEP status ceases for EDD purposes one year after leaving office (or longer at firm's discretion).
- Annual Financial Crime Report must be submitted to the FCA within 60 business days of accounting reference date (SUP 16.23). Most firms must submit; credit unions and some limited-permission firms with revenue under £5m are exempt.

# COBS 10 — Appropriateness (non-MiFID and non-insurance-based investment products provisions)

## What Appropriateness Is and Is Not

Appropriateness is a **knowledge and experience only** test that applies to **non-advised services** for complex or risky products. It is narrower than suitability: a firm does not assess the client's financial situation or investment objectives — only whether the client has the knowledge and experience necessary to understand the risks of the specific product or service.

**Appropriateness ≠ Suitability.** Suitability (COBS 9) applies where a firm makes a personal recommendation or manages investments. Appropriateness (COBS 10) applies where a firm arranges or deals on a non-advised basis in particular product types. A firm that has assessed suitability need not also assess appropriateness **(COBS 10.6.1G)**.

---

## Scope

COBS 10 applies to a firm which **(COBS 10.1.2R)**:

**(1)** Arranges or deals in relation to:
- (i) a **non-readily realisable security**
- (ii) a **speculative illiquid security**
- (iii) a **derivative**
- (iv) a **warrant**
- (v) a **unit in a long-term asset fund (LTAF)**
- (vi) a **UK RIE cryptoasset exchange traded note**

with or for a **retail client**, other than in the course of MiFID or equivalent third country business; **or**

**(b)** facilitates a retail client becoming a **lender under a P2P agreement**; **or**

**(c)** **issues a unit in a LTAF** to a retail client; **or**

**(d)** transacts in a **qualifying cryptoasset** with or for a retail client,

and the firm **is aware, or ought reasonably to be aware**, that the application or order is in response to a **direct offer financial promotion**.

**(4)** COBS 10 does **not** apply to a firm that makes a **personal recommendation** or **provides targeted support** in relation to the relevant investment — appropriateness is not a layered obligation on top of suitability.

**Cryptoasset scope note:** The qualifying cryptoasset trigger applies to both authorised firms and registered persons. Where a registered person transacts in qualifying cryptoassets with or for a retail client in response to a direct offer financial promotion, the chapter applies to that registered person as it applies to an authorised person **(COBS 10.1.2R(3))**.

## The Assessment Obligation

### What a Firm Must Ask (COBS 10.2.1R)

When providing a service to which COBS 10 applies, a firm must:
1. Ask the client to provide information regarding their **knowledge and experience** in the investment field relevant to the specific type of product or service; and
2. Determine whether the client has the **necessary experience and knowledge** to understand the risks involved.

### Information to Be Collected (COBS 10.2.2R)

The knowledge and experience information includes, to the extent appropriate to the nature of the client, the service, and the type of product or transaction envisaged:
- **(1)** The types of service, transaction, and **designated investment** with which the client is familiar;
- **(2)** The nature, volume, and frequency of the client's **past transactions** in designated investments and the period over which they have been carried out; and
- **(3)** The client's **level of education, profession, or relevant former profession**.

### No Discouraging Disclosure (COBS 10.2.3R)

A firm **must not encourage** a client not to provide information required for the purposes of the appropriateness assessment.

### Reliance on Client Information (COBS 10.2.4R)

A firm is entitled to rely on client-provided information unless it **is aware** that the information is manifestly out of date, inaccurate, or incomplete. Note: the non-MiFID standard is "aware" only — not the stricter "aware or ought to be aware" that applies under COBS 10A.

### Use of Existing Information (COBS 10.2.5G)

When assessing appropriateness, a firm may use information it **already has in its possession** — there is no obligation to re-collect information that has previously been gathered.

### Knowledge Alone May Be Sufficient (COBS 10.2.6G)

Depending on the circumstances, a firm may be satisfied that the client's **knowledge alone** is sufficient to understand the risks. Where reasonable, a firm may **infer knowledge from experience**.

### No Duty to Communicate Assessment to Client (COBS 10.2.8G)

If the firm is satisfied that the client has the necessary knowledge and experience, there is **no duty to communicate this to the client**. If the firm does communicate it, the communication must not amount to a **personal recommendation** — doing so would move the transaction out of the appropriateness regime and into the suitability regime (COBS 9).

### Restricted Mass Market Investments — Question Guidance (COBS 10.2.9G)

When determining whether a retail client has the necessary knowledge to understand risks in relation to a **restricted mass market investment**, a firm should consider asking questions covering at least the matters in the relevant Annex:
- **COBS 10 Annex 1G** for non-readily realisable securities
- **COBS 10 Annex 2G** for P2P agreements or P2P portfolios
- **COBS 10 Annex 3G** for units in a long-term asset fund
- **COBS 10 Annex 4G** for qualifying cryptoassets
- **COBS 10 Annex 5G** for UK RIE cryptoasset exchange traded notes

## Warning Obligations

### Product Assessed as Inappropriate (COBS 10.3.1R)

If a firm considers, on the basis of information received, that the product or service is **not appropriate** for the client, the firm **must warn** the client. The warning may be provided in a **standardised format**.

### Client Provides Insufficient Information (COBS 10.3.2R)

If the client **elects not to provide** information, or provides **insufficient information** regarding knowledge and experience, the firm **must warn** the client that this decision means the firm cannot determine whether the product or service is appropriate. The warning may be provided in a **standardised format**.

### Client Insists on Proceeding After Warning (COBS 10.3.3G)

If a client asks the firm to go ahead with a transaction despite being given a warning, it is for the **firm to consider whether to do so** having regard to the circumstances. There is no hard prohibition on execution after warning — but the firm must exercise judgment, and its decision must be recorded (see Record Keeping).

## When Appropriateness Need Not Be Assessed

### The Execution-Only / Non-Complex Exemption (COBS 10.4.1R)

A firm is **not required** to ask the client to provide information or assess appropriateness if **all three** of the following conditions are satisfied:

**(a)** The service **only consists of execution and/or reception and transmission of client orders** (with or without ancillary services), it relates to particular **non-complex financial instruments** (see below), and it is provided **at the initiative of the client**;

**(b)** The client has been **clearly informed** (whether in a standardised format or not) that in the provision of this service the firm is **not required to assess suitability or appropriateness** and therefore the client **does not benefit from the protection of the rules** on assessing suitability; and

**(c)** The firm complies with its obligations in relation to **conflicts of interest**.

### What Counts as Non-Complex (COBS 10.4.1R(2)–(3))

The non-complex financial instruments for these purposes are:
- **(b)** Money market instruments, bonds or other forms of securitised debt (excluding those that **embed a derivative**); or
- **(d)** Other non-complex financial instruments satisfying all four criteria:

**(i)** It is not a derivative or other security giving the right to acquire or sell a transferable security or giving rise to a cash settlement determined by reference to transferable securities, currencies, interest rates or yields, commodities, or other indices or measures;

**(ii)** There are frequent opportunities to dispose of, redeem, or otherwise realise the instrument at prices that are **publicly available** to market participants and that are either market prices or prices made available or validated by **valuation systems independent of the issuer**;

**(iii)** It does not involve any **actual or potential liability for the client that exceeds the cost of acquiring** the instrument; and

**(iv)** Adequately comprehensive information on its characteristics is **publicly available** and is likely to be readily understood so as to enable the average retail client to make an informed judgment.

**COBS 10.4.3G:** COBS 10.4 is **not relevant** for the purpose of complying with the rules requiring an appropriateness assessment under COBS 4.12A in relation to restricted mass market investments — the non-complex exemption does not apply in that context.

### At the Initiative of the Client (COBS 10.5.1G–10.5.3G)

A service should be considered to be at the initiative of a client **unless** the client demands it in response to a **personalised communication** from or on behalf of the firm to that particular client which contains an invitation or is intended to influence the client in respect of a **specific financial instrument** or specific transaction **(COBS 10.5.1G)**.

A service can be considered at the client's initiative notwithstanding that the client demands it on the basis of any communication **containing a promotion or offer** that by its very nature is **general and addressed to the public or a larger group** — a mass-market promotion does not make a resulting order firm-initiated **(COBS 10.5.2G)**.

Key practical guidance on personalised communications **(COBS 10.5.3G)**:
- Mass communications (newspapers, billboards) are by nature general — not personalised.
- Emails, telephone calls, or letters addressed to a client may or may not be personalised depending on content.
- A communication is **not personalised** solely because it contains the client's name and address, or because a mailing list has been filtered.

### One Assessment Per Service Relationship (COBS 10.4.2R)

Where a client **engages in a course of dealings** involving a specific type of product or service through the firm's services, the firm is **not required to make a new assessment on the occasion of each separate transaction**. A firm complies with the rules provided it makes the necessary assessment **before beginning that service**. This is a significant operational simplification for execution-only platforms.

### Suitability Assessed Instead (COBS 10.6.1G)

A firm **need not assess appropriateness** if it is receiving or transmitting an order in relation to which it has:
- Assessed **suitability under COBS 9** (Suitability (including basic advice)); or
- Assessed **suitability under COBS 9B** (Targeted support).

## Product-Specific Question Matrices

### Non-Readily Realisable Securities (COBS 10 Annex 1G)

When assessing whether a retail client has the necessary knowledge to understand the risks of a non-readily realisable security, a firm should consider asking questions covering at least 13 matters:
1. The nature of the client's **contractual relationship** with the issuer and any underlying beneficiaries;
2. The possibility that the client could **lose all the money** they invest;
3. The risk of **issuer failure** and associated total loss risk;
4. The **regulated status** of the investment activity, including that the issuance of securities does not ordinarily involve regulated activity and the implications for FCA regulation;
5. The extent to which the **FOS or FSCS** apply — including that these services do not protect against poor investment performance, and that the FOS **cannot ordinarily consider complaints** in relation to unauthorised persons;
6. The **potential illiquidity** of non-readily realisable securities, including the unlikelihood or impossibility of selling the security and the nature of mechanisms through which the client could be paid back;
7. The **management and administration risk** in the event of issuer insolvency or failure;
8. The **role of the issuer** (including in assessing and making underlying investments);
9. That where a security is held in an **Innovative Finance ISA (IFISA), this does not reduce the risk** or otherwise protect the client from losing their money;
10. The **benefits of diversification** and that retail clients should not generally invest more than **10% of their net assets** in restricted mass market investments;
11. Where a **share**: likelihood of dividend payments; risk of dilution from further share issues; risk of further issues granting preferential rights;
12. Where a **debenture**: client's exposure to credit risk of the issuer; that investing in a debenture is not comparable to depositing money in a savings account; that returns may vary;
13. Where arranged by a firm: the nature of contractual relationships with the firm; the firm's role and scope of service (including due diligence undertaken); and the management/administration risk if the firm becomes insolvent.

### P2P Agreements and P2P Portfolios (COBS 10 Annex 2G)

14 matters to cover, including: contractual relationships with the borrower and the firm; exposure to borrower credit risk; total loss possibility; that P2P is not covered by FSCS and FOS does not protect against poor performance; returns may vary; P2P is not comparable to savings accounts; characteristics of any security interest, insurance, guarantee, contingency fund, or other risk mitigation measure — and that none of these can guarantee no loss; illiquidity including secondary market access being advertised but not guaranteed; the firm's role and scope; management/administration risk if firm becomes insolvent; IFISA wrapper does not reduce risk; 10% net assets guideline.

### Long-Term Asset Funds (COBS 10 Annex 3G)

9 matters to cover, including: value can go down; potential illiquidity of LTAFs and underlying assets; that it could take many years to profit; dealing frequency and notice period mechanics after redemption request accepted (client will not know redemption proceeds value until end of notice period); risk of LTAF investment failure and total loss; FOS/FSCS limitations; role of the authorised fund manager; 10% net assets guideline; where arranged by another firm, the three firm-specific matters (contractual relationship, firm role/due diligence, insolvency risk).

### Qualifying Cryptoassets (COBS 10 Annex 4G)

12 matters to cover, including: role of the business and what due diligence is and is not undertaken on underlying investments; nature of legal and beneficial ownership and associated risks; total loss possibility; potential complexity and associated difficulty of understanding risks; **high volatility** (value can fall as quickly as it can rise); **operational risks** (cyber-attacks, loss of private keys, comingling of funds, financial crime); management/administration risk if the business becomes insolvent; that the client **may not be able to readily sell** (market illiquidity or operational outages); regulated status of the business and FCA regulation implications; FOS/FSCS coverage limitations (FOS cannot ordinarily consider complaints about unauthorised persons); that cryptoassets are **not comparable to investing in mainstream investments** such as listed or exchange-traded securities; 10% net assets guideline.

### UK RIE Cryptoasset Exchange Traded Notes (COBS 10 Annex 5G)

12 matters, closely paralleling the qualifying cryptoasset matrix. Key additions for ETNs: the nature of the client's contractual relationship with the issuer and any underlying beneficiaries; the issuer's role (including in assessing and making underlying investments); credit risk exposure to the issuer; returns may vary over time. The operational risks (cyber-attacks, loss of private keys, comingling of funds, financial crime) and the non-comparability to mainstream investments apply as with qualifying cryptoassets.

## Record Keeping

### Record Content (COBS 10.7.1G)

Records must include the client information obtained to assess appropriateness, and must be adequate to indicate what the assessment was.

### Retention Period (COBS 10.7.2R)

The firm must retain its records relating to appropriateness for a **minimum of five years**.

---

## Key Points for Agents

- **Appropriateness applies to non-advised services; suitability applies to advised services (COBS 10.1.2R(4)).** The two regimes are mutually exclusive per transaction. Where a firm makes a personal recommendation or provides targeted support, it assesses suitability — not appropriateness. Where a firm has assessed suitability under COBS 9 or COBS 9B, it need not also assess appropriateness **(COBS 10.6.1G)**.
- **The test is knowledge and experience only (COBS 10.2.1R–10.2.2R).** Financial situation and investment objectives are not assessed under appropriateness. Three categories: types of instrument/service familiar with; nature/volume/frequency of past transactions; education/profession. A firm may infer knowledge from experience **(COBS 10.2.6G)** and may use existing information it already holds **(COBS 10.2.5G)**.
- **Two mandatory warnings (COBS 10.3.1R / 10.3.2R), both may be in standardised format:** (1) product assessed as not appropriate → must warn; (2) client refuses or gives insufficient information → must warn that assessment cannot be completed. After a warning, client may still proceed — it is for the firm to consider whether to execute **(COBS 10.3.3G)**.
- **Execution-only non-complex exemption (COBS 10.4.1R):** No appropriateness assessment needed if service is execution/RTO only, relates to non-complex instruments, is at the client's initiative, client has been clearly informed they do not benefit from appropriateness protection, and conflicts obligations are met. A general mass-market promotion does not make a resulting order firm-initiated. 4-criterion non-complex definition for non-MiFID (vs 6 for MiFID under COBS 10A).
- **One assessment per service, not per transaction (COBS 10.4.2R).** Where a client engages in a course of dealings in a specific product/service type, assess appropriateness before beginning the service — no need to reassess on each subsequent order.
- **Scope includes qualifying cryptoassets and UK RIE crypto ETNs (COBS 10.1.2R).** Triggered where the firm is aware or ought reasonably to be aware the order is in response to a direct offer financial promotion. Annex 4 provides a 12-item question matrix. Applies to registered persons as well as authorised firms.
- **Record retention: 5 years minimum (COBS 10.7.2R).** Records must be adequate to indicate what the assessment was — including client information gathered and the assessment outcome.

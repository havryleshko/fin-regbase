# FCA Dear CEO Letter — Expectations for Wealth Management and Stockbroking Firms (November 2023)

## Overview

Issued 8 November 2023 by Lucy Castledine, FCA Director for Consumer Investments. Addressed to CEOs of firms that manage portfolios, provide retail-focused stockbroking services, operate as private banks, or provide outsourced services to wealth managers. The letter frames two co-equal supervisory priorities: **preventing financial crime** and **meeting Consumer Duty outcomes**. Both are presented as equally weighted — not subordinate to each other.

**Sector scale:** 1.8m portfolios and 14.3m stockbroking accounts. The FCA describes this as "one of the higher risk sectors of financial service firms in our jurisdiction."

The FCA states it is moving to a "more assertive, intrusive, proactive and data driven" supervisory posture — including short-notice and unannounced visits already underway and significantly increased use of formal intervention powers.

## Financial Crime Expectations

The letter designates wealth management and stockbroking as "an inherently high-risk sector for enabling and/or participating in financial crime." Specific failures named include laundering illegitimate client assets through "greed or incompetence" and theft or squandering of legitimate client assets through fraud and scams.

### Firm-Level Expectations

The FCA expects firms to:

- Not knowingly or otherwise engage in or facilitate fraud, scams, or money laundering.
- Understand financial crime risks by identifying who clients are, including expected transaction patterns and corporate structure — i.e., substantive KYC, not tick-box compliance.
- Not outsource responsibility for financial crime prevention to third parties.
- Maintain robust and effective systems and controls, proportionate and risk-based.
- Ensure SMF 16/17 (MLRO/deputy MLRO) holders have the required **experience, skills, and independence**.
- Share and report information about wrongdoing to the FCA or relevant law enforcement immediately.
- Read and fully implement the **FCA Financial Crime Guide (FCG)** and Financial Crime Thematic Reviews (FCTR).

### FCA Supervisory Changes — Financial Crime

The FCA has established a **dedicated financial crime function for consumer investments**, focused solely on identifying firms with fraud, scams, or money laundering indicators. Short-notice and unannounced visits have already commenced, particularly for financial crime.

> FCG Chapter 3 (Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing) has been ingested. See [[fcg3-money-laundering-terrorist-financing]], [[aml-ctf-framework]], and [[customer-due-diligence]] for the full framework. The FCTR (Financial Crime Thematic Reviews) has not yet been ingested.

## Consumer Duty Expectations

The FCA states it expects Consumer Duty to have been implemented in full and to have resulted in "meaningful changes" to firms' business, service, and proposition. Firms should be able to demonstrate this on request.

### Products & Services and Consumer Understanding

**Named harms specific to this sector:**

- **Unsuitable portfolios obscured through relationship leverage.** Portfolio managers using established client relationships to obscure the risks of portfolios not aligned to client risk profiles. This is described as a deliberate exploitation of trust, not mere oversight.
- **Execution-only complexity failures.** Execution-only stockbrokers promoting products too complex for their customers to understand. The execution-only model provides no carve-out from the Consumer Understanding outcome.
- **Retail-to-professional uprating.** Firms must not uprate retail consumers to professional unless supported by robust systems and controls, given the significant loss of regulatory protections. **14% of firms uprated consumers in the last 12 months** — the FCA is actively challenging this practice.
- **Unjustified complex and unregulated investments.** Firms must fully justify any complex or unregulated investments they offer, with a clear view of suitability or appropriateness.
- **FOS/FSCS limitation disclosure.** Firms must ensure consumers understand any limitations on their Financial Ombudsman Service and FSCS protection status, and the associated risks of their investments.

**Vulnerability reassessment mandate:**

The FCA reiterates the 49%/69% data point (portfolio managers/stockbrokers identifying zero vulnerable consumers) and directs firms to reassess vulnerability status. The letter explicitly cross-references FCA vulnerability guidance (FG21/1).

### Price and Value

**Named poor practices specific to this sector:**

- **Overtrading.** Firms trading client portfolios excessively to generate transaction fees without corresponding client benefit. Named alongside charging for undelivered services as among the clearest value failures.
- **Client money interest not passed on.** Firms not passing fair interest on client money balances despite interest rates having risen materially. In some instances, firms also charge a separate fee for holding those funds — compounding the value erosion.
- **Securities lending revenue not shared.** Firms not providing a fair share of revenue from securities lending, despite the consumer bearing the associated risk. The FCA explicitly flags this as a value failure.
- **Opaque fee disclosures.** Firms not consistently providing clear disclosures on fees or charging structures, resulting in consumers unaware of high fees eroding investment returns. The FCA states it will "challenge firms to justify such high charges," particularly where fees are high at both the average and individual level.
- **Incomplete value chain analysis.** Firms failing to consider all revenue streams from consumers across the full value chain when conducting fair value assessments.

## Supervisory Approach

The letter announces a concrete shift in FCA supervisory methodology for this sector:

| Change | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Dedicated financial crime function | Focused solely on fraud, scams, money laundering indicators in consumer investments |
| Data-led outlier identification | Built on a 99% response rate to the 2022/23 data survey; follow-up survey announced for December 2023, tailored to each firm's business model |
| Short-notice and unannounced visits | Already underway, particularly for financial crime |
| Stronger formal interventions | Significantly increased use of intervention powers; Consumer Duty will be used to intervene quickly on an individual or multi-firm basis |
| Root cause accountability | Future supervisory engagement will assess whether firms have addressed root causes — poor leadership, governance, systems/controls, and conflicts of interest — not just surface symptoms |

Firms are reminded of their **Principle 11** obligation to proactively notify the FCA of any harm identified through remediation work.

## Key Points for Agents

- This letter designates wealth management/stockbroking as high-risk and treats financial crime prevention as a co-equal priority to Consumer Duty — not a subordinate obligation.
- Three new named Price & Value failures specific to this sector: overtrading to generate fees; withholding client money interest (and charging holding fees); not sharing securities lending revenue despite consumers bearing the risk.
- Retail-to-professional uprating is an active FCA supervisory concern: 14% of firms did this in the last 12 months; the FCA requires robust systems and controls to justify any uprating.
- FOS/FSCS protection limitations must be disclosed — consumers must understand what protection they do and do not have.
- The FCA's supervisory posture in this sector has shifted: data-led targeting, dedicated financial crime function, and unannounced visits are operational, not planned.

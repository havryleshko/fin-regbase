# MAR 1 — Market Abuse: FCA Guidance on UK MAR Behaviours

## Overview

MAR 1 is the FCA's guidance sourcebook on the market abuse regime. It provides interpretive guidance on how each market abuse behaviour — as defined in the UK Market Abuse Regulation (UK MAR) — operates in practice, what factors indicate or mitigate the behaviour, and what statutory exceptions apply.

The underlying prohibition is in **UK MAR directly** (an EU regulation retained in UK law post-Brexit). MAR 1 does not create new rules; it explains how to apply UK MAR and signals the FCA's enforcement approach.

---

## MAR 1.1 — Application

MAR 1 applies to any person to whom UK MAR applies **(MAR 1.1.1G)**. This includes FCA-authorised firms, their employees, and any person whose behaviour may constitute market abuse — whether or not they are authorised. Market abuse obligations therefore extend beyond the FCA's regulatory perimeter.

---

## MAR 1.2 — General Guidance

### Intent Not Required

UK MAR does **not require intent** to commit market abuse **(MAR 1.2.3G)**. A person can commit insider dealing, manipulation, or unlawful disclosure even if they did not intend to abuse the market. The test is objective — was the behaviour of the prohibited type and did it produce the prohibited effect?

This is the critical point for compliance programmes: systems designed to catch intentional abuse may miss negligent or accidental abuse, both of which are caught by UK MAR.

### Factors Relevant to Whether Information Is Inside Information

UK MAR defines inside information as information of a precise nature that has not been made public, relating to one or more issuers or financial instruments, which, if made public, would be likely to have a significant effect on price **(UK MAR Art. 7(1))**. MAR 1.2.6G lists factors relevant to this assessment, including:
- Whether the information relates to a particular instrument or issuer
- The specificity of the information
- Whether it reduces or eliminates uncertainty

### The "Made Public" Four-Factor Test

Information is "made public" if it falls within any of the following **(MAR 1.2.12G)**:
1. Published through a **Regulatory Information Service (RIS)** or equivalent channel
2. Contained in **publicly inspectable records** (e.g., Companies House, court records)
3. **Generally available** — including information available only via subscription or a fee, or information that can be derived from publicly available data, or information obtainable by observation

The FCA makes two important clarifications **(MAR 1.2.14G–1.2.15G)**:
- The fact that information is only available **outside the UK** does not prevent it from being "made public"
- The fact that information can only be derived or interpreted using **above-average expertise or resources** does **not** prevent it from being "made public" — if a market professional can derive it from public sources, it is public

## MAR 1.3 — Insider Dealing

Insider dealing occurs when a person who possesses inside information uses that information by acquiring or disposing of financial instruments to which it relates **(UK MAR Art. 8)**. MAR 1.3 provides examples of behaviours that constitute, or are factors indicating, insider dealing.

### Named Insider Dealing Behaviours (MAR 1.3.2G)

| Behaviour | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Trading ahead of a block order | Acquiring a position before executing a large client order likely to move the price, then trading out of that position — using the knowledge of the pending order |
| **Front-running / pre-positioning** | Trading own account ahead of a client order when information about that pending order constitutes inside information **(MAR 1.3.2G(2))** |
| Takeover-based dealing | Acquiring a target company's shares based on knowledge of a planned takeover bid, before announcement |
| False impression deals | Entering trades with no legitimate commercial rationale other than to benefit from inside information |

Front-running and pre-positioning are explicitly named. The key question is whether knowledge of the pending order constitutes inside information — which it can, particularly where a pending order is large enough to move the market.

### Factors Indicating Insider Dealing (MAR 1.3.3G)

- The transaction was profitable due to an announcement shortly after
- The person had access to or a realistic opportunity to acquire inside information
- The transaction was inconsistent with the person's normal trading pattern

### Accepted Market Practice and the Takeover Safe Harbour

A person does not commit insider dealing if their behaviour is in conformity with an **accepted market practice (AMP)** **(UK MAR Art. 13)**. The FCA publishes a list of recognised AMPs. Additionally, behaviour in connection with **takeover bids** under UK MAR Art. 9 (legitimate behaviour defence) may not constitute insider dealing where acquisition of the information was a natural consequence of carrying out the takeover.

## MAR 1.4 — Unlawful Disclosure of Inside Information

Unlawful disclosure occurs when a person possessing inside information discloses it to any other person, other than in the normal exercise of an employment, a profession, or a duty **(UK MAR Art. 10)**.

### Selective Analyst Briefing Named as Unlawful Disclosure

MAR 1.4.2G expressly identifies **selective briefing of analysts** as an example of unlawful disclosure. A company that provides a preferred analyst or investor with information that other market participants do not have — even if the intent is commercial relationship maintenance — risks committing unlawful disclosure.

### The Confidentiality + Necessity Safe Harbour (MAR 1.4.5G)

A disclosure is in the "normal exercise" of employment/profession/duty if all of the following conditions are met:
1. The disclosure is **necessary** for the legitimate purpose (not merely convenient)
2. The recipient is under a **duty of confidentiality** (contractual, regulatory, or professional)
3. The purpose is one of: employment function; professional adviser (legal, accounting, financial); commercial negotiation; a takeover; compliance with a **legal obligation** (e.g., disclosure to a regulator)

If any element is missing, the disclosure is potentially unlawful. For example, disclosure to an analyst in a commercial briefing is not protected unless the analyst is under a formal NDA and the disclosure is strictly necessary.

## MAR 1.6 — Manipulating Transactions

Manipulation by transaction occurs when a person enters into a transaction or places an order that gives a false or misleading impression of supply, demand, price, or value; or that secures an artificial price level **(UK MAR Art. 12(1)(a))**.

### Legitimate Reasons Defence

A person is not manipulating if they can demonstrate that their transactions were carried out for **legitimate reasons** and conform with an **accepted market practice (AMP)** **(UK MAR Art. 12(1)(a), MAR 1.6.5G)**. Legitimate reasons include genuine investment portfolio rebalancing and hedging. The combination of both elements is required — legitimate reasons alone, without AMP conformance, may not be sufficient.

### Abusive Squeeze — Key Indicator: Willingness to Lend

An abusive squeeze occurs when a person holds a dominant position in the supply of a financial instrument (or the underlying deliverable) and uses that position to distort the price at which others must settle delivery obligations **(UK MAR Art. 12(2)(a))**.

MAR 1.6.11G identifies the most important indicator of an abusive (as opposed to legitimate) squeeze: whether the person **is or is not willing to lend the instrument** to those who need to deliver against their settlement obligations. A person in a dominant position who is willing to lend is far less likely to be found to be running an abusive squeeze.

Additional indicators **(MAR 1.6.11G)**:
- Whether settlement default risk exists **across multiple parties** in linked chains (not merely bilateral) — multilateral distortion is a stronger indicator of abuse
- Whether the **spot-forward price relationship** is being artificially distorted (spot prices inflating relative to forward prices in a way inconsistent with carrying costs)

## MAR 1.7 — Manipulating Devices

This behaviour involves using fictitious devices or any other form of deception or contrivance that gives a false or misleading impression of the price or value of a financial instrument **(UK MAR Art. 12(1)(b))**. Examples from MAR 1.7 include:

- Wash trades (buying and selling between connected parties to generate artificial volume)
- Painting the tape (creating the impression of trading activity)
- Improper matched orders
- Layering / spoofing (placing and cancelling orders to move the price before trading)

---

## MAR 1.8 — Dissemination

Dissemination of information that gives or is likely to give false or misleading signals about supply, demand, or price of a financial instrument constitutes market abuse **(UK MAR Art. 12(1)(c))**. This extends to media, research notes, online forums, and social media.

### The Chinese Wall Defence

MAR 1.8.5G provides that if individuals within a firm could only have known that the information they were disseminating was false or misleading by having access to information held **behind a Chinese wall**, that fact indicates that those individuals did not know and could not reasonably be expected to know that the information was false or misleading.

This is the primary Chinese wall defence for dissemination claims — it does not provide a complete exemption, but it is a significant mitigating factor. The strength of the defence depends on the robustness of the information barrier and whether the individuals in question were genuinely unable to access the restricted information.

---

## MAR 1.9 — Misleading Behaviour and Market Distortion

MAR 1.9 addresses behaviour that does not involve a transaction but that distorts or is likely to distort the price of a financial instrument, or that provides false or misleading signals **(UK MAR Art. 12(1)(c)–(d))**. This includes:

- Making misleading statements about an instrument or issuer
- Creating an artificial appearance of activity through dissemination
- Acting in ways that would distort a benchmark or index

---

## MAR 1.10 — Statutory Exceptions

UK MAR provides three principal safe harbours that exempt otherwise potentially abusive behaviour:

| Exception | Statutory basis | Key conditions |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| **Buy-back programmes** | UK MAR Art. 5 | Disclosed, pre-announced, within volume/price limits, reported to FCA |
| **Price stabilisation** | UK MAR Art. 5; FCA MAR 2 | Stabilisation window, disclosure, overallotment, buy-back restrictions |
| **Accepted market practices (AMPs)** | UK MAR Art. 13 | FCA-recognised list; firm must demonstrate conformance |

These are complete defences — behaviour within the safe harbour **cannot** constitute market abuse even if it would otherwise fall within a prohibited behaviour.

## Key Points for Agents

- **Intent is not required (MAR 1.2.3G).** UK MAR catches market abuse regardless of whether the person intended to commit it. Negligent or accidental behaviour can constitute market abuse. Compliance programmes must be calibrated accordingly.
- **"Made public" includes information derivable with above-average expertise (MAR 1.2.12G–1.2.15G).** The test is whether the information is accessible to the market, not whether the average person would understand or find it. Fee-gated data and data requiring specialist analysis can be public.
- **Front-running and pre-positioning are named insider dealing (MAR 1.3.2G(2)).** Trading own account ahead of a pending client order, where knowledge of that order constitutes inside information, is expressly identified as insider dealing. Firms with execution flow must assess whether pending orders constitute inside information.
- **Selective analyst briefing is named unlawful disclosure (MAR 1.4.2G).** The confidentiality+necessity safe harbour requires: disclosure necessary for a legitimate purpose AND recipient under a duty of confidentiality. Both elements are required. Commercial relationships alone are not sufficient.
- **Willingness to lend is the key indicator in abusive squeeze assessment (MAR 1.6.11G).** A dominant holder who is willing to lend the instrument is far less likely to be found to be running an abusive squeeze. Additional factors: multilateral (not merely bilateral) settlement default risk; spot-forward price distortion.
- **Chinese wall is a dissemination defence but not a complete exemption (MAR 1.8.5G).** If an individual could only have known information was false/misleading by accessing restricted information held behind a Chinese wall, this indicates they lacked the relevant knowledge — the strength of the defence depends on the robustness of the barrier.

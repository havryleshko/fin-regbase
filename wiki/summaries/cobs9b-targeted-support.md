# COBS 9B — Targeted Support

COBS 9B creates a **new third category of investment service** sitting between execution-only and personalised investment advice. Introduced in April 2026, it allows firms to provide "ready-made suggestions" — recommendations designed for a pre-defined group (a "consumer segment") rather than for the specific individual. It is an optional regime: Consumer Duty does not require firms to provide targeted support (COBS 9B.1.2G), but firms that choose to do so must comply with the full COBS 9B framework.

The legal basis for targeted support is **Article 55A of the Regulated Activities Order** (RAO), which creates a new regulated activity distinct from investment advice under Art. 53 RAO.

---

## COBS 9B.1 — Purpose and Application

**(COBS 9B.1.1G)** The chapter is designed to help retail customers navigate investment decisions without having to receive full personalised investment advice. It addresses the "advice gap" by enabling firms to provide segment-level recommendations at lower cost than individually tailored suitability assessments.

**(COBS 9B.1.2G)** Consumer Duty does **not** require firms to provide targeted support. Firms that choose to provide it must comply with COBS 9B in full. Firms that do not provide it continue to rely on other support mechanisms (execution-only, appropriateness, full advice, signposting to MoneyHelper).

### Scope: Retail Clients Only

**(COBS 9B.2.1R/9B.3.4R)** Targeted support is available only for retail clients. Critically, even if a client is classified as a professional client for other purposes, **the firm must treat them as a retail client** for the purposes of COBS 9B. There is no MiFID opt-up equivalent for targeted support.

---

## COBS 9B.2 — Hard Prohibition: Safeguarded Benefits

**(COBS 9B.2.3R)** Where a client has **safeguarded benefits** (typically defined benefit pension rights, guaranteed annuity rates, or other protected benefits), a firm **must not** provide a ready-made suggestion unless the client has first received **regulated financial advice** from a firm authorised to give such advice.

This is an absolute prohibition — it cannot be waived by the client or by firm policy. It applies regardless of the value of the safeguarded benefits.

---

## COBS 9B.3 — Relationship to Suitability and Appropriateness

**(COBS 9B.3.3G)** Targeted support is **not subject to the suitability requirements** in COBS 9 or COBS 9A. The firm does not need to assess whether the ready-made suggestion is suitable for the individual client.

**(COBS 9B.3.3G)** Targeted support **displaces** both:
- The appropriateness obligation (COBS 10/10A) — because the firm is making a suggestion, not responding to a client-initiated transaction
- The suitability obligation (COBS 9/9A) — because the suggestion is at segment level, not individualised

See [[appropriateness-framework.md]] for the full hierarchy.

---

## COBS 9B.4 — Consumer Segments and Ready-Made Suggestions

This is the operational heart of COBS 9B. The firm designs consumer segments and ready-made suggestions in advance; the delivery mechanism then aligns individual clients with the appropriate segment.

### Consumer Segment Definition

**(COBS 9B.4.4R)** A consumer segment is a group of retail customers defined by common financial characteristics relevant to an investment decision. Segments are defined using:
- **Including characteristics** — characteristics a client must have to be in the segment
- **Excluding characteristics** — characteristics that disqualify a client from the segment

Both including and excluding characteristics must be specified.

### Pre-Definition Requirement

**(COBS 9B.4.5R)** A firm must:
1. **Pre-define all consumer segments before** providing any targeted support service
2. Specify exactly **one ready-made suggestion per consumer segment**

Consumer segments and their ready-made suggestions cannot be created on the fly for individual clients.

### One Segment Per Client

**(COBS 9B.4.7R)** A client may only be aligned with **one consumer segment** at any given time (though they may receive targeted support on different topics in different service interactions, subject to other rules).

### Granularity Rule

**(COBS 9B.4.9R)** Consumer segments must be defined with sufficient granularity that the ready-made suggestion is genuinely suitable for all individuals within the segment. An overly broad segment (e.g., "all retail clients") will not satisfy this requirement — the firm must identify characteristics specific enough to support a meaningful recommendation.

### Evidenced Assumptions

**(COBS 9B.4.11R)** All assumptions made about individuals in a consumer segment must be capable of being evidenced. The firm must be able to demonstrate, on request, the basis on which it concluded that clients with the defined characteristics constitute a coherent group for the purposes of the ready-made suggestion.

### Ready-Made Suggestion: Suitability at Segment Level

**(COBS 9B.4.20R)** The ready-made suggestion must be **suitable for the consumer segment as a whole**. This is a segment-level suitability assessment, not individual suitability — but it is still a suitability standard, not merely appropriateness. The firm must consider whether the suggestion would be suitable for any client who satisfies the segment characteristics.

## COBS 9B.4 — Hard Prohibitions

### Annuity Special Rules (COBS 9B.4.28R)

Where a ready-made suggestion relates to annuities:
- The firm must **not recommend a specific annuity product**
- The firm must **not obtain or share annuity quotes** as part of the targeted support service
- The firm **must direct the client to MoneyHelper** (or a whole-of-market comparison service) for further exploration

The firm may suggest that an annuity is an appropriate option for the segment, but cannot go further without triggering the full advice regime.

### Pension Consolidation Prohibition (COBS 9B.4.38R)

A firm must **not** make a ready-made suggestion that involves the consolidation of a client's pension rights into a new scheme. Pension consolidation is categorically excluded from targeted support — it requires full personalised suitability advice.

### Restricted Product Prohibition (COBS 9B.4.42R)

A firm must **not** make a ready-made suggestion that involves:
- **Restricted mass market investments (RMMIs)** — qualifying cryptoassets and certain other high-risk products
- **Non-mass market investments** — speculative illiquid securities, including mini-bonds and similar instruments
- **COBS 22-restricted products** — products subject to distribution restrictions

These products require the appropriateness regime (COBS 10/10A) or full advice. Targeted support cannot substitute.

## COBS 9B.5 — Delivery

### Firm-Initiated Delivery

**(COBS 9B.5.3R)** A firm may initiate a targeted support interaction without the client requesting it, provided:
1. The firm believes the client may benefit from targeted support at that point
2. The firm does not initiate a targeted support interaction with a client who has opted out (COBS 9B.5.14R)
3. The delivery complies with the financial promotions rules (COBS 4) applicable to the communication

### Alignment Gate (COBS 9B.5.5R)

Before delivering a ready-made suggestion, the firm must confirm that the client **aligns with the consumer segment** for which that suggestion was designed. A suggestion may not be delivered to a client who does not satisfy all the including characteristics or who satisfies any excluding characteristic for the relevant segment.

### Known-Unsuitability Prohibition (COBS 9B.5.11R)

Even where a client technically aligns with a consumer segment, the firm must **not deliver the ready-made suggestion** if it is aware (or ought reasonably to be aware) of information about the client's individual circumstances that indicates the suggestion would be **unsuitable for that specific client**.

This is the most significant delivery-stage obligation: it requires the firm to apply a known-individual-unsuitability filter on top of the segment-level suitability design.

### Client Opt-Out Right (COBS 9B.5.14R)

A firm must **provide the means** by which a client can, at any time, elect not to receive a targeted support service from the firm. Once a client has opted out, the firm must not deliver targeted support to that client unless the client subsequently re-opts in.

### Inconsistent Client Instructions (COBS 9B.5.15G)

Where a client requests execution of a transaction that is inconsistent with the ready-made suggestion the firm has provided, the firm must have regard to:
1. The **client's best interests rule**
2. The firm's obligation under **Consumer Duty** (PRIN 2A.5.3R — communications must support effective decision-making; PRIN 2A.6.2R(3) — give clients sufficient opportunity to understand and assess their options)

The firm is not required to prevent the transaction, but must not simply process it without considering these obligations.

## COBS 9B.6 — Disclosure

### Labelling Requirement (COBS 9B.6.1R)

When providing a ready-made suggestion, the firm must clearly refer to the service as **"Targeted Support"**. A firm may use a different brand name for the service in other communications, but at the point the ready-made suggestion is provided the description "Targeted Support" must be used.

### Nature and Limitations Disclosure (COBS 9B.6.3R)

The firm must disclose the nature and limitations of the targeted support service, including that it is **not a type of investment advice that involves more comprehensive, individualised advice** (for example, following a holistic review by an independent financial adviser).

### Segment Characteristics Disclosure (COBS 9B.6.5R)

When providing a ready-made suggestion, the firm must disclose to the client:
1. The **common characteristics** (both including and excluding characteristics) of the consumer segment with which the client has been aligned
2. That the ready-made suggestion was **designed for the consumer segment**, not specifically for the client
3. The **nature of any scope limitations** — in particular, if the firm has only considered products it issues or provides itself (i.e., if the ready-made suggestion is not based on a whole-of-market review)

### Article 55A RAO Statutory Statement (COBS 9B.6.6G)

The firm must make a statement to the client specifying:
1. That the recommendation is **not based on a comprehensive consideration** of the client's individual characteristics or circumstances
2. That the recommendation is **not specific to the client**
3. The characteristics, circumstances, or both, of the **group** used as the basis for the recommendation

This statement satisfies the conditions in Article 55A(4) and (5) RAO. A firm may satisfy COBS 9B.6.3R and 9B.6.5R simultaneously through this statement.

### Durable Medium and Timing (COBS 9B.6.17R)

The disclosures required by COBS 9B.6.3R and 9B.6.5R must be provided **in a durable medium** at the point the ready-made suggestion is delivered (or as soon as reasonably practicable thereafter if durable medium delivery at that exact point is not possible).

### Client Understanding (COBS 9B.6.9R)

A firm must **test its disclosures** around targeted support and take reasonable steps to ensure consumer understanding of those disclosures. This is an ongoing obligation, not a one-off exercise.

### Advised Clients (COBS 9B.6.16G)

Where a client is already receiving, or has recently received, investment advice from the firm or from another firm, the firm must take particular care to ensure the client understands the difference between targeted support and investment advice. The firm should take reasonable steps before delivering a ready-made suggestion to ensure the client understands this distinction.

---

## COBS 9B.7 — When Unable to Provide a Ready-Made Suggestion

If a firm cannot align a client with any consumer segment (e.g., because the client has an excluding characteristic, or the firm has identified known unsuitability), it must:
1. Consider what **alternative support** it can provide, including directing the client to other services such as investment advice or MoneyHelper
2. Comply with its Consumer Duty obligations — in particular PRIN 2A.2.1R (act in good faith) and PRIN 2A.2.2R (act consistently with reasonable consumer expectations)

Firms are reminded that FG21/1 (vulnerable customers guidance) is relevant to the treatment of clients who cannot be aligned with a consumer segment (COBS 9B.7.4G).

## COBS 9B.8 — Charging and Remuneration

### Commission Ban (COBS 9B.8.2R)

A firm providing targeted support must **not solicit or accept** (and must ensure its associates do not solicit or accept) any:
- Fees, commissions, or monetary benefits
- Non-monetary benefits

from any third party, or any person acting on behalf of a third party, **in connection with providing a targeted support service or any related service**.

"Related service" is defined broadly and includes:
- Arranging or executing a transaction that was the subject of a ready-made suggestion
- Managing a client relationship between the firm and a discretionary investment manager (DIM)
- Recommending a DIM or IFA to the client

The commission ban applies regardless of whether the firm intends to refund the payment to the client.

### Annuity Exception (COBS 9B.8.4R)

The commission ban does not apply to fees, commissions, or benefits received in connection with a targeted support service that:
1. Relates **exclusively to annuities** (as defined)
2. Is conducted in accordance with COBS 9B.4.28R to 9B.4.37G (the annuity special rules)
3. Is received as a result of a **referral to a whole-of-market annuity brokerage service** in accordance with COBS 9B.4.33R and 9B.4.35R

Outside this narrow exception, commissions from annuity providers in connection with targeted support are prohibited.

### Adviser Charging Rules Do Not Apply (COBS 9B.8.1G)

The adviser charging and remuneration rules in COBS 6.1A do **not** apply to firms providing targeted support. Targeted support has its own remuneration framework in COBS 9B.8.

### Non-Charging Firms: Fair Value Obligation (COBS 9B.8.7R)

A firm that does not charge clients for targeted support must ensure that **the arrangements by which it is remunerated** for providing the service are consistent with its obligation under Consumer Duty to ensure that products provide fair value (PRIN 2A.4.2R). This prevents cross-subsidy arrangements that conflict with fair value obligations.

### Charge Difference Disclosure (COBS 9B.8.8R)

Where the costs and charges of an investment differ between:
- A client who buys having received targeted support, and
- A client who buys without having received targeted support

The firm must clearly disclose the nature and extent of that difference to the client when the ready-made suggestion is communicated.

### Issuer/Product Provider Obligations (COBS 9B.9.3R)

Product issuers and providers must **not** offer or pay any fees, commissions, or monetary or non-monetary benefits to a firm in connection with that firm's provision of a targeted support service (or related services). This is the mirror of COBS 9B.8.2R on the product side. The same annuity exception (COBS 9B.8.4R) applies.

## COBS 9B.10 — Systems and Controls

### Policies and Procedures (COBS 9B.10.1R)

A firm must establish and maintain **systems and controls** to ensure compliance with the COBS 9B requirements relating to the design and delivery of targeted support. This includes:
- Segment design and documentation
- Alignment processes
- Delivery procedures and opt-out mechanisms
- Monitoring of outcomes

### Employee Training (COBS 9B.10.3G)

Employees involved in the design or delivery of targeted support must be **appropriately trained and supervised**, in particular to:
1. Identify when a client aligns with a consumer segment
2. Appropriately consider and respond to information provided by a client that goes beyond what is needed for alignment

### Ongoing Monitoring (COBS 9B.10.4G / 9B.10.6R)

**(COBS 9B.10.4G)** Firms must monitor the outcomes experienced by retail customers from their targeted support service. Monitoring must cover whether:
- Consumer segments are appropriately designed
- Ready-made suggestions remain suitable for their segments
- Alignment processes are correctly implemented
- Ready-made suggestions are not leading to harm

**(COBS 9B.10.6R)** For each product that has been or may be recommended in a ready-made suggestion, the firm must have arrangements in place to review information from the product manufacturer about significant product adaptations or changes, and consider whether those changes mean the product should no longer be recommended.

### No Periodic Individual Suitability Assessment Required (COBS 9B.10.5G)

Unlike COBS 9A (which requires periodic suitability reviews at least annually for ongoing advised relationships), **COBS 9B does not require periodic individual suitability reassessments** for clients who have received a targeted support service. Monitoring is at the segment and product level, not the individual level.

### Regular Review (COBS 9B.10.9R)

A firm must review its targeted support service — including its consumer segments and ready-made suggestions — **regularly and with appropriate frequency**. Factors relevant to frequency include: complexity of segments, complexity of products recommended, number of clients served, and relevant market or regulatory changes.

---

## Key Points for Agents

- **Three hard prohibitions** that cannot be waived: (1) safeguarded benefits — client must get advice first (9B.2.3R); (2) pension consolidation — categorically excluded (9B.4.38R); (3) RMMIs, non-mass market investments, and COBS 22-restricted products — cannot be recommended via targeted support (9B.4.42R).
- **Commission ban** (9B.8.2R): no third-party fees, commissions, or monetary/non-monetary benefits in connection with providing targeted support or any related service (including arranging the resulting transaction). The only exception is the narrow whole-of-market annuity brokerage referral route.
- **Art. 55A RAO statement mandatory** at point of delivery (9B.6.6G): the recommendation is not comprehensive, not specific to the client, and is based on group characteristics. Must be in durable medium (9B.6.17R).
- **Known-unsuitability prohibition** (9B.5.11R): even if a client aligns with a segment, a ready-made suggestion must not be delivered if the firm knows (or ought to know) of individual circumstances making it unsuitable for that specific client.
- **No periodic individual suitability review** required (unlike COBS 9A) — monitoring is at segment and product level, not the individual client level.

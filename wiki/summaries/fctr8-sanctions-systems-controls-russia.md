# FCA FCTR 8 — Sanctions Systems and Controls: Firms' Response to Increased Sanctions (Russia)

## What This Document Is

This is FCTR 8 — the FCA's thematic review of sanctions systems and controls, cross-referenced from FCG 7.3.1. Published June 2023. It reports findings from assessments of sanctions controls across **over 90 firms** spanning retail banking, wholesale banking, wealth management, insurance, electronic money, and payments. It was prompted by the unprecedented scale of UK and international sanctions imposed following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022.

The FCA assessed firms using two mechanisms: its own **Sanctions Screening Tool (SST)** — an in-house analytics-based tool that objectively tests how well a firm identifies sanctioned individuals and entities using test data — and firm-specific intelligence from multiple sources including self-reporting.

---

## Theme 1 — Governance and Oversight

### Horizon Scanning and Scenario Planning

Firms that had conducted risk exposure assessments and scenario planning **before** the Russian invasion were materially better placed to implement sanctions at speed. The FCA explicitly frames this horizon-scanning as "an important process for firms to adopt as part of their risk management procedures."

Firms that had not done so are expected to have conducted a **lessons learned exercise** of their Russia response. This is not optional post-event reflection — the FCA frames it as preparation for future events.

### Senior Management MI Failures

The FCA identified instances where senior management were not given sufficient MI to discharge their responsibilities, including:

- Multinational firms relying on globally-run systems with limited knowledge of how those systems operated in the UK context
- Firms with inadequate oversight and MI of UK-related activities undertaken by globally-run teams
- MI limited to only basic or no metrics — lacking the number of sanctions alerts, number of alerts awaiting analysis, or reports submitted to OFSI

**What adequate sanctions MI must contain:**
- Number of screening alerts generated
- Number of alerts awaiting analysis (backlog metric)
- Number of reports submitted to OFSI
- Qualitative and quantitative trend analysis sufficient for risk identification and effective decision-making

Without this, the FCA found that senior management were "not able to understand the risks at the firm to aid effective decision making or understand how it was performing."

### Global vs. UK Sanctions Policies — OFAC Divergence Risk

Multiple firms operated global sanctions policies that were not aligned with the UK sanctions regime. The named failure pattern: firms focused on US OFAC sanctions and applied insufficient focus to the UK regime (OFSI / Consolidated List), particularly where sanctions controls were run from global centres of excellence.

Since UK sanctions diverge from US sanctions — especially post-Brexit — a policy calibrated for OFAC is not a substitute for UK compliance. The FCA noted that poor communication between global and regional sanctions teams compounds this risk. Firms must ensure UK legislation is understood and followed even where global teams lead on sanctions operationally.

### Over-reliance on Third-Party Screening Tools

Firms that could not explain how their third-party screening tool was calibrated, when lists were updated, or whether the correct lists were being screened were assessed as unable to demonstrate adequate risk management. This means:

- Firms did not know if their system was missing names that should be identified
- Firms did not know if their system was producing too many false positives
- Firms were unable to show they were adequately managing their risk

The FCA's position: third-party screening tools are treated like any outsourced service. Firms must have appropriate control and oversight, including regular testing and agreed SLAs covering the time taken for lists to be updated following a new designation.

### Contingency Planning

Good practice: firms that had conducted Russia-specific risk assessments and developed contingency plans (enhanced high-risk country lists, revised thresholds, escalation policies, suspending payments to/from Russia) were better placed to act. Lessons learned exercises and forward-looking planning for future escalations are expected.

## Theme 2 — Skills and Resources

The FCA identified **significant backlogs** in the assessment, escalation, and reporting of screening alerts at many firms. These backlogs:

- Directly affected firms' ability to promptly identify and report sanctions exposures
- Persisted for significant periods due to lack of appropriate resourcing
- Were compounded by lack of governance and appropriate internal SLAs

In many cases, firms had taken partial action by blocking accounts or transactions at the point of alerting — but this did not resolve the backlog, and it left unclear which true positive alerts required action and how they should be prioritised.

Increased alert volumes and pressure on sanctions teams increase the risk of errors. Resource strain in operational teams was found to reduce clarity on alert prioritisation.

Some firms lacked adequate internal expertise entirely, relying on external legal or consulting resource to perform effective timely screening.

## Theme 3 — Screening Capabilities

### Calibration Balance

The FCA found that miscalibration ran in both directions:

| Calibration failure | Consequence |
|---------------------|-------------|
| Too sensitive (too many false positives) | Increased stretch on already busy teams; alert review becomes operationally inefficient; increased risk of errors |
| Not sensitive enough (insufficient fuzzy matching) | Minor name variations cause sanctioned individuals to be missed entirely |

Firms must understand how their systems work and how they are calibrated. Ongoing sample testing and parameter tuning — demonstrated to the FCA — was found to be good practice.

### SST Testing Results

The FCA's SST testing found that **some firms' systems were unable to generate alerts against certain names on OFSI's Consolidated List** — and some of those firms were unable to provide reasonable justification for the omissions. This is the clearest statement in any FCA publication that screening gaps will be identified and must be explained.

### List Update SLAs

Updating of screening lists following designations was often not subject to SLAs. Firms were not monitoring how quickly their lists were being updated. The FCA expects SLAs for list update timing and monitoring of compliance with those SLAs.

## Theme 4 — Customer Due Diligence and KYC

As well as backlogs created by increased designation volumes, the FCA found qualitative CDD failures that raised independent sanctions risk:

- CDD that did not articulate full ownership structures of entities — preventing firms from screening all relevant parties
- Low-quality CDD and KYC assessments generally increasing the risk that sanctioned individuals would not be identified
- CDD review backlogs creating an ongoing gap in sanctions screening coverage

**Core point:** CDD quality is a direct input to sanctions screening effectiveness. A firm with weak beneficial ownership identification cannot screen completely regardless of how good its screening tool is.

## Theme 5 — Breach Reporting to the FCA

Firms must report to OFSI (mandatory) and notify the FCA under Principle 11, SUP 15.3.8G(2), and FCG 7 where: a person they deal with is a designated person; they hold frozen assets; or they discover or suspect a breach.

The FCA found inconsistencies in reporting practice:

| Failure | FCA position |
|---------|-------------|
| Firms taking weeks or months from identifying a breach to reporting | Unacceptable — timeliness is a compliance obligation, not a discretionary choice |
| Firms fully investigating the breach before reporting | Wrong — investigation completion is **not** a precondition for notification |
| Firms that failed to report at all | Named as a distinct failure mode |

**The FCA's rationale:** "Firms delaying breach reporting, or not reporting at all, undermines our ability to understand systems and controls issues as they occur and to work with firms to establish that those issues are being correctly remedied."

## FCA Supervisory Actions and Tools

| Action | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Sanctions Screening Tool (SST) | In-house analytics-based tool; objectively tests firms' screening against test data; already deployed across the 90+ firm assessment programme |
| ~10,000 firm and professional body communications | Letters setting out FCA expectations on sanctions systems and controls, including notification obligation for OFSI reports |
| OPBAS engagement | Professional Body Supervisors directed to raise awareness and promote compliance within legal and accountancy sectors |
| Regulatory tools applied | Skilled person reviews; business restrictions; enforcement where serious misconduct identified |
| Ongoing intelligence-based monitoring | FCA acting on self-reporting and multi-source intelligence; following up directly with firms |

## Key Points for Agents

- The FCA operates a **Sanctions Screening Tool (SST)** — an in-house analytics-based tool that proactively tests firms' screening systems against test data. Firms can be assessed against it. Some firms were found unable to generate alerts for Consolidated List names and unable to justify the omissions.
- **Horizon scanning and lessons learned** are positive obligations: firms should have conducted a Russia-response lessons learned exercise and should maintain forward-looking contingency planning as part of sanctions risk management.
- **UK vs. OFAC divergence:** Global firms focused on US OFAC policies face explicit regulatory risk — UK sanctions (OFSI / Consolidated List) diverge from OFAC. Global policies not calibrated to the UK regime are a named failure.
- **Adequate sanctions MI** must include at minimum: number of alerts generated, number of alerts awaiting analysis (backlog), reports submitted to OFSI, and qualitative trend data. MI that lacks these metrics is insufficient for senior management to discharge their responsibilities.
- **Alert backlogs** are a direct sanctions compliance risk — they prevent timely identification and reporting of true positives and increase the risk of errors. Backlogs compounded by lack of internal SLAs are a named poor practice.
- **Breach reporting timeline:** Investigation completion is **not** a precondition for FCA notification. Weeks- or months-long delays between identifying a breach and reporting it to the FCA are a named failure. Failure to report at all is a separate named failure.
- **Third-party tool oversight:** SLAs covering list update timing — and monitoring of compliance with those SLAs — are required. Firms unable to explain how a third-party tool is calibrated or when lists are updated are assessed as unable to demonstrate adequate risk management.

# DISP 3 — FOS Complaint Handling Procedures

## Purpose and Scope

DISP 3 sets out: (1) the procedures of the Financial Ombudsman Service for investigating and determining complaints; (2) the basis on which the Ombudsman makes decisions; and (3) the awards the Ombudsman can make **(DISP 3.1.1G)**. It applies to the Ombudsman and to respondents.

---

## Jurisdiction Check

Before proceeding on the merits, the FOS will consider whether a complaint is out of jurisdiction **(DISP 3.2.1R)**.

Where the respondent has not yet had 8 weeks to consider the complaint (or, for EMD complaints and PSD complaints, the time period specified in DISP 2.8.1R(2A)), and the respondent has not issued a final response or summary resolution communication, the FOS will **refer the complaint back to the respondent** — except where DISP 2.8.1R(4) applies (exceptional circumstances where immediate FOS access is permitted without waiting) **(DISP 3.2.2R)**.

Similarly, if the complaint falls within a consumer redress scheme and the scheme's time limits have not expired, the FOS will refer it to the respondent to be dealt with under the scheme **(DISP 3.2.2AR)**.

Where either party raises a jurisdiction question, both parties (or the complainant) are given an opportunity to make representations before the Ombudsman decides **(DISP 3.2.3R–3.2.4R)**. Reasons are given for all jurisdiction decisions **(DISP 3.2.5R–3.2.6R)**.

---

## Dismissal Without Considering the Merits

### Post-9 July 2015 Complaints (DISP 3.3.4AR)

For complaints referred to the FOS on or after 9 July 2015, the Ombudsman may dismiss without considering merits only on five grounds:

1. The complaint is frivolous or vexatious.
2. The subject matter is being dealt with, or has been dealt with, by a comparable ADR entity.
3. The subject matter has been the subject of court proceedings where there has been a decision on the merits.
4. The subject matter is the subject of current court proceedings — unless proceedings are stayed or sisted so the FOS may consider it.
5. Dealing with the complaint would otherwise seriously impair the effective operation of the Financial Ombudsman Service.

**Critical difference from the pre-2015 regime:** There is no "fair offer made" dismissal ground for post-2015 complaints. A respondent that has made what it considers a fair offer cannot use that offer to obtain FOS dismissal without consideration of the merits — the FOS will still determine the complaint.

### Pre-9 July 2015 Complaints (DISP 3.3.4R)

A much broader set of 17 dismissal grounds applies to complaints referred before 9 July 2015. These include (among others): no financial loss/distress/inconvenience; no reasonable prospect of success; respondent has already made a fair and reasonable open offer; complaint already reviewed under regulatory standards (with redress offer); previously considered by the FOS or former scheme; subject of court proceedings; commercial judgment complaint; investment performance complaint; employment complaint; will/trust discretion complaint.

In all cases, the complainant is given an opportunity to make representations before the Ombudsman decides to dismiss **(DISP 3.3.1R)**, and reasons are given **(DISP 3.3.2R)**.

---

## Test Cases

The Ombudsman may, with the complainant's consent, cease to consider the merits of a complaint so that it may be referred to a court as a test case, if **(DISP 3.4.2R)**:

1. Before determination, the respondent has provided in writing: (a) a detailed statement of why the complaint raises an important or novel point of law with significant consequences; and (b) an undertaking to pay the complainant's reasonable costs if the complainant or respondent commences court proceedings within six months of the complaint being dismissed.
2. The Ombudsman considers the complaint raises an important or novel point of law with important consequences, and would more suitably be dealt with by a court.

Factors include: centrality of the legal point; novelty; significance of consequences; amount at stake; remedies a court could impose; stage already reached; representations made **(DISP 3.4.3G)**.

---

## Investigation Procedure

### Resolution Attempt

The Ombudsman will attempt to resolve complaints at the earliest possible stage by whatever means appear most appropriate, including mediation or investigation **(DISP 3.5.1R)**.

### Investigation Steps

Where investigation is necessary **(DISP 3.5.4R)**:

1. Both parties are given an opportunity to make representations.
2. A **provisional assessment** is sent to both parties, setting out the Ombudsman's reasons and a time limit for response.
3. If either party **indicates disagreement** within that time limit, the Ombudsman proceeds to **determination**.

If neither party disagrees within the time limit, the provisional assessment stands as the determination.

### Hearings

The Ombudsman will determine a complaint without a hearing unless he considers one is necessary **(DISP 3.5.5R)**. A party wishing to request a hearing must do so in writing, setting out the issues and any reasons why the hearing should be in private **(DISP 3.5.6R)**. Hearings may be held by any means the Ombudsman considers appropriate, including by telephone. No hearing may be held after determination.

### Evidence

The Ombudsman may **(DISP 3.5.8R–3.5.12G)**:
- Give directions as to what evidence is required, whether it should be oral or written, and how it should be presented.
- **Exclude evidence** that would otherwise be admissible in a court, or **include evidence** that would not be admissible in a court.
- Accept information **in confidence** — where information is accepted confidentially, only an edited version, summary or description is disclosed to the other party.
- Reach a decision on the basis of what has been supplied, and **take account of a party's failure** to provide requested information — in effect, adverse inferences may be drawn.
- Treat the complaint as withdrawn if a complainant fails to supply requested information.
- Take evidence from third parties, including the FCA, other regulators, experts in industry matters, and experts in consumer matters.

### Time Limits

The Ombudsman may fix and extend time limits for any aspect of consideration **(DISP 3.5.13R)**.

- **Respondent fails to comply with a time limit:** The Ombudsman may proceed and may include in any award an amount for material distress or inconvenience caused by the respondent's failure to comply **(DISP 3.5.14R)**.
- **Complainant fails to comply with a time limit:** The Ombudsman may proceed, or may treat the complaint as withdrawn and cease to consider the merits **(DISP 3.5.15R)**.

---

## Determination

### The "Fair and Reasonable" Standard

The Ombudsman will determine a complaint by reference to **what is, in his opinion, fair and reasonable in all the circumstances of the case** **(DISP 3.6.1R)**. This is the operative standard for the Compulsory Jurisdiction (FSMA s.228) and is extended to the Voluntary Jurisdiction by DISP 3.6.1R.

In considering what is fair and reasonable, the Ombudsman will take into account **(DISP 3.6.4R)**:
1. **Relevant law and regulations**
2. **Regulators' rules, guidance and standards**
3. **Codes of practice**
4. **(Where appropriate) what the Ombudsman considers to have been good industry practice at the relevant time**

This is not a purely legal test. The Ombudsman applies the standard of what a reasonable, compliant firm would have done at the time — including compliance with guidance and codes that may not be strictly legally binding.

Where complaints against multiple respondents arise from connected circumstances, the Ombudsman may apportion the overall award between respondents **(DISP 3.6.3G)**.

### Determination Process

When the Ombudsman has determined a complaint **(DISP 3.6.6R)**:

1. The Ombudsman gives both parties a **signed written statement** of the determination, with reasons.
2. The statement requires the complainant to notify the Ombudsman, before a specified date, whether they **accept or reject** the determination.
3. If the complainant **accepts within the time limit**: the determination is **final and binding on both parties**.
4. If the complainant **does not notify acceptance within the time limit**: the complainant is treated as having **rejected** the determination, and **neither party is bound** by it.
5. **Exception to deemed rejection (DISP 3.6.6R(4A)):** A complainant is not treated as having rejected the determination if all of: (a) the complainant notifies acceptance after the specified date; (b) the complainant has not previously notified rejection; and (c) in the Ombudsman's view, the failure to comply was due to exceptional circumstances.
6. The Ombudsman notifies the respondent of the outcome and, if the complainant is treated as having rejected, of the effect of DISP 3.6.6R(4A).

**Clerical corrections:** The Ombudsman may correct clerical mistakes in the written statement, whether or not the determination has been accepted or rejected. A procedural irregularity does not of itself render the determination void **(DISP 3.6.7R)**.

## Awards

### Types of Award

Where a complaint is determined in favour of the complainant, the Ombudsman's determination may include **(DISP 3.7.1R)**:

1. A **money award** against the respondent
2. An **interest award** against the respondent
3. A **costs award** against the respondent
4. A **direction** to the respondent

### Money Awards

A money award may be fair compensation for one or more of the following **(DISP 3.7.2R)**:

- Financial loss, including **consequential or prospective loss**
- Pain and suffering
- Damage to reputation
- Distress or inconvenience

This compensation is available **whether or not a court would award it** — the FOS is not bound by the limitations of tortious or contractual damages assessment.

**Maximum award limits (DISP 3.7.4R)** — CPI-adjusted on 1 April each year:

| Date complaint referred to FOS | Act/omission before 1 April 2019 | Act/omission on/after 1 April 2019 |
|-------------------------------|----------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| On or after 1 April 2026 | **£205,000** | **£455,000** |
| On or after 1 April 2025 | £200,000 | £445,000 |
| On or after 1 April 2024 | £195,000 | £430,000 |
| On or after 1 April 2023 | £190,000 | £415,000 |

For the purpose of calculating the maximum, the following are excluded: interest awarded on the money award; costs awarded; interest on costs **(DISP 3.7.5G)**.

Where fair compensation requires a larger amount than the cap, the Ombudsman may **recommend** the respondent pays the balance — this recommendation is not legally binding but FOS records it **(DISP 3.7.6G)**. The Ombudsman maintains a register of each money award **(DISP 3.7.7R)**.

### Interest Awards

An interest award may provide for the amount payable under the money award to bear interest at a rate and from a date specified in the award **(DISP 3.7.8R)**.

### Costs Awards

A costs award may be such amount as the Ombudsman considers fair, to cover some or all of the complainant's reasonably incurred costs, plus interest **(DISP 3.7.9R)**. In most cases complainants should not need professional advisers, so costs awards are expected to be uncommon **(DISP 3.7.10G)**.

### Directions

A direction may require the respondent to take **such steps as the Ombudsman considers just and appropriate — whether or not a court could order those steps** **(DISP 3.7.11R)**. This is a broader power than a court judgment.

### Compliance with Awards

A respondent must comply promptly with any award or direction made by the Ombudsman, and with any settlement agreed at an earlier stage of the procedure **(DISP 3.7.12R)**. A complainant who has accepted a money award or direction may enforce it through the courts **(DISP 3.7.13G)**.

---

## Information Handling

In dealing with information received during complaint consideration, the FOS will have regard to the parties' rights of privacy **(DISP 3.8.1R)**. This does not prevent the Ombudsman disclosing information: to the extent required or authorised by law; to the parties; in his determination; at a hearing **(DISP 3.8.2BR)**.

The Ombudsman may disclose information to the FCA or any other body exercising regulatory or statutory functions for the purpose of assisting that body or the FOS to discharge its functions **(DISP 3.8.3R)**.

---

## Delegation

The Ombudsman may delegate most powers to FOS staff. Non-delegable powers are: (1) determination of a complaint; and (2) authorising disclosure of information to the FCA or other regulatory bodies **(DISP 3.9.1AR)**.

In practice, most references to "the Ombudsman" in DISP 2 to DISP 4 include FOS staff to whom the relevant power has been delegated **(DISP 3.9.2G)**.

---

## Key Points for Agents

- **The "fair and reasonable" standard (DISP 3.6.1R; FSMA s.228) is not a legal test.** The Ombudsman applies what a reasonable, compliant firm would have done — including compliance with guidance, codes, and good industry practice at the time. A respondent that was technically legally compliant but departed from FCA guidance or industry codes may still lose at FOS.
- **Post-9 July 2015 complaints have only five dismissal grounds (DISP 3.3.4AR).** There is no "fair offer made" dismissal ground — making a reasonable settlement offer does not allow a respondent to obtain FOS dismissal without merits consideration. The FOS will assess the complaint regardless.
- **The provisional assessment stage is an opportunity to settle.** After the Ombudsman issues a provisional assessment with reasons, either party may indicate disagreement and trigger a full determination. Respondents should use the provisional assessment stage to assess whether settlement is preferable to determination.
- **Award limits (from 1 April 2026): £455,000 for post-April 2019 acts; £205,000 for pre-April 2019 acts (DISP 3.7.4R).** Adjusted by CPI annually. Interest, costs, and interest on costs are excluded from the cap calculation. Above-cap recommendations are non-binding.
- **FOS money awards cover distress, pain, suffering, and reputational damage — categories not available in many court claims (DISP 3.7.2R).** This means FOS awards can exceed what a court would order for the same underlying facts.
- **Direction power is broader than court order (DISP 3.7.11R).** FOS may require steps a court could not order.
- **Acceptance = final and binding (DISP 3.6.6R).** Silence within the time limit = deemed rejection (neither party bound). Late acceptance is only possible if: no prior rejection + exceptional circumstances.
- **Respondent's time limit failure can itself generate an award (DISP 3.5.14R).** Delay in responding to FOS requests is not a cost-free strategy — it may result in an additional distress component in the award.
- **FOS may draw adverse inferences from non-cooperation** — evidence requests that respondents ignore or obstruct may lead to findings against the respondent based on the available evidence.
- **Compliance with awards and settlements is mandatory and promptly required (DISP 3.7.12R).** Money awards and directions are court-enforceable by the complainant.

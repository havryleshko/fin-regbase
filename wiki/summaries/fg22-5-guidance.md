# FCA FG22/5 — Final Non-Handbook Guidance for Firms on the Consumer Duty

## What This Document Is

FG22/5 is the FCA's finalised non-Handbook guidance for firms on the Consumer Duty, published July 2022 alongside PS22/9. It is issued under section 139A of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000. **(FG22/5 §1.10)**

Where PS22/9 sets out the rules and explains policy decisions, FG22/5 explains **how the rules apply in practice** — with worked examples of good and poor practice, sector-specific clarifications, and the supervisory questions firms can expect to be asked. It is the primary operational reference for Consumer Duty compliance.

FG22/5 does not replace or create obligations beyond those in the rules. Where it says "must" that tracks a rule; "should" means a recommended course of action to comply; "may/could" means one of several permissible approaches. **(FG22/5 §1.15)**

## What FG22/5 Adds to PS22/9

FG22/5 goes substantially beyond PS22/9 in five areas:

### 1. Concrete Good/Poor Practice Examples

Each outcome chapter contains named scenarios illustrating the boundary between compliant and non-compliant conduct. These are directly usable as assessment benchmarks. Selected examples:

- **Sludge (poor practice):** A firm requires a radio-button opt-in for a savings rate increase, deliberately designed using its own behavioural analysis showing customers won't find it — so it avoids paying the higher rate. **(FG22/5 §9, savings rate example)**
- **Sludge (poor practice):** A firm requires customers to phone to switch provider, then subjects them to a lengthy retention process. **(FG22/5 §9.27)**
- **Bereavement (poor practice):** Banks with over-complex bereavement procedures, over-reliant on manual processes, leading to errors in returning funds to beneficiaries. **(FG22/5 §9.37)**
- **Outsourcing (poor practice):** An investment platform delays processing a customer's platform switch instruction because there is no commercial benefit to the platform in doing so. **(FG22/5 §9.38)**
- **Monitoring (good practice):** A firm identifies calls being terminated before answered, proactively contacts those customers, and redirects resource. **(FG22/5 §9.51)**

### 2. Sludge and Positive Friction

FG22/5 explicitly names **sludge** (harmful friction) and distinguishes it from **positive friction** (beneficial friction for high-risk products). Both concepts are absent from PS22/9. See [[sludge-and-friction]].

### 3. Supervisory Questions Per Outcome

Each chapter ends with FCA supervisory questions. These are not in PS22/9. See [[fca-supervisory-questions-consumer-duty]].

### 4. Sector-Specific Scope Clarifications

FG22/5 §2.3 maps the "retail customer" definition to the specific sourcebook test in each sector:

| Sector | Sourcebook | Retail customer scope |
|--------|------------|----------------------|
| Consumer credit | CONC | All regulated credit-related activities |
| Deposit-taking | BCOBS | Consumers, micro-enterprises, charities <£1m, natural persons acting as trustees outside trade/business |
| Insurance | ICOBS | Excludes reinsurance, large commercial risks, group insurance extension |
| Investments | COBS | Customers who are not professional clients (elected under COBS) |
| Mortgages | MCOB | All regulated mortgage contracts; excludes unregulated buy-to-let, commercial lending |
| Payments/e-money | PSRs/EMRs | Consumers, micro-enterprises, small charities |

**(FG22/5 §2.3)**

**Critical addition:** A firm that **encourages a customer to opt up to professional client status specifically to avoid Consumer Duty protections breaches the Duty**. The Duty applies to the process of determining client status. **(FG22/5 §2.10)**

### 5. Culture Governance — Four Drivers Model

FG22/5 Chapter 10 sets out a four-driver culture model: **Purpose, Leadership, People, Governance**. Each driver must actively embed the Duty. The Consumer Duty champion at board level should be an Independent Non-Executive Director (NED) where possible. **(FG22/5 §10.6, §10.10)** See [[vulnerable-customers]] for the monitoring implications.

## Co-Manufacturer Written Agreement Requirement

Where two firms co-manufacture a product or service, they must have a **written agreement** setting out their respective roles and responsibilities under the products and services and price and value outcomes. **(FG22/5 §6.11–§6.12)** This is operationally new — not explicitly stated in PS22/9.

## Vested Rights — Clarified Definition

FG22/5 §3.19–§3.20 provides a more precise definition of "vested contractual rights" than PS22/9:

- Vested rights include: pre-existing contractual rights to which the firm already has legal entitlement (e.g. annual fees due); rights to payments on a contractually specified event (e.g. exit charges).
- Where a customer can terminate without an exit charge, future charge payments are **not** vested rights — they are only expectations. **(FG22/5 §3.20)**

This clarification matters for closed-book reviews: a firm assessing whether it needs to change charges must first determine whether those charges constitute vested rights.

## Key Points for Agents

- FG22/5 is the operational companion to PS22/9 — it provides worked examples, sector scope mappings, supervisory questions, and the four-driver culture model.
- Firms should be assessed against both the rules (PS22/9/PRIN 2A) and the guidance (FG22/5) — the guidance clarifies what the rules require in practice.
- Co-manufacturers must have a written agreement on their respective Duty responsibilities.
- Encouraging a customer to opt up to professional client status to avoid Consumer Duty protections is itself a breach.
- Vested rights under closed-book contracts are narrower than firms may assume: where a customer can terminate without penalty, future charges are not vested.

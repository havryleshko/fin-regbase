# FCA Priorities for Payments Firms — Portfolio Letter (March 2023)

## What This Document Is

A supervisory portfolio letter issued on 16 March 2023 by Matthew Long (Director, Payments & Digital Assets) to CEOs of all firms supervised by the FCA within the payments portfolio — specifically firms authorised or registered under the Payment Services Regulations 2017 (PSRs) and the Electronic Money Regulations 2011 (EMRs): Payment Institutions (PIs), Electronic Money Institutions (EMIs), and Registered Account Information Service Providers (RAISPs).

The letter sets out three outcomes the FCA expects payments firms to deliver, three cross-cutting priorities, and discrete action items for each. It explicitly states that the FCA will "take swift and assertive action to protect customers" where it identifies issues, and warns that firms that cannot meet authorisation conditions will face removal or sanction.

---

## Three Outcomes

### Outcome 1: Customers' Money Is Safe

Three priorities under this outcome:

**Priority 1: Safeguarding.** Customer funds must be returned quickly if the firm enters insolvency. Named common failings: (i) no documented process for identifying "relevant funds"; (ii) inadequate reconciliation procedures; (iii) lack of due diligence and acknowledgement of segregation from credit institutions providing safeguarding accounts. Firms are required to undertake reconciliations at least once a day and to notify the FCA without delay if safeguarding requirements are not being met. Since July 2020 (now incorporated in the Approach Document), firms subject to a statutory audit must also undertake an annual safeguarding audit. *(Portfolio letter, Outcome 1, Priority 1)*

**Priority 2: Prudential Risk Management.** The FCA flags that many PIs and EMIs are unprofitable and reliant on external funding. Named common failings: inadequate liquidity risk management; failure to consider holding capital above the regulatory minimum; lack of scenario planning and stress testing. Firms must meet the regulatory capital requirement at all times, set or review risk appetite, and forecast financial performance under stressed scenarios. Reference guidance: Approach Document and FG20/1. *(Portfolio letter, Outcome 1, Priority 2)*

**Priority 3: Wind-Down Planning.** Guidance issued July 2020, now in the Approach Document §3.73–3.76. Named common plan failings: over-optimistic timelines; insufficient step-by-step detail; no consideration of wind-down triggers; inadequate cost and cash analysis. Firms may refer to Wind-down Planning Guide and TR22/1 thematic review as good practice. *(Portfolio letter, Outcome 1, Priority 3)*

---

### Outcome 2: Financial System Integrity

Two priorities under this outcome:

**Priority 1: Money Laundering and Sanctions.** Named common failings: failure to evidence adequate KYC/CDD; business-wide risk assessments not supported by robust methodology; EDD not commensurate with risk; failure to review and refresh risk assessments; policies insufficiently detailed; control framework not evolving with business growth; third-party screening solutions not adequately calibrated; inability to justify why sanctions screening solutions do not generate alerts. Firms must ensure AML systems are effective, commensurate with business risk, and that SARs submissions under POCA 2002 and the Terrorism Act 2000 are accurate and timely. *(Portfolio letter, Outcome 2, Priority 1)*

**Priority 2: Fraud.** Named common failings: insufficient customer education on fraud prevention; lack of engagement with industry information-sharing bodies; weak anti-fraud controls; backlogs in actioning customer fraud reports; high proportion of accounts receiving proceeds of fraud. Firms must immediately review risk appetite, policies and systems for fraud prevention and ensure CDD controls detect fraud-enabling accounts. *(Portfolio letter, Outcome 2, Priority 2)*

---

### Outcome 3: Customers' Needs Are Met

Firms must implement the Consumer Duty. The FCA references its February 2023 Consumer Duty letter to payments firms as the primary action document. *(Portfolio letter, Outcome 3)*

---

## Three Cross-Cutting Priorities

### Cross-Cutting Priority 1: Governance, Leadership, and Agent/Distributor Oversight

Named governance failings: lack of knowledgeable and experienced personnel in key functions (including MLRO and compliance); governance arrangements not proportionate to scale and complexity; inadequate pre-onboarding due diligence on agents and distributors; lack of ongoing monitoring of agents/distributors.

**Agent/distributor obligations (specific reminders in the letter):**

- **Agents:** The PI or EMI is responsible for registering its agents with the FCA. The firm must take all reasonable steps to ensure agents comply with the PSRs/EMRs, both at onboarding and on an ongoing basis, with appropriate oversight systems and controls.
- **Distributors (EMIs only):** EMIs must identify proposed use of distributors to the FCA — but individual distributors do not need to be registered. The EMI is responsible for anything done or omitted by a distributor. **Distributors are not permitted to provide payment services** — they may only distribute and redeem e-money.
- **Communications:** Where agents or distributors advertise or promote regulated activities, communications must be fair, clear and not misleading, and must identify the PI or EMI for whom the agent or distributor is acting.

*(Portfolio letter, Cross-Cutting Priority 1)*

---

### Cross-Cutting Priority 2: Operational Resilience

SYSC 15A (in force 31 March 2022) requires all firms, including payment firms, to identify important business services and set impact tolerances. Firms must review important business services at least annually or on material change.

**Hard deadline:** By 31 March 2025, firms must have completed mapping and testing so they can remain within impact tolerances for each important business service, and made necessary investments to operate consistently within those tolerances.

The FCA states it is proactively monitoring compliance and will take action where deficiencies are identified. Firms must also monitor dependency on critical service providers (technology, banking services) and maintain contingency plans to move providers if necessary. *(Portfolio letter, Cross-Cutting Priority 2)*

---

### Cross-Cutting Priority 3: Regulatory Reporting

PS 21/4 (March 2021) extended the annual financial crime reporting requirement to all PIs and EMIs supervised by the FCA for AML. Details of required reports are in Chapter 13 of the Approach Document.

The FCA flags sustained non-compliance and warns that it will make more frequent use of its right to charge a £250 administrative charge per failure to meet reporting deadlines, with ongoing failure risking referral to enforcement for cancellation. *(Portfolio letter, Cross-Cutting Priority 3)*

---

## Other Topics Covered

**Changes in Control (FSMA s.178):** Acquiring or increasing control of a PI or EMI without prior FCA approval is a criminal offence. Proposed controllers must submit s.178 notices and await FCA approval before completing the transaction. Controllers disposing of control must also notify the FCA in writing beforehand. The FCA may use its powers to object to the transaction, restrict shares/voting power, or prosecute.

**ESG and Diversity and Inclusion:** Firms should have governance arrangements for material ESG risks and opportunities. The FCA flags DP21/2 (diversity and inclusion discussion paper) and states this remains a core supervisory focus.

---

## Supervisory Posture

The portfolio letter closes with an explicit statement of the FCA's approach:

> "The FCA 2022/2025 strategy has committed to act earlier and more assertively in dealing with problem firms. We will continue to intervene using our full range of supervisory tools. In cases where firms can't meet the conditions for authorisation, we will take more assertive action sooner and will remove or sanction firms who cannot or will not meet our standards."

Boards or Executive Committees are expected to consider which highlighted risks apply to their firm and explain actions taken in response if asked by the FCA.

---

## Key Points for Agents

- This letter is addressed to all PIs, EMIs, and RAISPs — it reflects the FCA's supervisory posture for the entire payments portfolio as of March 2023.
- Three outcomes: customers' money safe (safeguarding, prudential, wind-down); financial system integrity (AML/sanctions, fraud); customers' needs met (Consumer Duty).
- Three cross-cutting priorities: governance/leadership/agent oversight; operational resilience (SYSC 15A, mapping/testing by 31 March 2025); regulatory reporting (PS 21/4 annual financial crime report).
- Agent registration is the PI/EMI's responsibility; EMI distributors cannot provide payment services; communications through agents/distributors must be fair, clear, not misleading, and identify the authorised firm.
- The FCA has stated explicitly it will use cancellation powers against firms that fail to meet authorisation conditions — this is a stronger supervisory posture than typical "Dear CEO" letters.

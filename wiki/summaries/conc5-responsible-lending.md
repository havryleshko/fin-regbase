# CONC 5 — Responsible Lending

## What This Document Is

CONC 5 sets out responsible lending obligations for consumer credit. The April 2026 version contains three active sections: CONC 5.2A (creditworthiness assessment for consumer credit lenders), CONC 5.4 (conduct of business for credit brokers), and CONC 5.5A (creditworthiness assessment for P2P platform operators). CONC 5.1 and 5.3 were deleted in a prior amendment; CONC 5.2 was replaced by CONC 5.2A.

---

## CONC 5.2A — Creditworthiness Assessment (Consumer Credit Lenders)

### Application and Exclusions

CONC 5.2A applies to firms with respect to consumer credit lending **(CONC 5.2A.1R)**. It does not apply to **(CONC 5.2A.2R)**:
- Pawn agreements where total liability cannot exceed the market value of the pawned item
- Current account agreements where overrunning (unarranged overdraft) is possible
- Non-commercial agreements
- Small borrower-lender-supplier restricted-use credit agreements

### The Assessment Gate — Three Hard Triggers

A firm must undertake a creditworthiness assessment before **(CONC 5.2A.4R)**:
1. Entering into a regulated credit agreement
2. Significantly increasing the amount of credit under a regulated credit agreement
3. Significantly increasing a credit limit for running-account credit

CONC 5.2A.5R is a hard gate: the firm cannot take any of these steps unless it can demonstrate it has (a) undertaken the assessment and (b) had proper regard to its outcome in respect of affordability risk.

**Cumulative increases (CONC 5.2A.6G):** If individual increases are not each significant but aggregate since the last assessment to a significant total, a fresh assessment is required. A series of small credit limit increases may each fall below the threshold but cumulatively trigger the obligation.

### The Two-Track Assessment

The assessment must address two distinct risks **(CONC 5.2A.10R)**:

1. **Credit risk** — the risk the customer will not make repayments by their due dates
2. **Affordability risk** — the risk to the customer of not being able to make repayments in accordance with CONC 5.2A.12R

A single missed payment may be relevant to creditworthiness even if not conclusive **(CONC 5.2A.11G)**.

### The Affordability Test

The firm must consider the customer's ability to make repayments **(CONC 5.2A.12R)**:
- As they fall due over the life of the agreement (or for open-end agreements, within a reasonable period)
- Out of income, jointly held savings, or assets the customer has clearly indicated they will use
- **Without** having to borrow to meet the repayments
- **Without** failing to meet other contractual or statutory obligations
- **Without** the repayments having a significant adverse impact on the customer's financial situation

If the customer intends to repay using savings or assets, the firm must additionally assess: the purpose those assets are held for, the likelihood they will be available, and whether using them would significantly harm the customer's financial position **(CONC 5.2A.13R)**.

**Security exclusion (CONC 5.2A.14R):** When assessing affordability risk, the firm must not take into account any guarantee, indemnity, or other form of security. Security is assessed separately (see guarantor provisions below).

### Income Assessment

Unless it is obvious repayment can be met (making income determination disproportionate) or the customer is repaying from savings/assets, the firm must **(CONC 5.2A.15R)**:
- Take reasonable steps to determine or estimate the customer's current income
- Where income reduction is reasonably foreseeable during the agreement term, estimate the amount of that reduction
- Only take into account expected future income increases where reasonably believed on the basis of appropriate evidence

**Self-declaration not sufficient (CONC 5.2A.16G(3)):** "It is not generally sufficient to rely solely on a statement of current income made by the customer without independent evidence" — e.g. from a credit reference agency or third-party documentation. A firm relying on the "obvious" exception bears the burden of demonstrating it.

Income can include non-salary sources. Estimates may use ranges or minimums if assumptions are reasonable **(CONC 5.2A.16G(2), (5))**.

### Expenditure Assessment

The firm must take reasonable steps to determine or estimate non-discretionary expenditure **(CONC 5.2A.17R)**, unless:
- The obvious repayment exception applies, or
- Non-discretionary expenditure is unlikely to have a material impact on affordability risk

Non-discretionary expenditure includes priority debts, essential living expenses, mortgage/credit agreement obligations, and expenditure the customer is contractually or statutorily obliged to make **(CONC 5.2A.18G)**. It includes the non-discretionary expenditure of others whose financial obligations the customer meets.

**Statistical data (CONC 5.2A.19G):** Statistical expenditure data may be used unless the firm knows or has reasonable cause to suspect the customer's expenditure differs significantly (e.g. different household composition, dependants, or existing indebtedness).

### Scope and Proportionality of Assessment

The extent of the assessment is proportionate to individual circumstances **(CONC 5.2A.20R)**. The firm must consider: types of information to use, content and detail level, sufficiency of information in its possession, whether to obtain additional information from the customer or a credit reference agency, how to verify accuracy, degree of evaluation required, and steps to estimate income/expenditure.

The factors that determine required rigour include **(CONC 5.2A.20R(3))**:
- Type, amount, credit limit, duration, repayment frequency/amounts of the credit
- Total amount payable, total charge for credit, APR
- Whether rates/charges are fixed or variable
- Any other potential adverse consequences of failing to repay

High affordability risk indicators include **(CONC 5.2A.25G)**: high outstanding debt relative to income, or high likelihood of missed payments. A single high-risk repayment may itself indicate high affordability risk **(CONC 5.2A.25G(2))**.

Firms should also have regard to **(CONC 5.2A.22G)**:
- Financial difficulty indicators (CONC 1.3 — see [[conc-financial-difficulty-indicators]])
- Vulnerability indicators (mental health, mental capacity — CONC 2.10 and CONC 7.2)

Previous dealings may inform the assessment but time-sensitivity must be considered **(CONC 5.2A.23G)**.

### Open-End Agreements

For open-end agreements, the firm must make a reasonable assumption about likely duration, taking into account the agreement terms, pre-contractual disclosures, and the customer's known intentions **(CONC 5.2A.26R)**.

### Running-Account Credit — Mandatory Worst-Case Assumption

For running-account credit (credit cards, revolving facilities, overdrafts) **(CONC 5.2A.27R)**:
- On entering: assume the customer draws the entire credit limit at the earliest opportunity and repays by equal instalments over a reasonable period
- On significant credit limit increase: assume the customer draws the entire available balance up to the new limit and repays by equal instalments

The "reasonable period" benchmark is the typical repayment period for a fixed-sum unsecured personal loan of the same amount **(CONC 5.2A.28G)**.

### Guarantors

Where a guarantor is providing a guarantee or indemnity and the firm must assess the borrower, the firm must also assess the guarantor's ability to meet their potential obligations under the guarantee **(CONC 5.2A.31R)**. This is a separate, additional assessment — it does not reduce the borrower assessment obligation. The same income/expenditure framework applies, adapted to the guarantee context **(CONC 5.2A.32G)**.

Joint borrowers: the firm should consider whether to assess each borrower separately as well as together, having regard to the risk if one borrower becomes solely liable **(CONC 5.2A.30G)**.

### Policies, Procedures and Record-Keeping

A firm must **(CONC 5.2A.33R)**:
1. Establish, implement and maintain written creditworthiness assessment policies and procedures
2. Have them approved by the governing body or senior personnel (not applicable to sole traders)
3. Periodically assess and review their effectiveness and the firm's compliance
4. Take appropriate measures to address any deficiencies found
5. Maintain a record of every transaction where an assessment was required, sufficient to demonstrate the assessment was carried out and was reasonable
6. Establish and maintain robust governance and internal controls (not applicable to sole traders)

Record-keeping guidance: SYSC 9.1.4G and 9.1.5G **(CONC 5.2A.34G)**.

### Unfair Business Practices

- A firm must not complete application sections intended for the customer without the customer's consent or opportunity to check **(CONC 5.2A.35R)**
- A firm must not accept a credit application where it knows or has reasonable cause to suspect the customer has been untruthful about information relevant to the creditworthiness assessment **(CONC 5.2A.36R)**
- Clear inconsistency between customer-supplied income/employment data and other information held by the firm is an example of reasonable suspicion **(CONC 5.2A.37G)**

## CONC 5.4 — Conduct of Business: Credit Brokers

Credit brokers (firms arranging or introducing credit) must **(CONC 5.4.2R)**:
- Pay due regard to the customer's needs and circumstances when giving explanations, advice, or making recommendations
- Pay due regard to whether the credit product is affordable and whether there are any factors the firm knows or reasonably ought to know that would make the product unsuitable for that customer

A credit broker that represents it will search the market (or part of it) before effecting an introduction must carry out that search to the extent stated **(CONC 5.4.3R)**.

## CONC 5.5A — Creditworthiness Assessment: P2P Agreements

### Structure and Dual Obligation

CONC 5.5A applies to firms operating an electronic system in relation to lending (P2P platforms) in relation to borrowers/prospective borrowers **(CONC 5.5A.1R)**. The same carve-outs as CONC 5.2A apply (pawn, overrunning current accounts).

**Critical structural point (CONC 5.5A.3G):** CONC 5.2A applies to the *lender* under a P2P agreement; CONC 5.5A applies to the *platform operator*. Both have separate creditworthiness assessment obligations. Neither can delegate its obligation to the other, though SYSC 8 outsourcing rules apply (with the outsourcing firm remaining fully responsible).

### Assessment Framework

The assessment obligations in CONC 5.5A mirror CONC 5.2A almost exactly: three triggers, two-track assessment (credit risk + affordability risk), five-condition affordability test, income/expenditure assessment, proportionality framework, running-account assumptions, guarantor assessment, and the policies/procedures/record-keeping requirement.

**Key P2P-specific difference — unfair business practices (CONC 5.5A.37R):** In P2P, the platform operator must inform the *prospective lender* where it knows or has reasonable cause to suspect the borrower has not been truthful about information relevant to the creditworthiness assessment. This is an active disclosure obligation to the lender, unlike CONC 5.2A where the equivalent provision (CONC 5.2A.36R) simply prohibits accepting the application.

## Key Points for Agents

- Creditworthiness assessment is required at three hard gates: new agreement, significant credit increase, significant credit limit increase. Cumulative small increases can aggregate into a trigger.
- Two risks must be assessed separately: credit risk (will they pay?) and affordability risk (can they pay without harm?). Both are mandatory — passing one does not satisfy the other.
- Self-declared income is not sufficient. Independent corroboration (e.g. CRA data, payslips) is required unless it is obvious the customer can repay, and the firm bears the burden of demonstrating that exception.
- Security, guarantees, and indemnities cannot be used to offset affordability risk in the borrower assessment. Guarantor assessment is additional, not a substitute.
- Running-account credit requires a worst-case drawdown assumption: assess as if the customer draws the entire limit immediately and repays over a period equivalent to a personal loan of the same amount.
- Creditworthiness policies must be in writing, governing-body approved, periodically reviewed, and every qualifying transaction must be on record.
- In P2P: both the platform operator (CONC 5.5A) and the lender (CONC 5.2A) have separate assessment obligations. The platform must disclose borrower untruthfulness to the prospective lender.

# PRIN 3 — Rules About Application

## Overview

PRIN 3 is the governing chapter that specifies **who, what, and where** the Principles apply. It sets activity scope, territorial scope, eligible counterparty (ECP) carve-outs, and the relationship between different Principles — including the P6/P7 displacement rule and the no-private-action rule. Every compliance question about whether a Principle applies must start here.

---

## PRIN 3.1: General Application

### Who the Principles Apply To

The Principles apply to every firm that is an authorised person **(PRIN 3.1.1R)**. Application is modified for firms conducting MiFID business (where certain Principles do not apply) and Annex II benchmark administrators.

### Payments Institutions, E-Money Institutions, and RAISPs

**PRIN 3.1.1AR** (hard rule) extends application to payment service providers and electronic money issuers that are not FSMA "firms" — including payment institutions, e-money institutions, and registered account information service providers (RAISPs) — in relation to their regulated payment services or e-money activities. References to "firm" in PRIN 2 and PRIN 2A include these persons.

### PSR/EMD Limitations on PRIN 2A

Where a payment institution or e-money institution is also a "firm" subject to PRIN 2A, the Payment Services Regulations 2017 or Electronic Money Regulations 2011 may impose conflicting or overlapping obligations. The FCA's approach is that PRIN 2A applies to the extent permitted by those Regulations — if compliance with a specific PSR/EMD requirement would preclude compliance with a specific PRIN 2A rule, the PSR/EMD rule takes precedence in that context **(PRIN 3.1.8G)**. This does not create a general PSR/EMD carve-out from the Consumer Duty; it is a narrow conflict resolution rule.

## PRIN 3.2: Which Principles Apply to Which Activities

### Principles 1–11: General Application

Principles 1–11 apply in connection with regulated activities generally, subject to modifications for:
- MiFID business (certain Principles are replaced by equivalent MiFID conduct rules).
- ECP business (see section on PRIN 3.4 below).

### Principle 12 Activity Scope (PRIN 3.2.6R)

**PRIN 3.2.6R** (hard rule): Principle 12 and PRIN 2A apply to a firm in relation to regulated activities to the extent that those activities are addressed by rules in the following sourcebooks:

| Sourcebook | Scope |
|---|---|
| COBS | Investment business, designated investment business |
| ICOBS | Insurance distribution, general insurance mediation |
| MCOB | Mortgage and home finance conduct |
| BCOBS | Banking conduct (current accounts, savings accounts, e-money) |
| CMCOB | Claims management conduct |
| FPCOB | Funeral plan conduct |
| PROD | Product governance (cross-sector) |
| CONC | Consumer credit, consumer hire, P2P, debt activities |

**Implication:** A regulated activity that is not addressed by any rule in one of these sourcebooks is outside the scope of Principle 12 and PRIN 2A, even if retail customers are involved. Examples: bespoke professional services, certain FCA-regulated activities that predated the Duty's introduction, activities regulated only under SYSC or GEN with no corresponding outcome-specific sourcebook chapter.

### Principle 12 in Distribution Chains (PRIN 3.2.7R)

**PRIN 3.2.7R** (hard rule): In a distribution chain involving more than one firm, Principle 12 applies to each firm only to the extent that the firm **determines or materially influences** the outcomes that retail customers experience.

This has three practical consequences:
1. A firm with no control over product design or pricing that merely transmits a product to customers is not in breach of P12 for the features of that product — but it remains responsible for its own conduct in the distribution process.
2. A manufacturer that sets pricing and product features it knows will harm retail customers cannot escape P12 liability by pointing to the fact that a distributor completes the sale.
3. "Materially influences" is a low threshold — advising, recommending, or selecting a product from a panel is sufficient to engage P12 even without a direct customer relationship.

### The Outer Boundary — No Extension of P12 Beyond Listed Sourcebooks (PRIN 3.2.8R)

**PRIN 3.2.8R** (hard rule): Principle 12 and PRIN 2A do **not** apply to a firm in relation to an activity merely because the activity is mentioned in a rule in one of the listed sourcebooks, if the activity is not itself addressed by that rule.

This is the outer boundary rule. It prevents PRIN 3.2.6R from being read expansively to capture peripheral activities. Compliance teams should map each firm activity to a specific rule in a listed sourcebook, not to the sourcebook as a whole.

### Guidance on the P12 Scope Boundary (PRIN 3.2.9G)

The FCA provides guidance **(PRIN 3.2.9G)** that the question is whether the activity is addressed by a rule — meaning a rule that directly imposes a substantive obligation in relation to that activity. Generic application rules, interpretive provisions, and definitions do not bring an activity into scope.

### P6 and P7 Displacement (PRIN 3.2.10R)

**PRIN 3.2.10R** (hard rule): Where Principle 12 and PRIN 2A apply to a firm in connection with an activity, **Principles 6 and 7 do not apply** to that firm in connection with that activity.

Key points:
- This is a clean displacement — P6 and P7 are inapplicable, not merely superseded. A breach of what P6 or P7 would have required is not an independent breach where P12 applies.
- P6 and P7 remain fully applicable to: wholesale business; SME business outside P12 scope; and activities not in scope of PRIN 3.2.6R.
- Firms must not conflate P12/PRIN 2A compliance with P6/P7 compliance. They are different legal tests — meeting P12 does not tell you whether you would have met P6/P7, and vice versa.

## PRIN 3.3: Where — Territorial Application

### General Territorial Rule

The territorial application of each Principle is **(PRIN 3.3.1R)**:

| Principle | Territorial Reach |
|---|---|
| P1 (Integrity) | UK activities + worldwide prudential context |
| P2 (Skill, care) | UK activities + worldwide prudential context |
| P3 (Management and control) | UK activities + worldwide prudential context |
| P4 (Financial prudence) | Worldwide |
| P5 (Market conduct) | UK activities + worldwide where negative effect on UK financial system confidence |
| P6 (Customers' interests) | UK activities |
| P7 (Communications) | UK activities |
| P8 (Conflicts) | UK activities |
| P9 (Suitability) | UK activities |
| P10 (Client assets) | UK activities |
| P11 (Regulators) | Worldwide |
| P12 (Consumer Duty) | Where retail customers are **located** in the UK |

### Critical Distinction: "Located" vs "Habitually Resident"

Principle 12's territorial trigger is that retail customers are **located** in the UK — *not* "habitually resident" as used in CONC 1.2.5R **(PRIN 3.3.1R)**. These are different tests:
- "Habitually resident" (CONC) refers to the customer's long-term established home.
- "Located" (PRIN 3.3.1R) encompasses customers who are present in the UK at the time of the transaction or service — including temporary residents, visitors, and short-stay individuals.

In practice, "located" is a wider trigger than "habitually resident." A firm serving customers present in the UK — even if their permanent home is elsewhere — may be within scope of Principle 12 for those customers. Firms cannot rely on CONC's habitual residence test to determine their P12 territorial scope.

### Overseas Activities

For activities outside the UK, the FCA takes into account the standards expected in the market where the firm operates when considering regulatory action under Principles 1–3 **(PRIN 1.1.6G)**.

## PRIN 3.4: ECP Business and Special Provisions

### Eligible Counterparty Business (PRIN 3.4.1R)

Where a firm has properly classified a client as an eligible counterparty (ECP) under PRIN 1 Annex 1, the only Principle 7 obligation for non-MiFID ECP business is that the firm must communicate information in a way that is **not misleading** **(PRIN 3.4.1R)**. Principles 6, 8, 9, and 10 are effectively disapplied for ECP business.

See **[[prin1-2-introduction-principles]]** for the ECP classification requirements.

### ECP Classification Not Available for Consumer Credit, Payment Services, or E-Money

**PRIN 3.4.-1R** (hard rule): ECP classification under PRIN 1 Annex 1 is **not available** for:
- Credit-related regulated activities (i.e. CONC activities).
- Payment services regulated under the Payment Services Regulations 2017.
- E-money regulated under the Electronic Money Regulations 2011.

This means the P6/P7/P8/P9/P10 disapplication that ECP classification achieves is entirely unavailable in these sectors. Lenders, credit brokers, debt collectors, and payment service providers cannot classify any client as an ECP for PRIN purposes.

### Guarantors Treated as Retail Customers (PRIN 3.4.3AR)

**PRIN 3.4.3AR** (hard rule): A person who provides a **guarantee or indemnity** under:
- A regulated credit agreement,
- A regulated consumer hire agreement, or
- A P2P agreement,

and who is **not the borrower or hirer** under that agreement, is treated as a **retail customer** for the purposes of Principle 12 and PRIN 2A.

Key implications:
- Guarantors are within P12 scope even though they are not parties to the primary credit or hire relationship.
- A firm must deliver good outcomes for guarantors, not just borrowers and hirers.
- This extends the Consumer Duty scope in consumer credit to cover guarantor relationships — adding to the pool of persons to whom the outcome rules apply.
- There is no equivalent guarantor extension in CONC's scope rule (CONC 1.2.5R) — this is specific to PRIN/Consumer Duty.

### No Private Right of Action for PRIN Breaches (PRIN 3.4.4R)

**PRIN 3.4.4R** (hard rule):

> A contravention of a rule in PRIN does not give rise to a right of action under section 138D of FSMA.

Section 138D FSMA is the private right of action for breach of FCA rules that may give rise to a damages claim by a private person. **PRIN 3.4.4R expressly excludes PRIN from that right.** Therefore:
- A breach of any Principle — including Principle 12 — does not give a retail customer a direct claim for damages against the firm under FSMA.
- Consumers' remedies for Principle breaches are: [[disp2-fos-jurisdiction|FOS complaint]] (if within scope), FCA enforcement action (resulting in restitution or redress), or private law claims on separate causes of action (contract, tort).
- This is a deliberate design choice: Principles are high-level obligations that inform supervision and enforcement, not a basis for individual litigation.
- Note: specific PRIN 2A rules are Rules (R) — but the exclusion applies to all PRIN rules, including PRIN 2A.

## Key Points for Agents

- **PRIN 3.2.6R**: Principle 12/PRIN 2A only applies where the activity is addressed by a rule in COBS, ICOBS, MCOB, BCOBS, CMCOB, FPCOB, PROD, or CONC. If no specific rule in those sourcebooks addresses the activity, P12 is out of scope.
- **PRIN 3.2.7R**: In distribution chains, P12 applies only to the extent the firm determines or materially influences retail customer outcomes. Each firm in the chain must map its own contribution to outcomes.
- **PRIN 3.2.10R**: P6 and P7 are displaced — not merely supplemented — wherever P12 applies. They remain live for wholesale business and out-of-scope activities.
- **PRIN 3.3.1R**: P12's territorial trigger is retail customers *located* in the UK — not "habitually resident" (cf. CONC 1.2.5R). "Located" is a wider test and may catch firms serving UK visitors or temporary UK-based customers.
- **PRIN 3.4.-1R**: ECP classification that disapplies P6/P7/P8/P9/P10 is entirely unavailable for consumer credit, payment services, and e-money activities.
- **PRIN 3.4.3AR**: Guarantors and indemnifiers under regulated credit, consumer hire, or P2P agreements are retail customers for P12/PRIN 2A purposes — the Duty extends to them even though they are not borrowers or hirers.
- **PRIN 3.4.4R**: A breach of any PRIN rule — including any PRIN 2A rule — does not give retail customers a right to sue for damages under FSMA s.138D. Remedies are FOS, FCA enforcement, and separate private law claims.
- **PRIN 3.1.1AR**: Payment institutions, e-money institutions, and RAISPs are expressly caught by PRIN (including PRIN 2A) in relation to their regulated activities.

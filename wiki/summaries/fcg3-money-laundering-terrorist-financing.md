# FCA Financial Crime Guide — FCG 3: Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing

## What This Document Is

FCG 3 is Chapter 3 of the FCA's Financial Crime Guide (FCG), a sourcebook-status document providing guidance on how firms should counter financial crime risks. This chapter covers money laundering and terrorist financing (ML/TF). The FCG supplements but does not replace JMLSG guidance, which remains a key resource for operational compliance detail.

**Legal status:** Sourcebook guidance. The FCA will consider whether firms have followed FCG provisions when assessing their systems and controls against ML/TF. **(FCG 3.1.6–3.1.8)**

**Scope:** All firms subject to SYSC 3.2.6A–J or SYSC 6.3, and Annex I financial institutions and e-money institutions for which the FCA is the AML supervisory authority. Payment institutions are supervised by HMRC and are out of scope, though the guidance may still be useful to them. **(FCG 3.1.1–3.1.2)**

## Regulatory Architecture

AML/CTF obligations in the UK derive from multiple overlapping legal sources:

| Source | Primary obligations |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (MLR)** | Risk assessment (Reg 18), CDD (Regs 27–35), EDD (Regs 33–34), SARs (via nominated officer), record keeping (Reg 40), senior manager appointment (Reg 21) |
| **SYSC 3.2.6R–J** | Firms not subject to MLR but subject to FCA rules on financial crime systems and controls |
| **SYSC 6.3** | AML systems and controls for banks; MLRO appointment requirement |
| **POCA 2002 s.330–331** | Criminal obligation on employees and nominated officers to report suspicions of money laundering to NCA |
| **Terrorism Act 2000 s.21A** | Criminal obligation to report terrorist financing suspicions |

AML/CTF is a **separate regulatory regime** from the Consumer Duty. The two regimes can apply simultaneously to the same firm: a failure in AML controls may also give rise to Consumer Duty concerns (e.g. fraud victim support), but they are assessed independently.

## Governance

Senior management must take responsibility for the firm's AML measures, including knowing about ML risks the firm faces and ensuring steps are taken to mitigate them. **(FCG 3.2.1)**

**Board-level MLRO appointment:** Where appropriate to the size and nature of the business, a board member (or equivalent senior management) must be designated as the officer responsible for compliance with the MLR. **(MLR Reg 21(1)(a); FCG 3.2.1)**

**Notification:** Firms must notify the FCA of this individual's identity within 14 days of appointment. **(MLR Reg 21(4))**

**Named good practice:** Reward structures that account for AML compliance failings; senior management involvement in approving high-risk customer relationships, including PEPs; documentation provided to senior management gives an accurate picture of ML risk. **(FCG 3.2.1)**

**Named poor practice:** AML treated as a legal/regulatory necessity rather than a genuine concern; senior management attaches greater importance to reputational risk than corruption risk; board never considers MLRO reports. **(FCG 3.2.1)**

## The MLRO

Firms subject to SYSC 3.2.6A–J or SYSC 6.3 must appoint an individual as Money Laundering Reporting Officer. The MLRO is responsible for oversight of the firm's AML compliance and should act as the focal point for all AML activity. **(FCG 3.2.2)**

**Requirements:** The MLRO must be independent, knowledgeable, robust and well-resourced; have a direct reporting line to executive management or the board; be able to pose effective challenge to the business; and be able to retrieve information about the firm's highest-risk customers on request without delay. **(FCG 3.2.2)**

**Named failure modes:**
- MLRO lacks credibility or authority due to inexperience or seniority
- MLRO does not understand the policies they are supposed to oversee or the rationale behind them
- MLRO unable to retrieve high-risk customer information promptly
- MLRO has no role in monitoring high-risk relationships

See [[aml-ctf-framework]] for full MLRO detail.

## Risk Assessment

Firms must undertake a documented risk assessment identifying their exposure to ML, TF, and proliferation financing (PF) risk. The assessment must be proportionate to the nature, scale and complexity of the firm's activities and must be regularly reviewed. **(MLR Reg 18; FCG 3.2.3)**

Risk factors to assess include: customer types and beneficial ownership structures; products and services; geographic exposure; delivery channels; sector risk; and involvement in public contracts. **(FCG 3.2.3)**

**Key failure mode:** Risk classification systems designed so it is almost impossible for a relationship to be classified as high-risk; relationship managers able to override customer risk scores without sufficient justification; risk assessments unduly influenced by the profitability of relationships. **(FCG 3.2.3)**

The **Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023** now permits firms to share information with each other for the purpose of preventing, detecting and investigating economic crime. Firms should use this to inform risk-based decisions. **(FCG 3.2.3)**

## Customer Due Diligence (CDD)

Firms must identify customers and, where applicable, their beneficial owners, and verify both identities. Firms must also understand the purpose and intended nature of the business relationship. **(FCG 3.2.4)**

**Beneficial ownership threshold:** For corporate customers, firms must identify individuals who ultimately control more than **25% of shares or voting rights**, exercise control over management, or control the body corporate. **(FCG 3.2.4)**

Where a beneficial owner cannot be identified, firms must keep written records of all steps taken and take reasonable measures to verify the identity of the senior person responsible for managing the body corporate. **(FCG 3.2.4)**

Where CDD cannot be completed, the firm **must not enter into or continue** the business relationship. **(FCG 3.2.4)**

See [[customer-due-diligence]] for the full CDD framework including EDD triggers, source of wealth/funds, and ongoing monitoring.

## Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) — Mandatory Triggers

The following situations legally require EDD and, in some cases, **senior management approval before establishing the relationship**:

- **PEPs** and their family members and known close associates — mandatory EDD and senior management approval **(MLR Reg 35)**
- **High-risk third countries** (FATF Call for Action and Increased Monitoring lists) — mandatory EDD including source of funds/wealth and senior management approval **(MLR Reg 33)**
- **Correspondent banking relationships** from third countries — EDD plus understanding of the correspondent's AML regime quality **(MLR Reg 34)**
- **Complex, unusually large, or unusual pattern transactions** without apparent economic purpose — EDD to examine background and purpose **(MLR Reg 33)**

See [[customer-due-diligence]] for full EDD detail.

## Ongoing Monitoring and Transaction Monitoring

Firms must conduct ongoing monitoring of business relationships on a risk-sensitive basis — scrutinising transactions for consistency with the firm's knowledge of the customer and keeping CDD information current. **(FCG 3.2.5)**

Transaction monitoring systems (automated or manual) must be calibrated to the firm's risk profile and relevant typologies, tested for effectiveness, and regularly reviewed. Senior management must have realistic expectations of what automated monitoring can achieve; manual scrutiny must be adequately resourced. **(FCG 3.2.5A)**

**HSBC enforcement case (FCG 3.2.5B):** The FCA imposed a £63,946,800 penalty on HSBC for transaction monitoring failures over an extended period. The bank failed to: consider whether monitoring scenarios covered relevant risks; carry out timely risk assessments for new scenarios; appropriately test and update monitoring parameters; and check the accuracy of data fed into monitoring systems — resulting in millions of transactions worth billions of pounds monitored incorrectly or not at all. **(FCG 3.2.5B)**

## Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs)

The nominated officer has a **legal obligation** to report knowledge or suspicion of money laundering to the National Crime Agency (NCA) through a SAR. **(POCA s.330–331; FCG 3.2.10)**

**Named poor practices:**
- Passing all internal reports to NCA without genuine assessment ("defensive SARs") — of little value to law enforcement
- Nominated officer dismissing concerns without documenting reasons
- Nominated officer turning a blind eye where a SAR might harm the business — **explicitly flagged as a potential criminal offence** **(FCG 3.2.10)**

**Good practice:** SARs set out a clear narrative with actionable detail (names, addresses, passport numbers, phone numbers, email addresses); reasons for suspicion explained in plain English; previous related SARs cross-referenced. **(FCG 3.2.10)**

**Alpari case study:** Alpari (UK) Ltd fined £140,000 for poor AML controls (inadequate CDD at account opening; failure to monitor accounts). The former MLRO was personally fined £14,000 for failing to fulfil duties. **(FCG 3.2.14)**

## Record Keeping

CDD documents and information must be retained for **five years** after the business relationship ends or five years after an occasional transaction. Records relating to transactions within a business relationship need not be kept beyond **ten years**. Data must be deleted after the applicable retention period. **(MLR Reg 40; FCG 3.2.11)**

Firms must be able to retrieve records promptly in response to a Production Order. **(FCG 3.2.11)**

## Counter-Terrorism Financing (CTF)

Many AML controls overlap with CTF obligations — risk assessment, CDD, transaction monitoring, SAR reporting, and law enforcement liaison all apply to terrorist financing. Financial crime training must explicitly address terrorist financing, not only money laundering. **(FCG 3.2.12)**

Cross-border firms must assess terrorism-related risks in every country where they have a presence or do business. **(FCG 3.2.12)**

## Wire Transfers and the Travel Rule

Banks must collect and attach payer and payee information to wire transfer payment messages, check that inbound payments contain complete information, and chase missing data. **(FCG 3.2.13)**

From **1 September 2023**, the same obligations apply to cryptoasset transfers undertaken by cryptoasset businesses registered with the FCA — the "Travel Rule." **(FCG 3.2.13)**

**Wire transfer enforcement cases:**
- **Lloyds TSB:** Agreed to pay **US$350m** to US authorities after offices in the UK and Dubai deliberately removed customer names and addresses from US wire transfers to evade sanctions filters. **(FCG 3.2.15)**
- **Barclays Bank PLC:** Agreed to pay **US$298m** to US authorities for implementing practices to evade US sanctions — stripping payer/payee information from payment messages — over a decade-long period. **(FCG 3.2.15)**

## Relationship to Consumer Duty

AML/CTF and Consumer Duty are distinct regulatory regimes that operate in parallel:

- Consumer Duty pages treat **fraud victim support** as a consumer support obligation and **scam prevention** as a foreseeable harm issue (FCA implementation review, February 2024).
- FCG 3 treats the same underlying situations as AML/CTF compliance obligations under the Money Laundering Regulations and SYSC.
- Both frameworks can be engaged simultaneously. A fraud incident may trigger both Consumer Duty (post-incident victim support) and AML obligations (SAR reporting, transaction monitoring failures). These are **not contradictory** — they impose different but overlapping obligations on the same firm.

## Key Points for Agents

- AML/CTF is a separate regulatory regime from Consumer Duty. Both can apply simultaneously. Financial crime prevention is not subsumed by Consumer Duty.
- The ML/TF regulatory architecture has three layers: primary legislation (POCA, Terrorism Act), the Money Laundering Regulations, and FCA rules (SYSC) + FCG guidance.
- A firm subject to the MLR must appoint a senior-management-level board officer for MLR compliance and notify the FCA within 14 days of appointment.
- CDD must identify and verify both the customer and the beneficial owner (25% control threshold for corporates). Where CDD cannot be completed, the relationship must not proceed.
- Three mandatory EDD triggers requiring senior management approval: PEPs (and family/associates), high-risk third countries (FATF lists), and correspondent banking relationships from third countries.
- The nominated officer has a personal legal obligation to report ML suspicions to NCA — turning a blind eye to protect the business is a potential criminal offence.
- CDD records must be retained for five years post-relationship end; deleted after the applicable retention period.
- The Travel Rule applies to cryptoasset transfers from 1 September 2023.

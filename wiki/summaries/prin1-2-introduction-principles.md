# PRIN 1–2 — Introduction and the Principles

## PRIN 1: Application and Purpose

### Application

The Principles (see PRIN 2) apply in whole or in part to every firm **(PRIN 1.1.1G)**. The application of the Principles is modified for firms conducting MiFID business and Annex II benchmark administrators; PRIN 3 specifies to whom, to what, and where the Principles apply. The Principles also apply to certain payment service providers and electronic money issuers that are not "firms" — PRIN 3.1.1AR sets out the application to those persons; references to a "firm" in PRIN 2 include such persons **(PRIN 1.1.1AG)**.

### Purpose

The Principles are a general statement of the fundamental obligations of firms and other persons to whom they apply under the regulatory system. They derive their authority from the FCA's rule-making powers under the Act, including as applied by the Payment Services Regulations and the Electronic Money Regulations, and reflect the statutory objectives **(PRIN 1.1.2G)**.

### Link to Fit and Proper Standard

The Principles express the main dimensions of the "fit and proper" standard in threshold condition 5 (Suitability), although they do not derive their authority from that standard. Being ready, willing, and organised to abide by the Principles is a critical factor in applications for Part 4A permission. Breaching the Principles may call into question whether a firm with Part 4A permission is still fit and proper **(PRIN 1.1.4G)**. For PSPs and EMIs authorised under the Payment Services Regulations or Electronic Money Regulations, the relevant "fit and proper standards" are those set in those Regulations **(PRIN 1.1.4AG)**.

### Group Activities

Principles 3 (Management and control), 4 (Financial prudence), and (so far as it relates to disclosing to the FCA) 11 (Relations with regulators) take into account the activities of members of a firm's group **(PRIN 1.1.5G)**. Compliance by another person subject to the Principles with Principles 3, 4, and 11 can also be affected by the activities of other group members. A subsidiary's inadequate risk management systems are therefore relevant in assessing the parent's compliance with Principle 3.

### Standards in Markets Outside the United Kingdom

Principles 1, 2, and 3 apply worldwide in a *prudential context*. Principle 5 (Market conduct) applies worldwide where activities might have a negative effect on confidence in the UK financial system. In considering regulatory action for overseas activities, the FCA takes into account the standards expected in the market in which the firm is operating. Principle 11 applies to worldwide activities; for overseas regulator cooperation, the FCA has regard to the duties owed by the firm to that regulator **(PRIN 1.1.6G)**. Full territorial detail is in PRIN 3.3.1R.

### Consequences of Breaching the Principles

Breaching a Principle makes a firm liable to **disciplinary sanctions**. The FCA must show the firm was **at fault** **(PRIN 1.1.7G)**. What constitutes fault varies by Principle:
- **Principle 1** (Integrity): FCA must demonstrate a lack of integrity in the conduct of the firm's business.
- **Principle 2** (Skill, care and diligence): breach if the firm would have failed to act with due skill, care and diligence.
- **Principle 3** (Management and control): breach arises not simply because the firm failed to control or prevent unforeseeable risks, but if it failed to take reasonable care to organise and control its affairs responsibly and effectively.

The Principles are also relevant to the FCA's powers to vary Part 4A permission, authorisation, or registration; powers of investigation and intervention; and the basis for injunction or restitution orders. **The Principles do not give rise to actions for damages by a private person (PRIN 3.4.4R)** **(PRIN 1.1.8G)**.

### Responsibilities of Providers and Distributors

RPPD contains guidance on the responsibilities of providers and distributors for the fair treatment of customers under the Principles **(PRIN 1.1.10G)**.

## PRIN 1.2: Clients and the Principles

### Characteristics of the Client

Principles 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, and 12 impose requirements on firms expressly in relation to their clients or customers. The applicable standard depends on the characteristics of the client or customer — what is "due regard" (Principles 6 and 7), "fairly" (Principles 6 and 8), "clear, fair and not misleading" (Principle 7), "reasonable care" (Principle 9), "adequate" (Principle 10), or "good outcomes" (Principle 12) varies accordingly **(PRIN 1.2.1G)**.

### Acting Through an Agent

If a person is acting through an agent (for example, a policyholder through a general insurance broker acting as agent for a disclosed policyholder), the policyholder is the firm's client — not the agent. The ability to treat the agent as the client (COBS 2.4.3R) is not available in these circumstances **(PRIN 1.2.6G)**.

## PRIN 1 Annex 1: Eligible Counterparty Classification

For non-designated investment business, a firm may categorise certain clients as **eligible counterparties (ECPs)** for PRIN purposes **(PRIN 1 Annex 1 R)**. Types eligible to be treated as ECPs include:
1. Properly constituted governments (including quasi-governmental bodies and agencies) of any country
2. Central banks and national monetary authorities
3. Supranationals whose members are countries or central banks
4. State investment bodies or bodies managing public debt at national level
5. Another firm or overseas financial services institution
6. Associates of a firm or overseas financial services institution (with consent)
7. A client classified as an ECP under point 1.2 (large corporates — see below)
8. Recognised investment exchanges, EU regulated markets, clearing houses

**Large corporate classification (PRIN 1 Annex 1 — 1.2):** A firm may classify a client (other than another firm, a regulated collective investment scheme, or overseas financial services institution) as an ECP if:
- The client is (a) a body corporate with called-up share capital ≥ £10m; or (b) a body corporate meeting two of: balance sheet ≥ €12.5m, net turnover ≥ €25m, employees ≥ 250; or (d) a partnership or unincorporated association with net assets ≥ £10m; or (e/f) qualifying pension trustees; **and**
- The firm has: (a) advised the client in writing of the ECP classification; (b) given a written warning that protections will be lost; (c) for body corporates — taken reasonable steps to deliver to an authorised decision-maker and not received an objection; or for other entity types — also obtained written consent.

**Effect:** See PRIN 3.4.1R — the only Principle 7 obligation for ECP business (non-MiFID) is that the firm must communicate information in a way that is not misleading. Principles 6, 8, 9, and 10 are effectively disapplied for ECP business. Note: ECP classification is not available for credit-related activities, payment services, or e-money — see PRIN 3.4.-1R.

## PRIN 2: The 12 Principles

All 12 Principles are **hard Rules (R)** — not guidance **(PRIN 2.1.1R)**:

| No. | Name | Obligation |
|-----|------|------------|
| 1 | Integrity | A firm must conduct its business with integrity. |
| 2 | Skill, care and diligence | A firm must conduct its business with due skill, care and diligence. |
| 3 | Management and control | A firm must take reasonable care to organise and control its affairs responsibly and effectively, with adequate risk management systems. |
| 4 | Financial prudence | A firm must maintain adequate financial resources. |
| 5 | Market conduct | A firm must observe proper standards of market conduct. |
| 6 | Customers' interests | A firm must pay due regard to the interests of its customers and treat them fairly. |
| 7 | Communications with clients | A firm must pay due regard to the information needs of its clients, and communicate information to them in a way which is clear, fair and not misleading. |
| 8 | Conflicts of interest | A firm must manage conflicts of interest fairly, both between itself and its customers and between a customer and another client. |
| 9 | Customers: relationships of trust | A firm must take reasonable care to ensure the suitability of its advice and discretionary decisions for any customer who is entitled to rely upon its judgment. |
| 10 | Clients' assets | A firm must arrange adequate protection for clients' assets when it is responsible for them. |
| 11 | Relations with regulators | A firm must deal with its regulators in an open and cooperative way, and must disclose to the FCA appropriately anything relating to the firm of which that regulator would reasonably expect notice. |
| 12 | Consumer Duty | A firm must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers. |

## Key Points for Agents

- All 12 Principles are hard Rules — not guidance. A Principle breach gives rise to disciplinary sanctions but not a private right of action for damages by consumers (PRIN 3.4.4R, PRIN 1.1.8G).
- Breaching a Principle may call into question whether the firm is fit and proper for its Part 4A permission (PRIN 1.1.4G) — the enforcement stakes extend beyond fines to authorisation itself.
- Principles 3, 4, and 11 take into account group activities: a parent cannot disregard a subsidiary's deficient risk management when assessing its own Principle 3 compliance (PRIN 1.1.5G).
- Principles 4 and 11 apply worldwide. Principles 1, 2, 3 apply worldwide in a prudential context (PRIN 1.1.6G; PRIN 3.3.1R). There is no territorial safe harbour for overseas activities.
- ECP classification (PRIN 1 Annex 1) reduces Principle 7 to a non-misleading-only obligation and effectively disapplies Principles 6, 8, 9, and 10 — but ECP classification is NOT available for consumer credit, payment services, or e-money activities (PRIN 3.4.-1R).

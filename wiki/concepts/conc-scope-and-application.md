# CONC — Scope and Application

## What CONC Is

The Consumer Credit sourcebook (CONC) is the FCA's specialist sourcebook for credit-related regulated activities. It sets detailed obligations that build on top of high-level requirements in PRIN, GEN, SYSC and the Consumer Credit Act 1974 **(CONC 1.1.2G)**. Other sourcebooks continue to apply alongside CONC: complaints in DISP, supervision and reporting in SUP, client money in CASS.

CONC is not a standalone regime. Firms in scope must comply with CONC and with all applicable general Handbook requirements simultaneously.

## Activities in Scope

CONC applies to ten credit-related regulated activities **(CONC 1.2.4G)**:

| Activity | Notes |
|---|---|
| Consumer credit lending | Core lending — personal loans, credit cards, overdrafts, BNPL |
| Credit broking | Introducing customers to lenders; arranging credit |
| Debt counselling | Advising on repayment of debts |
| Debt adjusting | Negotiating terms with creditors on a customer's behalf |
| Debt administration | Managing credit agreements on behalf of a lender |
| Debt collecting | Collecting debts under a credit agreement |
| Providing credit information services | Credit repair; credit file access services |
| Providing credit references | Operating a credit reference agency |
| Operating an electronic system in relation to lending | P2P platforms — but only insofar as relating to the borrower/prospective borrower under a P2P agreement |
| Consumer hiring | Hire agreements that are not hire-purchase |

**Implication:** A fintech that does not lend but brokers credit, manages debt collection, or provides credit information services is caught by CONC. The scope is wider than most firms assume.

## Jurisdictional Reach

CONC (except CONC 3 on financial promotions) applies to activities carried on **(CONC 1.2.5R)**:

1. With a customer whose **habitual residence is in the UK** from an establishment in the UK, or
2. With a customer whose **habitual residence is in the UK** from an establishment **outside the UK**.

The trigger is the customer's habitual residence, not the firm's location. A firm incorporated and operating from outside the UK that serves UK-resident consumers must comply with CONC. This is a hard rule (R), not guidance.

## MCOB Boundary — What CONC Does Not Cover

CONC does **not** apply to credit agreements secured on land **(CONC 1.2.7G)**. Those are governed by MCOB. The narrow exceptions where CONC may still apply:

- Agreements where the borrower is a "relevant recipient of credit" (RAO article 60L) but is not one or more individuals or trustees — e.g. a partnership where one (not all) partners is a body corporate **(CONC 1.2.7G(3)(a))**
- MCD article 3(1)(b) credit agreements secured on land, where less than 40% is used as or in connection with a dwelling **(CONC 1.2.7G(3)(b))**

**Credit broking carve-out:** Broking in relation to secured agreements may still constitute credit broking under RAO article 36A, whether the underlying agreement is regulated or exempt. For example, arranging a business loan over £25,000 secured on the borrower's home — exempt from regulation as a credit agreement — is still potentially regulated credit broking **(CONC 1.2.7G(4))**.

P2P platform operators are excluded from CONC where MCOB applies to home financing they facilitate **(CONC 1.2.12R)**.

## Firm Obligations: Employees, Agents and Third Parties

CONC 1.2.2R is a hard rule imposing two obligations on every firm:

1. Ensure that **employees and agents** comply with CONC.
2. Take **reasonable steps** to ensure that **other persons acting on the firm's behalf** comply with CONC.

### Appointed Representatives

CONC does not bind appointed representatives directly. However, under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 s.39(3)–(4) **(CONC 1.2.3G)**:

- The principal firm is **always responsible** for the acts and omissions of its ARs in the business the firm has accepted responsibility for.
- Anything done or omitted by an AR acting as such is **treated in law as if done or omitted by the firm**.

There is no partial responsibility. An AR's CONC breach is the principal firm's CONC breach.

## Consumer Duty Applies to CONC Firms

CONC 1.1.4G explicitly lists all nine Principles for Businesses as applying to credit-related regulated activities, including **Principle 12** (act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers) and **PRIN 2A** (Consumer Duty). This is the CONC-internal confirmation of Consumer Duty applicability.

The consumer-duty-overview sector scope table (citing FG22/5 §2.3) maps Consumer Duty to CONC: "Consumer credit | CONC | All regulated credit-related activities." The two sources are consistent and mutually reinforcing. See [[consumer-duty-overview]].

## MCD Article 3(1)(b) Firms — Reduced Application

Creditors and credit intermediaries under MCD article 3(1)(b) — primarily non-standard secured lending — have limited CONC exposure. Full details at CONC 1.2.8R. High-net-worth borrowers may waive regulated agreement protections subject to conditions at CONC 1.2.10R (credit exceeds £60,260; borrower declaration; income/asset statement provided to lender before entry).

## Key Points for Agents

- CONC applies to 10 credit-related activity types. A firm that does not lend but brokers, collects, administers or advises on credit is likely in scope.
- The jurisdictional trigger is the **customer's habitual residence in the UK** — not where the firm is based. Extraterritorial reach is a hard rule (CONC 1.2.5R).
- Credit secured on land goes to MCOB, not CONC. Broking of such agreements may still be caught as credit broking under RAO article 36A.
- An AR's CONC breach is the principal firm's breach by statute (FSMA s.39(4)). CONC 1.2.2R requires firms to ensure compliance by employees, agents and third parties.
- Consumer Duty (Principle 12 / PRIN 2A) explicitly applies to CONC firms (CONC 1.1.4G).

# FCA Consumer Duty — Consumer Understanding Outcome

## The Obligation

> Firms' communications must support and enable consumers to make **informed decisions** about financial products and services. Consumers must receive the information they need, **at the right time**, presented in a way they are **likely to understand**.
> **(PS22/9 §8.1)**

This outcome is broader than existing disclosure requirements and applies to **all communications with consumers**: verbal (including adviser conversations), online, in letters, in product terms and conditions. **(PS22/9 §8.7)**

## What Has Changed from Principle 7

Under Principle 7, the standard was that communications must be "clear, fair and not misleading." The Consumer Understanding outcome goes further:

- The test is not whether a communication *is* clear, but whether it **supports effective decision-making in practice**. **(PS22/9 §8.11)**
- Firms must **test** their communications — not just design them for clarity.
- The goal is evidenced understanding, not presumed understanding.

## The "Customers Intended to Receive" Standard

The FCA dropped the concept of the "average customer." Instead, firms must ensure communications are likely to be understood by **the customers intended to receive them**. **(PS22/9 §8.10)**

In practice, this means:
- For **mass-market products**: communicate as simply as possible given population-level literacy and numeracy statistics (1 in 7 adults have literacy at or below that of a 9–11 year old; 34% have poor numeracy for financial concepts). **(PS22/9 §8.10)**
- For **complex products with sophisticated target markets**: a higher register may be appropriate.
- In **one-to-one interactions**: firms must adapt communications where it becomes apparent an individual customer needs particular information or has a characteristic of vulnerability. **(PS22/9 §8.10)**

This is not a requirement to tailor every communication to each individual — it requires firms to account for the **range of needs in their target market**. **(PS22/9 §8.10)**

## Testing

Testing is a core obligation under this outcome. **(PS22/9 §8.11)**

Firms should develop an approach to testing that provides assurance customers can identify and understand:
- Actions required from them and consequences of inaction
- Key features, benefits, costs and risks of a product or service where customers must make a choice
- How to access additional information or support

**Key FCA self-test question:** Are the same standards and testing capabilities being applied to consumer understanding as to communications designed to maximise sales? **(PS22/9 §8.11)**

Not all communications need to be tested before sending — the rule was amended to include "where appropriate." **(PS22/9 §8.12)** But firms must be able to demonstrate a coherent testing approach.

## Relationship to Existing Disclosure Requirements

Firms must continue to comply with existing legislative and regulatory disclosure requirements. Where those requirements produce complex mandatory disclosures, firms should consider **layered approaches** — simple cover letters or summaries signposting to detailed documents — to support understanding. **(PS22/9 §8.7)**

Where firms have discretion within a disclosure framework, they must exercise it in a way likely to be understood by the target audience. **(PS22/9 §8.7)**

The FCA acknowledged that some EU-legacy disclosure requirements can be prescriptive and may limit flexibility. Where firms have evidence that mandatory disclosures are not effective (through testing), they should share this with the FCA. **(PS22/9 §8.7)**

## The Advice Boundary

This outcome does not alter the **advice/guidance boundary**. Non-advisory firms must equip customers with information to make effective decisions — but must do so within the scope of what they are authorised to do. **(PS22/9 §8.8)**

## Key Points for Agents

- The operative question is whether communications support effective decision-making in practice — not whether they are technically clear or non-misleading.
- The "average customer" standard has been replaced by "customers intended to receive the communication" — firms must account for the actual range of needs in their target market.
- Testing communications is a formal obligation; firms must be able to demonstrate their testing approach delivers good outcomes.
- This outcome applies to all communications: verbal, written, online, in product terms — not only regulated disclosures.
- Existing mandatory disclosure requirements must still be met; this outcome applies on top of and around them.

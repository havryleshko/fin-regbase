# Advice Suitability — Personal Recommendations (COBS 9)

> **Source note:** The empirical findings on this page derive primarily from the FCA's May 2017 Assessing Suitability Review, which assessed advice delivered in 2015 (pre-MiFID II implementation). The COBS 9 suitability obligation itself remains in force and has been updated by MiFID II transposition. Where specific rule references are given, verify against the current handbook.

---

## The Suitability Obligation

Under **COBS 9**, a firm that makes a personal recommendation must take reasonable steps to ensure that the recommendation is **suitable** for the client. Suitability is assessed against three dimensions:

1. **Knowledge and experience** — the client has sufficient knowledge and experience in the relevant investment field to understand the risks.
2. **Financial situation** — the client is financially able to bear any related investment risks consistent with their investment objectives.
3. **Investment objectives** — the recommendation meets the client's investment objectives, including their risk tolerance.

A firm that cannot demonstrate that a recommendation was suitable for these three dimensions has prima facie breached COBS 9.2. Where there is insufficient information on file to evaluate suitability — even if the advice may have been suitable — this is itself a breach: the firm has not taken reasonable steps to ensure suitability. **(COBS 9.2)**

## Two Named Root Causes of Unsuitable Advice

The FCA's 2017 sector review identified two recurring root causes across unsuitable and unclear-rated advice files. These patterns apply across investment, pension accumulation, and retirement income advice.

### 1. Risk Profiling Failures

Risk profiling tools are widely used to assess client risk tolerance. Failures occur in two ways:

**Tool limitation failures:** Firms use a risk profiling tool without considering or mitigating its inherent limitations. Most tools capture expressed risk tolerance (what the client says they are comfortable with) but do not independently assess risk capacity (what the client can financially afford to lose). Treating tool outputs as definitive — rather than as one input into a holistic assessment — is a named failure mode.

**Solution mismatch:** The recommended product or portfolio does not match the risk rating produced by the profiling process. This can occur where a firm's centralised investment proposition (CIP) does not map cleanly to the profiling outputs, or where advisers override profiling outputs without adequate justification.

The FCA has published finalised guidance on risk profiling (FSA FG11/05) specifically addressing these issues. Firms should document both the tool's limitations and how those limitations were mitigated in the specific case.

### 2. Replacement Business Failures

Replacement business arises where a firm recommends that a client:
- Transfer out of a pension scheme (particularly defined benefit or schemes with guaranteed annuity rates), or
- Surrender an existing policy (particularly one with guaranteed benefits), in favour of a new product.

Failures occur where:
- **Valuable guarantees are given up without adequate justification.** Guaranteed annuity rates, defined benefit pension entitlements, and similar protected benefits have significant financial value that must be quantified and weighed against the proposed alternative. The burden of justification is on the firm.
- **Costs outweigh benefits.** Where the additional costs of the recommended solution (charges, tax consequences, loss of guarantees) appear to outweigh the benefits, the advice is unsuitable unless the firm can demonstrate specific client circumstances that justify the recommendation.

Replacement business consistently generates the highest regulatory risk in the advisory sector. The FCA has published finalised guidance on centralised investment propositions and replacement business (FG12/16).

## Disclosure Framework for Advice Firms

Advice firms face three distinct disclosure obligations. These are assessed separately. A firm can comply with its suitability report obligations while simultaneously failing on initial charges disclosure.

> **Caveat:** This framework reflects COBS rules as assessed against 2015 advice, pre-MiFID II. Cross-reference current COBS before applying. MiFID II and subsequent FCA rules modified these requirements.

### Component 1: Initial Disclosure of Charges and Services

**Rules:** COBS 6.1A (adviser charging and remuneration) and COBS 6.2A (describing advice services)

Initial disclosure encompasses two stages:
- **Generic disclosure:** A document provided to all clients describing the firm's charging structure and the nature of its advice service (independent or restricted). This is the most problematic disclosure component — 34% of generic disclosures were rated unacceptable in the FCA's 2017 review.
- **Customer-specific disclosure:** A personalised disclosure of the charges applicable to the specific client and recommendation. This performed better (14.6% unacceptable).

**Two specific failure modes identified by the FCA (COBS 6.1A.20G):**

1. **Hourly rates without time estimates.** Where a firm charges by the hour, it must provide an approximation of how long each service is likely to take. A bare hourly rate without a time estimate fails this obligation — clients cannot evaluate the expected total cost. **(COBS 6.1A.20G(2))**

2. **Uninformatively wide charging ranges.** Where a firm expresses its charges as a percentage range (e.g. 0.5%–3.0%), the range must be narrow enough to be meaningful. Ranges so wide that they communicate nothing about expected cost fail the obligation. **(COBS 6.1A.20G(1))**

The FCA noted both failures were persistent — previously identified in a 2014 RDR thematic review — and remained disappointed that they continued after prior regulatory communication.

### Component 2: Product Disclosure

**Rule:** COBS 14 (providing product information to clients)

Firms must provide clients with appropriate information about the recommended product in good time before the client is bound by the agreement. In the 2017 review, product disclosure was broadly compliant (3.2% unacceptable) — the least problematic of the three components.

### Component 3: The Suitability Report

**Rule:** COBS 9.4 (suitability reports)

Where a firm makes a personal recommendation, it must provide the client with a suitability report explaining why the recommendation is suitable for the client. The report must cover the client's demands and needs, any disadvantages, and why the recommendation is considered suitable.

In the 2017 review, suitability reports were broadly compliant (5.6% unacceptable) — second-best component. The FCA noted that **some suitability reports were too long and/or complex**, reducing their utility to consumers. Length and complexity are a disclosure quality concern even when the substantive content is present.

## Sector Segmentation — Supervisory Risk Indicators

The FCA identified statistically significant differences in suitability and disclosure quality across firm segments. These inform supervisory risk prioritisation.

| Dimension | Higher risk | Lower risk |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| Firm authorisation | Directly authorised (DA) | Network firms |
| Advice scope | Independent | Restricted |
| Firm size | 3–24 advisers | 25+ advisers |
| Advice type | Pension accumulation / retirement income | Investment advice |

Mid-size (3–24 adviser) DA independent firms consistently showed the weakest performance on both suitability and disclosure. The FCA attributed this partly to fewer compliance resources. Larger firms (25+ advisers) and network-supported firms outperformed on both dimensions — consistent with the compliance infrastructure available to them.

## Key Points for Agents

- COBS 9 suitability is assessed across three dimensions: knowledge and experience, financial situation (risk capacity), and investment objectives (risk tolerance). All three must be addressed on file. Insufficient file documentation is itself a breach even if advice may have been suitable.
- **Two root causes of unsuitable advice:** risk profiling failures (tool limitations not mitigated; mismatch between profiling output and recommended solution) and replacement business failures (valuable guarantees surrendered without adequate justification; costs outweighing benefits).
- **Initial charges disclosure (COBS 6.1A) is the highest-risk disclosure component** — 34% of generic disclosures were unacceptable in the 2017 review. The two specific failures: hourly rates without time estimates (COBS 6.1A.20G(2)); ranges too wide to be informative (COBS 6.1A.20G(1)).
- Suitability reports (COBS 9.4) and product disclosure (COBS 14) were broadly compliant in the 2017 review. Non-compliance concentrated in initial disclosure of firm charges and services.
- Risk profiling tools capture tolerance, not capacity. Treating a tool output as a complete suitability assessment — without separately assessing the client's ability to bear losses — is a named FCA failure mode.
- Pension accumulation and retirement income advice carry higher suitability risk than investment advice; replacement business in pension contexts carries the highest regulatory risk of any advisory transaction type.
- Pre-MiFID II baseline: this page reflects the regulatory landscape as assessed in 2015. Current COBS rules must be verified before applying specific rule references.

# FCA Consumer Duty — Vulnerable Customers

## Overview

Vulnerability is not a standalone outcome under the Consumer Duty — it is a **lens that applies across all four outcomes and the cross-cutting rules**. The Duty requires firms to consider customers with characteristics of vulnerability throughout the customer journey, at both target market and individual level. **(PS22/9 §10.1)**

The FCA's existing guidance on the fair treatment of vulnerable consumers remains relevant and is preserved, despite the disapplication of Principle 6. It is referenced in the Consumer Duty guidance (FG22/5). **(PS22/9 §10.8)**

## Two Relevant Categories

Under the Duty, firms must consider two overlapping but distinct categories: **(PS22/9 §10.15)**

### 1. Customers with Characteristics of Vulnerability

Customers who, due to their personal circumstances, are especially susceptible to harm. Drivers of vulnerability include (but are not limited to):
- Low financial capability or literacy
- Erratic or inadequate income
- Over-indebtedness or low savings
- Bereavement, serious illness, or mental health conditions
- Cognitive impairment, visual or hearing impairment

Vulnerability is **dynamic and temporary** — anyone can become vulnerable at any point, including tech-savvy customers who lose internet access, or customers experiencing bereavement. Firms must account for this throughout the product lifecycle. **(PS22/9 §9.7)**

### 2. Customers with Protected Characteristics (Equality Act 2010)

Firms have pre-existing legal obligations under the Equality Act 2010 (and equivalent legislation in Northern Ireland) not to discriminate and to make reasonable adjustments for disabled customers. **(PS22/9 §10.15)**

The Consumer Duty reinforces these obligations — it does not replace them. Where data shows that customers sharing a protected characteristic are disproportionately experiencing harm, firms must investigate and satisfy themselves their conduct complies with both FCA regulation and the Equality Act. **(PS22/9 §10.15)**

## How Vulnerability Applies Across Each Outcome

### Products and Services

Manufacturers must consider the needs, characteristics and objectives of customers in their target market who have characteristics of vulnerability — including diverse needs such as visual impairment, bereavement or low financial capability — when designing and reviewing products. Inclusive design methodology is encouraged but not required. **(PS22/9 §6.9, §10.10)**

### Price and Value

Where a product is identified as offering poor value, the impact on vulnerable customers is a key factor in deciding what remedial action to take. Firms must not withdraw products in ways that disproportionately harm vulnerable customers without engaging the FCA. **(PS22/9 §10.10)**

### Consumer Understanding

Communications must be designed for the **actual range of customers** receiving them — not a hypothetical average. This explicitly includes customers with vulnerability characteristics such as low literacy, numeracy difficulties, or cognitive impairment. **(PS22/9 §8.10)**

For example: for a sub-prime credit product, the target market is more likely to include customers with financial difficulty — so signposting to support services should be more prominently featured across a broader range of communications. **(PS22/9 §8.10)**

### Consumer Support

Firms must respond **flexibly** to customers with characteristics of vulnerability. Support channels and processes must accommodate diverse needs. **(PS22/9 §9.7)**

Example from PS22/9: a customer unable to read large print or braille asked their bank to send communications by email so they could be converted to speech. The bank continued sending paper communications. The FCA specifically cited this as the type of scenario where firms must respond to customer needs. **(PS22/9 §9.7)**

## Four-Driver Culture Model (FG22/5)

FG22/5 Chapter 10 sets out a four-driver model that firms must use to embed the Duty — including vulnerability obligations — into their culture. Each driver must actively support the delivery of good outcomes, including for vulnerable customers. **(FG22/5 §10.6)**

| Driver | What it requires |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Purpose** | The firm's purpose should be consistent with the Duty. Staff must understand how the firm's purpose is relevant to delivering good outcomes for customers. |
| **Leadership** | Leaders should be competent and accountable, and demonstrate commitment to delivering good outcomes for customers. |
| **People** | Delivering good outcomes must be reflected in how people are managed and rewarded. Staff must be trained to deliver good outcomes, including for vulnerable customers. |
| **Governance** | Controls and key processes must be set up to identify where the firm is not delivering good outcomes, with a strategy to understand root causes and manage and mitigate poor outcomes. |

The board-level Duty champion — who should be an Independent Non-Executive Director (NED) where possible — is responsible for ensuring the Duty, including the vulnerability dimension, is regularly discussed at board level. **(FG22/5 §10.10)**

Firms must produce an annual board-level assessment of whether they are delivering good outcomes for customers, including evidence of whether any group (including vulnerable groups) is receiving worse outcomes than another. The board must agree remedial action before signing off this assessment. **(FG22/5 §10.12–§10.13)**

## Data and Monitoring

Firms are **not required to systematically collect new data** on customers' protected characteristics — the FCA recognises data protection constraints and that customers may not welcome this. **(PS22/9 §10.15)**

However, firms must:
- Use any protected characteristics data they already hold, in accordance with data protection law. **(PS22/9 §10.15)**
- Monitor whether **distinct groups of customers** (including those with vulnerability characteristics, those from particular geographic regions, long-standing customers, or those using a specific distribution channel) are getting worse outcomes. **(PS22/9 §13.11)**
- Use methods other than direct data collection where relevant — complaints analysis, customer feedback, staff observations. **(PS22/9 §10.15)**

### Enhanced Monitoring Approaches (FG22/5)

Where firms cannot or choose not to collect protected characteristics data directly, FG22/5 identifies additional methods for gaining insight into the experiences of customers with vulnerability or protected characteristics: **(FG22/5 §11.42)**

- **Focus groups:** Research into the experiences and needs of particular groups — e.g. focus groups with older customers or deep dives into their specific needs.
- **Consumer organisation partnerships:** Working with organisations that represent a particular group can provide insight into needs and experiences without direct data collection.
- **Customer journey audits:** An audit of journeys can identify differences in experience or frictions that affect certain customer groups but not others.
- **Staff networks:** Drawing on the diversity of the firm's own staff — e.g. a network of staff with a particular characteristic providing insight into how that group experiences the firm's services.
- **Proxy data:** Firms may use proxy variables (e.g. customer name and postcode as a proxy for ethnicity) to infer outcomes for different groups. This requires careful management of data protection obligations. **(FG22/5 §11.42)**

### Special Category Data — GDPR Basis Required

Where a firm processes special category data (e.g. health data relating to vulnerability, disability, or mental health) in this context, it must identify: **(FG22/5 §11.40)**

1. A lawful basis under **Article 6** of the UK GDPR; and
2. A separate condition for processing under **Article 9** of the UK GDPR.

These two bases do not need to be linked. See FCA Guidance for firms on the fair treatment of vulnerable customers (Appendix 1) for further data protection considerations. **(FG22/5 §11.40–§11.41)**

Firms must be proactive where evidence emerges that consumers sharing a protected characteristic are disproportionately experiencing harm — they must review their conduct and satisfy themselves they are complying with both the Duty and the Equality Act 2010. **(FG22/5 §11.43)**

## The "Average Customer" Drop

The FCA removed all references to "average customer" from the final rules, replacing it with "customers intended to receive the communication" and "customers in their target market." **(PS22/9 §8.10, §10.9)**

The reason: designing for an average obscures the needs of customers at the edges of the target market distribution — particularly vulnerable customers. Firms must account for the **range of needs**, not a single central case.

## Supervisory Findings — Named Poor Practices (February 2024 Implementation Review)

The FCA's February 2024 implementation review identified specific vulnerability failures observed in the regulated population. These go beyond the conceptual guidance in PS22/9 and FG22/5 and represent the FCA's supervisory red lines.

### Wealth Management: Near-Zero Vulnerability Identification

The FCA's wealth data survey found 49% of portfolio managers and 69% of stockbrokers identified no vulnerable customers at all in their customer base. The FCA issued a Dear CEO letter to wealth and stockbroking firms, noting that statistically 50% of people will be classed as vulnerable at some point. The FCA considers it implausible that firms in this sector have no vulnerable customers and is directly scrutinising this. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**

### Named Poor Practices to Avoid

- **Blanket age-based vulnerability assessment:** Automatically classifying all customers above a certain age as vulnerable. Age is relevant to vulnerability, but a generalised approach fails to tailor support to individual needs. The Duty requires individual-level consideration. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**
- **Demanding evidence of vulnerability:** Asking customers who self-identify as vulnerable to provide evidence of that vulnerability. This is a barrier to disclosure and to receiving appropriate support. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**
- **Warning customers that disclosure may affect their service:** Telling customers who identify as vulnerable that it might affect their ability to receive the service. The FCA treats this as a barrier to vulnerability identification. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**
- **Repeated re-disclosure between teams:** Requiring customers to repeatedly disclose their circumstances when passed between teams. The FCA frames the distress caused by repeated disclosure as itself a form of foreseeable harm — a direct breach of the cross-cutting rules. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**

## Key Points for Agents

- Vulnerability is embedded across all four outcomes — it is not a fifth outcome or a separate test.
- Two categories matter: characteristics of vulnerability (dynamic, circumstantial) and protected characteristics under the Equality Act 2010 (legal basis).
- Firms must design products, communications and support for the actual range of customer needs in their target market — including vulnerability characteristics — not for a hypothetical average.
- Firms are not required to collect new protected characteristics data, but must use data they hold and monitor outcomes for distinct customer groups.
- Vulnerability is dynamic — anyone can become temporarily or permanently vulnerable, including customers who were not vulnerable at point of sale.
- The four-driver culture model (Purpose, Leadership, People, Governance) from FG22/5 Ch.10 is the FCA's framework for embedding vulnerability obligations into firm culture — all four must actively support good outcomes for vulnerable customers.
- Where special category data is processed in connection with vulnerability monitoring, both an Article 6 lawful basis and an Article 9 condition under the UK GDPR must be identified.
- Four named supervisory red lines (from the February 2024 review): blanket age classification, demanding evidence of vulnerability, warning customers disclosure may affect their service, and repeated re-disclosure between teams.

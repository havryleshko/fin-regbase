# Prudential Risk Management — Payment Institutions and E-Money Institutions

## The Obligation

PIs and EMIs must manage their prudential risks — capital adequacy, liquidity, and financial resilience — in a manner that is proportionate to the nature, scale and complexity of their activities. This obligation is rooted in the PSRs 2017 and EMRs 2011. **(Portfolio letter, March 2023, Outcome 1, Priority 2)**

Guidance is available in:
- The **FCA Approach Document** — prudential risk management section
- **FG20/1** — FCA finalised guidance on assessing the adequacy of financial resources (applies across firm types but is directly referenced for payment firms)

---

## Capital Requirements

### Regulatory Capital Minimum

PIs and EMIs must meet their regulatory capital requirement at all times. The method and amount depend on the type of firm and the payment services or e-money activities it carries out:

- **PIs:** Capital requirements are calculated under one of three methods set out in the PSRs (Method A, B, or C) based on fixed amounts, overheads, or payment volumes. The applicable method depends on the payment services the firm is authorised to provide.
- **EMIs:** Capital requirements are calculated on the basis of average outstanding e-money and fixed minimum requirements under the EMRs.

Meeting the **minimum** regulatory capital requirement is necessary but not sufficient.

### Capital Above the Minimum

Firms should consider holding **additional capital above the minimum** where the risks they face — based on their specific business model and current macroeconomic conditions — warrant it. **(Portfolio letter, March 2023)**

**Named FCA finding:** A failure to consider whether additional capital is prudent given the firm's specific risk profile. The FCA distinguishes between a firm that has assessed its risks and concluded its minimum capital is adequate, and a firm that simply holds the minimum without having performed that assessment. The latter is a compliance failure.

---

## Liquidity Risk Management

Firms must identify and quantify the liquidity risks they face and actively manage them.

**Named FCA finding:** A lack of appropriate liquidity risk management, including inadequate identification and quantification of liquidity risks. **(Portfolio letter, March 2023)**

Liquidity risk for PIs and EMIs arises primarily from:
- Timing differences between receipt of customer funds and execution of payment transactions
- Dependence on external funding (credit lines, investor capital)
- Concentration risk — reliance on a small number of banking partners or settlement routes
- Settlement obligations in the event of an operational disruption

Firms must:
- Identify their liquidity risk exposures explicitly
- Quantify those risks under normal and stressed conditions
- Set key risk indicators (KRIs) for liquidity
- Plan ahead to maintain adequate liquidity resources — including, where appropriate, arranging access to credit lines that can be drawn when needed **(Portfolio letter, March 2023)**

---

## Scenario Planning and Stress Testing

**Named FCA finding:** Lack of scenario planning and stress testing. **(Portfolio letter, March 2023)**

Firms must forecast likely financial performance across a **range of plausible scenarios**, including stressed scenarios, and use that analysis to validate whether their capital and liquidity resources are adequate.

What adequate stress testing looks like:
- **Base case:** Expected financial performance over the planning horizon
- **Downside scenarios:** Scenarios reflecting revenue shortfall, customer loss, credit line withdrawal, or regulatory action
- **Severe scenarios:** Scenarios that approach or trigger wind-down conditions (linked to wind-down plan triggers — see [[wind-down-planning]])

Stress testing must be more than a box-checking exercise. The FCA expects firms to use the results to make decisions about capital, liquidity, and risk appetite.

---

## Risk Appetite

Firms must set or regularly review their **risk appetite** including key risk indicators. Risk appetite statements should be:
- Specific enough to be measurable
- Linked to the firm's actual business model and risk profile
- Reviewed as the business changes and as macroeconomic conditions evolve

**(Portfolio letter, March 2023)**

---

## Context: Macroeconomic Environment

The March 2023 portfolio letter was issued against the backdrop of the cost-of-living crisis and tightening financial conditions. The FCA explicitly noted that many PIs and EMIs are **unprofitable and reliant on external funding for survival** — a risk that is heightened during periods of rising interest rates and declining investor appetite for growth-stage financial services firms.

This context reinforces the importance of:
- Realistic financial forecasting
- Stress testing that includes external funding withdrawal scenarios
- Early engagement with the FCA if the firm's financial position is deteriorating

---

## Relationship to Wind-Down Planning

Firms whose stress tests indicate they may approach wind-down conditions should ensure their wind-down plans are up to date, include realistic cost analysis, and are capable of being executed with the financial resources available. Prudential risk management and wind-down planning are not independent — they must be designed and reviewed together. **(Portfolio letter, March 2023; Approach Document §3.73–3.76)**

---

## Reference: FG20/1

FCA Finalised Guidance FG20/1 ("Assessing the adequacy of financial resources") provides the FCA's framework for firms assessing their capital and liquidity adequacy. Although originally issued in the context of FCA-regulated investment firms, the FCA explicitly directs payments firms to it as relevant guidance. Key FG20/1 themes directly applicable to PIs/EMIs:
- Boards must own the assessment of financial resource adequacy
- Assessment must be based on risk-specific analysis, not only regulatory minimums
- Stress tests must be severe but plausible
- Management information must enable the board to monitor financial resource adequacy on an ongoing basis

---

## Key Points for Agents

- PIs and EMIs must meet regulatory capital requirements at all times; beyond the minimum, they must assess whether additional capital is prudent given their specific risk profile.
- Liquidity risk must be explicitly identified and quantified — not just acknowledged. KRIs must be set and monitored.
- Stress testing is a regulatory expectation, not a best practice. The FCA will ask whether the firm has tested plausible downside scenarios and used the results to validate capital and liquidity.
- Risk appetite statements must be specific, measurable, and linked to the firm's actual activities — not generic.
- FG20/1 is the primary reference guidance for financial resource adequacy assessment for payment firms, alongside the Approach Document.

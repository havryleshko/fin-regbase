# Wind-Down Planning — Payment Institutions and E-Money Institutions

## The Obligation

All PIs and EMIs must maintain a **wind-down plan** that enables an orderly, solvent winding down of the business. The expectation was first set in FCA guidance issued July 2020 and is now incorporated in the **Approach Document at §3.73–3.76**. **(Portfolio letter, March 2023, Outcome 1, Priority 3)**

The plan is a firm-level planning obligation — distinct from the insolvency regime that would apply after failure. Wind-down planning is about maintaining the ability to exit the market in a controlled, solvent manner, protecting customers throughout. It operates alongside — and depends on — the safeguarding framework: customer funds must be returned to customers during wind-down, not just in post-insolvency proceedings.

---

## What a Wind-Down Plan Must Contain

Drawing from Approach Document §3.73–3.76 and the FCA's TR22/1 thematic review findings:

### 1. Triggers for Commencing Wind-Down

Plans must include **clear triggers** that would cause the firm to commence an orderly wind-down. These should be pre-defined, objective, and tested against the firm's financial position on an ongoing basis.

**Named FCA failure:** Many firms have no trigger analysis at all, or triggers are so vague as to be non-operational. A plan that simply says "when the board decides it is appropriate" does not meet this requirement. Specific triggers should include capital or liquidity thresholds, loss of a key contract or customer concentration event, and regulatory intervention scenarios.

### 2. Step-by-Step Wind-Down Procedure

Plans must contain **sufficient detail** to make the plan practical and usable in reality — not a high-level summary.

**Named FCA failure:** Plans that describe outcomes rather than steps. The plan should address, at minimum: how payment processing will be wound down or transferred; how outstanding payment transactions will be settled; how customer funds will be returned (linking to the safeguarding framework); how third-party contracts will be managed and exited; staff notice periods; regulatory notification obligations; and timeline sequencing.

### 3. Timeline Estimates

Wind-down plans must include timeline estimates for each material step.

**Named FCA failure:** Plans that assume wind-down can be completed more quickly than is operationally realistic. Timelines must account for notice periods in contracts, regulatory approval requirements (e.g. for permission variations), and the time required to return customer funds.

### 4. Cost and Cash Analysis

Plans must address the **financial resources required** to execute the wind-down, including staff costs during the wind-down period, third-party costs, and legal and regulatory costs.

**Named FCA failure:** Insufficient analysis of the costs and cash requirements for wind-down. A firm that cannot fund its own wind-down will be unable to complete it in an orderly way, converting a planned exit into a disorderly insolvency.

## Relationship to Prudential Risk Management

Wind-down planning and prudential risk management are linked. A firm with adequate capital buffers and stress-tested financial plans will be better positioned to identify when wind-down triggers have been met and to fund the wind-down process itself. A firm that has not stress-tested its financial position may enter wind-down in a weaker financial state than its plan assumed. See [[psr-emi-prudential]].

---

## Relationship to Safeguarding

The return of customer funds during wind-down depends on the safeguarding framework being in place and operational: the firm must have segregated accounts and customer-level records that allow funds to be returned to the correct customers. A firm with adequate safeguarding records but a deficient wind-down plan may have the funds but lack the procedural framework to return them efficiently. See [[safeguarding-pis-emis]].

## Reference Materials

The FCA notes that while its **Wind-down Planning Guide** does not directly apply as guidance to payments and e-money firms, firms may refer to it and the **TR22/1 thematic review** as good practice and for information about what to consider when preparing wind-down plans. **(Portfolio letter, March 2023)**

Both are available on the FCA website. TR22/1 is the FCA's published review of wind-down plans across multiple firm types and sets out what the FCA considers good and poor practice in detail.

---

## FCA Supervisory Posture

The FCA confirmed in the portfolio letter that many firms have not yet created wind-down plans at all, and that of those reviewed, most fail to meet expectations. This is an area of active supervisory scrutiny for the payments portfolio.

Boards of PIs and EMIs should treat wind-down planning as a standing governance obligation — not a one-time exercise. Plans must be reviewed regularly and updated whenever there is a material change to the business model, customer base, or financial position.

---

## Key Points for Agents

- All PIs and EMIs must maintain a wind-down plan under FCA Approach Document §3.73–3.76 — this is a supervisory expectation, not merely guidance.
- Four named common plan failures: over-optimistic timelines; insufficient procedural detail; no trigger analysis; inadequate cost and cash analysis.
- The FCA Wind-down Planning Guide and TR22/1 thematic review are useful references even though they do not directly apply as guidance to payment firms.
- Wind-down plans and safeguarding arrangements are operationally dependent: customer funds can only be returned efficiently in wind-down if both are adequate.
- Plans must be kept current — review at least annually and on material business changes.

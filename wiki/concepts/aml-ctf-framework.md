# AML/CTF Regulatory Framework — Governance, MLRO, Risk Assessment, and SARs

## The Regulatory Architecture

UK AML/CTF obligations derive from multiple overlapping legal sources that apply simultaneously:

| Source | What it does |
|--------|-------------|
| **Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (MLR)** | The primary subordinate legislation implementing the EU 4th/5th AML Directives. Covers risk assessment (Reg 18), CDD (Regs 27–35), EDD (Regs 33–35), record keeping (Reg 40), and senior manager appointment (Reg 21). Applies to MLR-regulated firms directly. |
| **SYSC 3.2.6R–J** | FCA Handbook rules on financial crime systems and controls, applying to firms not subject to MLR but within FCA's scope. |
| **SYSC 6.3** | Additional FCA rules for banks specifically, including the MLRO appointment requirement. |
| **POCA 2002 s.330–331** | Criminal law. Nominated officers and employees commit an offence if they fail to report knowledge or reasonable suspicion of money laundering to NCA. |
| **Terrorism Act 2000 s.21A** | Criminal law. Obligation to report terrorist financing suspicions. |
| **FCG (Financial Crime Guide)** | FCA sourcebook guidance on how firms should operate their systems and controls to counter financial crime. Not rules, but the FCA considers FCG compliance when assessing firms. |

The JMLSG produces detailed operational guidance for firms that the FCA explicitly endorses. FCG is not intended to replace or compete with JMLSG guidance. **(FCG 3.1.7)**

## Scope — Who Must Comply

**Full scope:** Firms subject to SYSC 3.2.6A–J or SYSC 6.3 (most FCA-authorised firms with AML responsibilities), and Annex I financial institutions and e-money institutions supervised by the FCA under the MLR. **(FCG 3.1.1)**

**Out of scope of FCG:** Payment institutions — supervised for MLR compliance by HMRC, not the FCA. **(FCG 3.1.2)**

**Partial relevance:** Firms with more limited AML responsibilities (mortgage brokers, general insurers, general insurance intermediaries) — FCG provides useful guidance even where full MLR obligations do not apply. **(FCG 3.1.3)**

**MLRO section exception:** FCG 3.2.2G does not apply to sole traders with no employees. **(FCG 3.1.4)**

## Governance — Senior Management AML Responsibility

Senior management must take responsibility for the firm's AML measures. This is an active obligation — not delegatable to compliance or the MLRO. It requires: knowing the ML risks the firm faces; ensuring steps are taken to mitigate those risks effectively; and receiving informative, objective information to meet their AML obligations. **(FCG 3.2.1)**

### Board-Level Officer Appointment

Where appropriate to the size and nature of the business, firms must appoint a **board member (or equivalent senior management)** as the officer responsible for MLR compliance. A nominated officer must also be appointed under MLR Reg 21(3). These roles can be held by the same individual. **(MLR Reg 21(1)(a), 21(3); FCG 3.2.1)**

**Notification requirement:** The FCA must be notified of this individual's identity within **14 days** of appointment (including subsequent appointments). **(MLR Reg 21(4))**

### Senior Management Self-Assessment Questions (FCG 3.2.1)

- Who has overall responsibility for establishing and maintaining effective AML controls? Are they sufficiently senior?
- Do senior management receive informative, objective information sufficient to meet their AML obligations?
- How regularly do senior management commission reports from the MLRO? (Should be at least annually.)
- How are senior management involved in approving high-risk customer relationships, including PEPs?

### Good vs Poor Practice — Governance

| Good practice | Poor practice |
|---------------|--------------|
| Reward structures account for AML compliance failings | Little evidence AML is taken seriously by senior management — treated as a regulatory necessity, not genuine concern |
| High-risk relationship decisions reviewed and challenged independently of the business line | Senior management attach greater importance to reputational risk than corruption risk |
| Documentation to senior management gives accurate picture of ML risk | Board never considers MLRO reports |
| UK parent ensures AML policies apply to all overseas branches and subsidiaries | Branch or subsidiary relies on group policies that do not fully comply with UK AML legislation |

**(FCG 3.2.1)**

## The Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO)

Firms subject to SYSC 3.2.6A–J or SYSC 6.3 must appoint an MLRO. The MLRO is responsible for oversight of the firm's AML compliance and acts as the focal point for all AML activity. The MLR Reg 21(1)(a) senior management appointment and the MLRO can be the same individual — no separate FCA notification is required if so. **(FCG 3.2.2)**

### What the MLRO Role Requires

The MLRO must be:
- **Independent** — not subordinate to business lines whose conduct they are responsible for overseeing
- **Knowledgeable** — must understand the policies they oversee and the rationale behind them
- **Robust and well-resourced** — able to pose effective challenge to the business where warranted
- **Well-connected** — senior management and staff, including senior management, must consult the MLRO on AML matters
- **Accessible to high-risk customer data** — able to retrieve information about the firm's highest-risk customers on request and without delay

The MLRO must escalate relevant matters to senior management and, where appropriate, the board. **(FCG 3.2.2)**

### Named Failure Modes — MLRO

- **Lacks credibility or authority** due to inexperience or insufficiently senior role
- **Does not understand the policies** they are supposed to oversee or their rationale
- **Cannot retrieve high-risk customer information** promptly on request; plays no role in monitoring such relationships
- Where the firm is part of a group: has not considered whether **group policy adequately addresses UK AML obligations**

**(FCG 3.2.2)**

The Dear CEO letter to wealth management and stockbroking firms (November 2023) called for "experienced and independent SMF 16/17 holders" — FCG 3.2.2 provides the operational definition of what that requires. **(FCG 3.2.2; FCA Dear CEO letter, November 2023)**

## Risk Assessment

Firms must undertake a documented risk assessment of their exposure to ML, TF, and **proliferation financing (PF)** risk. **(MLR Reg 18, 18A; FCG 3.2.3)**

**PF risk assessment is a standalone MLR Reg 18A obligation** — it can be integrated into the practice-wide risk assessment document but is a separate legal requirement from the AML/CTF risk assessment under Reg 18. See [[proliferation-financing]] for the full PF framework, including trade controls and dual-use goods.

The assessment must be:
- **Comprehensive** — covering all material risk factors
- **Proportionate** — to the nature, scale and complexity of the firm's activities
- **Dynamic** — regularly reviewed to ensure it remains current
- **Operationally connected** — it must demonstrably inform the design of AML controls and day-to-day decisions, including CDD levels and customer acceptance decisions

### Risk Factors to Assess

Risk assessment must consider, at minimum: **(FCG 3.2.3)**

- Customer types and beneficial ownership structures (including complex or opaque structures)
- Products and services
- Geographic exposure (countries of customer operation and domicile)
- Delivery channels (internet, telephone, branches)
- Sector risk
- Transactions
- Involvement in public contracts

**Information sources:** National Risk Assessments, FATF mutual evaluations and typology reports, NCA alerts, press reports, court judgements, NGO reports, commercial due diligence providers. **(FCG 3.2.3)**

The **Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023** now allows firms to share information with each other for the purpose of preventing, detecting and investigating economic crime. This information should inform risk-based decision-making, not be shared for commercial reasons or to exclude customers inappropriately. **(FCG 3.2.3)**

### Named Risk Assessment Failures

- **Impossible-to-classify system:** Risk classification designed so it is almost impossible to classify a relationship as high-risk
- **Overrideable scores:** Relationship managers can override customer risk scores without sufficient evidence to support their decision
- **Profitability influence:** Risk assessments unduly influenced by the potential profitability of relationships
- **Group reliance:** UK branch or subsidiary relies on group risk assessments without assessing their compliance with UK AML requirements
- **Unjustified low scores:** Higher-risk countries allocated low-risk scores specifically to avoid enhanced due diligence

**(FCG 3.2.3)**

## Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) and Law Enforcement Liaison

### The SAR Obligation

The nominated officer has a **legal obligation** to report knowledge or suspicion of money laundering to the National Crime Agency (NCA) through a SAR. **(POCA s.330–331; FCG 3.2.10)**

All staff must report their suspicions to the nominated officer. The nominated officer then decides whether a report to NCA is necessary based on all available information. Staff must be trained to make internal reports — failure to train staff exposes them to **personal legal liability** and increases the risk that suspicious activity goes unreported. **(FCG 3.2.10)**

Law enforcement may also seek information from firms through **Production Orders** — firms must have clear, documented processes with defined timetables for responding. **(FCG 3.2.10)**

### What Makes a Good SAR

- Sets out a clear narrative of events
- Includes actionable detail: names, addresses, passport numbers, phone numbers, email addresses
- States reasons for suspicion in plain English
- References previous related SARs with context, not just a cross-reference
- Documents the decision-making rationale clearly

**(FCG 3.2.10)**

### Named Poor Practices — SARs

| Failure | Consequence |
|---------|------------|
| "Defensive SARs" — passing all internal reports to NCA without genuine assessment | Reports of little value to law enforcement |
| Nominated officer dismisses escalated concerns without documenting reasons | Creates accountability gap; may constitute a criminal offence |
| Nominated officer turns a blind eye where a SAR might harm the business | **Explicitly flagged as a potential criminal offence** **(FCG 3.2.10)** |
| Failure to train staff to make internal reports | Staff exposed to personal legal liability; suspicious activity may go unreported |

**(FCG 3.2.10)**

**Alpari enforcement case:** Alpari (UK) Ltd fined £140,000 for poor AML controls including inadequate CDD and monitoring. The former MLRO was personally fined £14,000 for failing to fulfil their duties — demonstrating the personal accountability that the SAR framework creates. **(FCG 3.2.14)**

**Operational detail — suspicion threshold, consent regime, and tipping off:** JMLSG Chapter 6 provides legal definitions of "knowledge," "suspicion" (subjective — beyond mere speculation, with some factual foundation), and "reasonable grounds to know or suspect" (an objective criminal test that applies even without personal suspicion). When suspicion arises before a transaction, the nominated officer must seek **DAML consent** from the NCA — not merely file a SAR. NCA has 7 working days to refuse; 31-day moratorium if refused (extendable to 186 days). Tipping off is a separate criminal offence. See [[sar-consent-tipping-off]] for the full framework. **(JMLSG §6.10–6.71)**

## Record Keeping

Firms must retain CDD documents, data and information for: **(MLR Reg 40; FCG 3.2.11)**

- **Five years** after the business relationship ends, or five years after an occasional transaction
- Records relating to transactions within a relationship need not be kept beyond **ten years**
- Data must be **deleted** after the applicable retention period (GDPR compliance)
- Records must be retrievable promptly in response to a Production Order

Where a firm is relied on by others to do CDD checks, it must keep records for the same period. **(FCG 3.2.11)**

**Personal data processing note:** Data collected under the MLR should only be processed for the purposes of preventing ML or TF. **(MLR Reg 41)**

## Counter-Terrorism Financing (CTF)

Many AML controls directly overlap with CTF obligations: risk assessment, CDD, transaction monitoring, SAR reporting, and law enforcement liaison all apply equally to terrorist financing. **(FCG 3.2.12)**

Key CTF-specific obligations:
- Financial crime training must **explicitly mention terrorist financing** — not only money laundering
- Firms with cross-border operations must assess terrorism-related risks in every country where they have a presence or do business
- Sources of CTF risk intelligence include: FATF typologies, NCA alerts, press reports, court judgements

**(FCG 3.2.12)**

## Relationship to Consumer Duty

AML/CTF and Consumer Duty are distinct regulatory regimes with overlapping subject matter:

- The FCA implementation review (February 2024) frames **fraud victim support** as a Consumer Duty consumer support obligation and **scam prevention** as a foreseeable harm issue under the cross-cutting rules.
- FCG 3 frames the **same underlying situations** as AML/CTF obligations under POCA, the Terrorism Act, and the MLR.
- Both frameworks apply simultaneously. A fraud incident may engage: (i) Consumer Duty — post-incident victim support obligation; and (ii) AML — SAR reporting obligation, transaction monitoring adequacy, and potentially a SYSC 6.3 systems and controls failure.
- The **Dear CEO letter to wealth management and stockbroking firms (November 2023)** explicitly frames financial crime prevention and Consumer Duty as co-equal supervisory priorities.

These are complementary obligations, not contradictory frameworks.

## Key Points for Agents

- AML/CTF is a separate regulatory regime from Consumer Duty — both can apply to the same firm simultaneously.
- Senior management must actively own AML — it cannot be delegated to compliance. The MLRO is a focal point, not the sole responsible party.
- The MLRO must be independent, knowledgeable, have a direct board/executive reporting line, and must be able to retrieve high-risk customer information promptly. Inexperience, lack of authority, or inability to access information are named failure modes.
- Firms must appoint a senior management/board-level MLR compliance officer and notify the FCA within 14 days.
- Risk assessments must be documented, comprehensive, and must demonstrably inform AML control design and customer acceptance decisions. Profitability must not influence risk classification.
- The nominated officer has a personal legal obligation to report ML suspicions to NCA — a "defensive" approach (passing everything through) or turning a blind eye are both failures; the latter is potentially criminal.
- When suspicion arises before a transaction, the nominated officer must seek DAML consent from the NCA — not merely file a SAR. See [[sar-consent-tipping-off]].
- Tipping off is a separate criminal offence — two variants (disclosing a SAR was made; disclosing an investigation is underway). Permitted disclosures (within-firm, within-group, common-customer inter-institution) do not trigger the offence.
- CDD records: five years post-relationship end; ten-year maximum; delete after retention period expires.
- CTF training must explicitly cover terrorist financing — not only money laundering.

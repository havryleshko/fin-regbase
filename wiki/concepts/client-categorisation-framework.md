# Client Categorisation Framework — COBS 3

## Why Categorisation Matters

Client category determines which regulatory protections apply. Most COBS obligations — suitability (COBS 9/9A), appropriateness (COBS 10/10A), best execution retail total consideration rule (COBS 11.2A.9R), costs and charges disclosure, and communications standards — differentiate by category. The highest protection applies to retail clients; the lowest (and most limited) to eligible counterparties.

---

## The Three Categories

| Category | Definition | Default? |
|---|---|---|
| **Retail client** | Not a professional client or ECP **(COBS 3.4.1R)** | Yes — residual |
| **Professional client** | Per se or elective professional **(COBS 3.5.1R)** | No — must qualify |
| **Eligible counterparty (ECP)** | Per se or elective ECP **(COBS 3.6.1R)** | No — must qualify |

**Retail is the default.** Any client who does not meet the criteria for professional or ECP is automatically a retail client. There is no positive application process for retail status.

---

## MiFID Trump Rule

**COBS 3.1.4R:** Where a firm conducts both MiFID and non-MiFID business for the same client, it must categorise that client using the MiFID provisions for all business. MiFID categorisation requirements override non-MiFID ones across the entire client relationship. This is not a choice — it applies automatically.

---

## Per Se Professional Clients

Per se professional clients are categorised by entity type or size. No application or assessment by the firm is required; the status arises by operation of law. **(COBS 3.5.2R)**

### Institutional category (any entity required to be authorised/regulated in financial markets)
Credit institutions, investment firms, other regulated/authorised financial institutions, insurance companies, CIS/UCITS and their management companies, pension funds and their management companies, commodity dealers, locals, and other institutional investors.

### Large undertaking — MiFID or equivalent third country business
Meeting **two of three** (company basis):
- Balance sheet total ≥ EUR 20,000,000
- Net turnover ≥ EUR 40,000,000
- Own funds ≥ EUR 2,000,000

### Large undertaking — non-MiFID business
Meeting **any one** of:
- Body corporate (incl. LLP) with called-up share capital or net assets ≥ **£5 million** (or group equivalent in previous 2 years)
- Undertaking meeting **two of**: EUR 12,500,000 balance sheet / EUR 25,000,000 net turnover / 250 average employees
- Partnership with net assets ≥ **£5 million**
- Trustee of non-pension trust with assets ≥ **£10 million**
- Trustee of occupational/SSAS or personal pension/stakeholder scheme with ≥ **50 members** AND ≥ **£10 million** AUM

### Governments and supranationals
National/regional governments managing public debt, central banks, international/supranational institutions (World Bank, IMF, ECB, EIB, etc.).

**Local public authorities exception:** A local public authority or municipality that does not manage public debt must be treated as a retail client — it cannot be a per se professional. It may only be treated as an elective professional if it separately satisfies the opt-up tests **(COBS 3.5.2BR)**.

---

## Elective Professional Clients — The Opt-Up

A firm may treat a client as an elective professional client only if all applicable elements of COBS 3.5.3R are satisfied. The client initiates the process; the firm cannot impose professional status. **(COBS 3.5.3R)**

### Step 1 — Qualitative test (always required)
The firm must undertake an adequate assessment of the client's expertise, experience, and knowledge, giving reasonable assurance that the client is capable of making its own investment decisions and understanding the risks involved, in light of the specific transactions or services envisaged.

Where the client is an entity, the qualitative test is performed in relation to the **person authorised to carry out transactions on behalf of the entity**, not the entity itself **(COBS 3.5.4R)**.

### Step 2 — Quantitative test (required for MiFID business)
At least **two of three** criteria must be satisfied:
- (a) **Transaction frequency:** 10 transactions per quarter on average over previous 4 quarters, in significant size, on the relevant market
- (b) **Portfolio size:** Cash deposits and financial instruments exceeding **EUR 500,000**
- (c) **Professional experience:** Worked in the financial sector for at least **one year** in a professional position requiring knowledge of the transactions or services envisaged

### Step 3 — Written procedure (always required, three elements)
- (a) Client states in writing that it wishes to be treated as a professional client (generally or for a specific service, transaction, type of transaction, or product)
- (b) Firm gives the client a **clear written warning** of the protections and investor compensation rights the client may lose
- (c) Client states in writing, **in a separate document from the contract**, that it is aware of the consequences of losing such protections

**Elective ≠ per se for knowledge purposes:** An elective professional client should not be presumed to possess market knowledge and experience comparable to a per se professional client **(COBS 3.5.7G)**. This distinction carries through to the COBS 9A professional client suitability assumptions.

---

## Ongoing Monitoring and Downward Re-Categorisation

**Client's obligation (COBS 3.5.8G):** Professional clients are responsible for keeping the firm informed about any change that could affect their current categorisation.

**Firm's obligation (COBS 3.5.9R):** If a firm becomes aware that a client no longer fulfils the initial conditions for elective professional status, it must take the appropriate action. Where that action involves re-categorising to retail, the firm must notify the client of its new categorisation.

This is a hard Rule. It creates an ongoing monitoring obligation — not merely a one-time assessment at the point of opt-up.

---

## Eligible Counterparties

ECPs receive the least regulatory protection. They can only hold ECP status in relation to **eligible counterparty business** (the business types listed in PRIN 1 Annex 1 R).

### Per se ECPs (COBS 3.6.2R)
Investment firms, credit institutions, insurance companies, UCITS CIS and management companies, pension funds and management companies, other FCA/UK-regulated financial institutions, national governments and public bodies managing national public debt, central banks, supranational organisations.

### Elective ECPs
For MiFID business: the client must be an undertaking, a per se professional client (excluding institutional investor under COBS 3.5.2R(5)), and must request ECP status **(COBS 3.6.4AR)**. The firm must follow the written warning and client confirmation procedure **(COBS 3.6.4BR)**.

For non-MiFID business: additional size test applies — body corporate with ≥ £10m called-up share capital, or meeting two quantitative tests under COBS 3.5.2R(3)(b) **(COBS 3.6.4R)**.

---

## Re-Categorisation to Higher Protection (COBS 3.7)

**Firm must allow requests (COBS 3.7.1R):** A firm must allow a professional client or ECP to request re-categorisation to a higher level of protection.

**ECP requests higher protection (COBS 3.7.3BR):**
- If the ECP does not expressly request retail treatment → the firm must treat them as a **professional client**
- If the ECP expressly requests retail → the firm must treat them as a **retail client**

This means the default result of an ECP's re-categorisation request is professional, not retail. A firm cannot assume retail treatment is requested.

**Per se professional requesting retail for MiFID business (COBS 3.7.5R):** Requires a written agreement specifying the scope (particular services, transactions, products, or applicable rules).

**Firm's own initiative (COBS 3.7.3AR):** A firm may on its own initiative re-categorise a client to a higher protection level — e.g., treating an ECP as professional or retail, or a per se professional as retail.

**Granularity (COBS 3.7.7G):** Re-categorisation can apply on a general basis, trade by trade, for specific rules, for particular services/transactions, or for specific product types.

**Re-categorisation as retail ≠ DISP eligible complainant (COBS 3.7.8G):** A client re-categorised as retail under COBS 3.7 does not automatically become an eligible complainant under DISP. The DISP 2.7 criteria apply entirely separately.

---

## Policies, Procedures and Records

**Written policies required (COBS 3.8.1R):** A firm must implement appropriate written internal policies and procedures for categorising its clients.

**Per-client records (COBS 3.8.2R):** For each client: (a) the categorisation established, with sufficient supporting information; (b) evidence of despatch of required notices; (c) a copy of any agreement entered into.

**Retention periods:**
| Business type | Period |
|---|---|
| Pension transfer/conversion/opt-out/FSAVC | Indefinitely |
| Life policy or pension contract | At least 5 years |
| MiFID or equivalent third country business | 5 years |
| Any other case | 3 years |

---

## Key Points for Agents

- **Retail is the default; no positive test.** Any client who is not a per se professional, elective professional, or ECP is a retail client by definition **(COBS 3.4.1R)**. There is no application or assessment for retail status.
- **MiFID categorisation prevails across mixed business (COBS 3.1.4R).** If a firm conducts any MiFID business with a client, MiFID categorisation rules govern all business — including non-MiFID business — with that client.
- **Elective professional requires all three steps (COBS 3.5.3R).** Qualitative test + quantitative test (MiFID) + written three-step procedure. Missing any element means the opt-up is invalid. The client initiates; the firm assesses and must verify.
- **Large undertaking thresholds diverge between MiFID and non-MiFID (COBS 3.5.2R).** MiFID: 2 of EUR 20m/40m/2m. Non-MiFID: any one of £5m capital, or 2 of EUR 12.5m/25m/250, plus separate partnership, trust, and pension scheme tests.
- **Ongoing obligation to re-categorise downward is a hard Rule (COBS 3.5.9R).** If a firm becomes aware an elective professional no longer qualifies, it must act — including notifying the client if re-categorised to retail.
- **ECP re-categorisation defaults to professional, not retail (COBS 3.7.3BR).** An ECP requesting higher protection without expressly requesting retail must be treated as professional client.
- **Re-categorisation ≠ DISP eligibility (COBS 3.7.8G).** Obtaining retail categorisation under COBS 3.7 does not make a client an eligible complainant under DISP. The DISP 2.7 criteria apply separately.

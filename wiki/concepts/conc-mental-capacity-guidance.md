# CONC — Mental Capacity Guidance

## What This Covers

CONC 2.10 provides FCA guidance on how firms must approach lending decisions where a customer may have a mental capacity limitation. This guidance applies at the three credit decision points: granting credit under a regulated credit agreement; significantly increasing the amount of credit; and setting a credit limit for running-account credit **(CONC 2.10.1G)**. It is all guidance (G), not binding rules, but the FCA expects compliance and the underlying obligation derives from Principle 6 and the duty to treat customers fairly.

The legal framework: the Mental Capacity Act 2005 governs for England and Wales; the Adults with Incapacity (Scotland) Act 2000 provides the equivalent framework for Scotland **(CONC 2.10.2G)**. References to the firm's knowledge or suspicion include the knowledge of its employees, appointed representatives, and agents **(CONC 2.10.2G(3))**.

**Mental capacity** is a person's ability to make a decision — specifically, the ability to understand, remember, and weigh up relevant information in order to reach a responsible borrowing decision **(CONC 2.10.3G)**.

---

## Presumption of Capacity

A firm should assume a customer has mental capacity at the time a credit decision is to be made, unless the firm **(CONC 2.10.4G)**:
- Knows otherwise; or
- Is told by a person it reasonably believes should know; or
- Reasonably suspects that the customer lacks capacity.

Where the firm reasonably suspects a customer has or may have a mental capacity limitation that would constrain their ability to make a borrowing decision, the firm should **not** treat the customer as lacking capacity unless it has first taken reasonable steps to assist the customer to make a decision and those steps have been unsuccessful **(CONC 2.10.5G)**.

Exclusion is the last resort. The obligation is to assist first.

---

## Common Causes of Mental Capacity Limitations

The FCA lists the following as among the most common potential causes of mental capacity limitations **(CONC 2.10.6G)**:
- Mental health conditions
- Dementia
- Learning disability
- Developmental disorders
- Neurological disability or brain injury
- Alcohol or drug (including prescribed drug) induced intoxication

The presence of any of these conditions does not automatically mean the customer lacks capacity to make a borrowing decision **(CONC 2.10.7G)**. Each case depends on the specific circumstances at the time.

---

## Nine Indicators of Mental Capacity Limitation

A firm is likely to have reasonable grounds to suspect a customer may have a mental capacity limitation if it observes a specific behavioural or other indication. CONC 2.10.8G lists the following examples:

| Indicator | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 | Where the firm has an existing relationship with the customer, the customer making a decision that appears unexpected or out of character |
| 2 | A relative, close friend, carer, or clinician raising a concern with the firm about the customer's capacity to make a borrowing decision |
| 3 | The firm has reason to believe the customer was diagnosed with an impairment that previously caused the customer not to have capacity for similar decisions |
| 4 | The firm has reason to believe the customer does not understand what they are applying for |
| 5 | The customer appears unable to understand the information and explanations provided by the firm, in particular concerning the key risks of entering into the agreement |
| 6 | The customer appears unable to retain the information and explanations provided by the firm to enable them to make the decision to borrow |
| 7 | The customer appears unable to weigh up the information and explanations provided by the firm to enable them to make the decision to borrow |
| 8 | The customer is unable to communicate a decision to borrow by any reasonable means |
| 9 | The customer appears confused about personal information that the firm requires, such as date of birth or address |

These are indicators to be considered in context — none is individually conclusive.

## Practices and Procedures Obligations

### Non-Discrimination

A firm should not unfairly discriminate against a customer who it understands, or reasonably suspects, has a mental capacity limitation — in particular by inappropriately denying access to credit **(CONC 2.10.9G(1))**. However, it is not inappropriate to decline to grant credit, or to decline to increase credit, where the firm reasonably believes the agreement would be voidable by the customer or would be void **(CONC 2.10.9G(2))**.

### Written Policies Required

Firms should take reasonable steps to have suitable business practices and procedures for the fair treatment of customers who may have a mental capacity limitation **(CONC 2.10.10G)**. These must be documented **(CONC 2.10.11G)**. Note: CONC 7.2.1R separately requires firms to establish and implement arrears policies and procedures which include procedures for the fair and appropriate treatment of customers with mental capacity limitations.

### What Procedures Must Do (CONC 2.10.12G)

Where a limitation is known or suspected, the firm's procedures must be designed to:
1. Assist the customer, where possible, to make an informed borrowing decision
2. Ensure its lending decision is informed and responsible and mitigates potential risks to the customer

The objective is to equalise outcomes — to place customers with limitations, to the extent possible, on an equivalent footing to customers without limitations **(CONC 2.10.16G)**.

---

## Practical Steps Firms Should Consider

### Clear Information and Alternative Formats

Firms should present clear, jargon-free information in explaining credit agreements **(CONC 2.10.14G)**. Where appropriate, firms should consider alternative, more accessible formats — consistent with statutory disclosure requirements.

### Allowing Sufficient Time (CONC 2.10.17G)

Where a limitation is known or suspected, the firm should consider allowing the customer:
1. Sufficient time to weigh up the information and explanations given
2. Sufficient time to make an informed borrowing decision
3. The option to defer the decision to a later date

### High Scrutiny on Creditworthiness

Where a mental capacity limitation is known or suspected, the firm should apply a high level of scrutiny to the customer's application in order to mitigate the risk of the customer entering into unsustainable borrowing **(CONC 2.10.18G)**. The firm should undertake an appropriate and effective creditworthiness assessment (see CONC 5.2A) and should not over-rely on information provided by the customer themselves **(CONC 2.10.19G)**.

The firm must balance the risk of unsustainable borrowing against the risk of inappropriately denying credit **(CONC 2.10.19G(1))**.

### The Unsuitable Credit Prohibition (CONC 2.10.20G)

A firm must not provide credit that the firm knows, or reasonably believes, to be unsuitable to the customer's needs — **even where that credit would be affordable** within the affordability assessment **(CONC 2.10.20G)**.

This is a critical point: affordability alone does not settle the question. A product can be affordable and still be unsuitable given the customer's individual circumstances and capacity limitations.

## Key Points for Agents

- Presume capacity. Do not assume a customer lacks mental capacity because they have a listed condition — each decision must be assessed individually at the time it is made.
- Nine indicators in CONC 2.10.8G give a practical checklist for when reasonable suspicion arises. The most operationally significant: customer cannot understand what they are applying for; behaviour out of character; third-party concern raised.
- The duty is to assist, not exclude. Where limitation is suspected, the firm must take reasonable steps to help the customer make a decision before treating them as lacking capacity.
- Apply high scrutiny to the creditworthiness assessment. Do not over-rely on the customer's own declarations about income, expenditure, or circumstances.
- Affordability does not settle suitability. A firm must not provide credit it knows or believes to be unsuitable to the customer's needs — even if it would pass the affordability test.
- Written procedures covering mental capacity are required. CONC 7.2.1R links this obligation to arrears policies as well.

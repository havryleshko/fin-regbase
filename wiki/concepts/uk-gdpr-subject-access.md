# UK GDPR — Right of Access and Subject Access Requests (Article 15)

## Overview

The right of access under Article 15 of the UK GDPR gives individuals the right to obtain a copy of their personal data and supplementary information. This right is exercised through a subject access request (SAR). It enables individuals to understand how and why their data is being used and to verify that processing is lawful.

The cross-cutting request-handling framework applies to SARs as to all other rights — see [[uk-gdpr-individual-rights-overview]] for full detail. Key obligations:

- **Verbal SARs are fully valid** — a request made by phone, in person, or via social media is as valid as a written request. No specific words, form, or named contact required. **(UK GDPR Art. 15)**
- **Response deadline: one calendar month** from receipt. **(UK GDPR Art. 12(3))** The ICO recommends targeting 28 days to allow time for review before the deadline. Extension to three months is available for complex or numerous requests, but the individual must be informed within the first month.
- **Clarification may pause the clock** — but only where the controller processes a large volume of data and the scope is genuinely unclear. The clock pauses from when clarification is requested until it is received. This is not a routine step; it cannot be used to delay processing routinely.
- **SAR response must include two things:** (a) a copy of all personal data; and (b) **supplementary information equivalent to Art. 13/14 privacy notice content** (purposes, legal basis, retention periods, rights, right to complain to ICO, existence of automated decision-making).

This page covers the SAR-specific rules that go beyond that cross-cutting framework.

**Source note:** The ICO's detailed sub-pages on SAR mechanics (identity verification, conducting searches, each DPA 2018 exemption in detail, format/security of delivery, special cases for credit, health, and educational data) have not been ingested. Where sub-page detail is needed, the ICO's full subject access guidance should be consulted.

## Recognising a SAR

A SAR is valid if it is clear that the individual is asking for their own personal data. No specific form of words is required — the individual does not need to:
- Use the phrase "subject access request" or cite Article 15
- Make the request in writing (verbal SARs are valid)
- Direct the request to a specific person or contact point
- Use a specific channel (including social media)

Any employee, in any part of the organisation, may receive a valid SAR. Firms must have processes to ensure any such request is identified and escalated appropriately.

## Third-Party SAR Mechanics

A third party may make a SAR on behalf of an individual. Common scenarios in financial services:
- A solicitor acting for a complainant pre-litigation
- A relative or carer acting for a vulnerable or incapacitated individual
- A request submitted via an online portal or representative service

**Before responding:** The controller must be satisfied that the third party is entitled to act on behalf of the individual. The **burden of providing evidence of authority sits with the third party** — the controller does not need to seek it out. The SAR need not be actioned until satisfactory evidence is received.

Evidence of authority may include: a signed letter of authority from the individual; a power of attorney; evidence of court-appointed deputyship. The level of evidence required should be proportionate to the sensitivity of the data held.

## Child SAR Framework

Where a SAR is made in relation to data held about a child, the controller must first assess whether the child is mature enough to understand their rights. The framework:

| Scenario | Response |
|---------|---------|
| Child is competent (understands their rights) and makes the SAR directly | Respond directly to the child |
| Child is competent and authorises a parent/guardian to act | Respond to the parent/guardian as authorised |
| Child is competent and authorises someone other than a parent/guardian | Respond to the authorised person |
| Child is not competent; parent/guardian requests on their behalf | Respond to parent/guardian if it is evidently in the child's best interests |
| Child is not competent; no clear best-interests basis | Exercise caution; may need to withhold or seek clarification |

Where disclosing to a child: use clear and plain language appropriate to the child's level of understanding.

## The Clarification Pause

Distinct from the 2-month extension, this is a **pause** of the response clock available where the controller processes a large amount of information about the individual and the scope of the request is not clear.

**Conditions for using the clarification pause:**
- The controller processes a large volume of data about the individual
- It is genuinely not clear what information or processing activities the request relates to
- The controller asks the individual to specify the scope

**Effect:** The response clock is paused from the date the clarification request is sent until the date clarification is received.

**Constraint:** Any supplementary information the controller is able to provide must still be supplied within the original 1-month period — the pause does not defer the supplementary information obligation.

**Not a routine step:** The clarification pause is not a standard first response to all SARs. It applies where the large volume of data genuinely makes it unclear what the individual wants. Firms that use it routinely to delay processing will not satisfy this condition.

## What Must Be Provided in a SAR Response

An individual is entitled to receive two things:

### 1. A Copy of Their Personal Data

All personal data the controller processes about the individual. This includes data in:
- Digital systems and databases
- Emails and documents
- Call recordings and chat logs
- Structured manual files
- (Unstructured manual records are subject to different search rules — see ICO sub-page guidance)

### 2. Supplementary Information

The supplementary information largely corresponds with what would be provided in a privacy notice under Articles 13 and 14 — i.e., the mandatory content of the right to be informed. This includes:

- The purposes of and legal basis for the processing
- The categories of personal data processed
- Recipients or categories of recipients
- Retention periods (or criteria for determining them)
- The existence of any automated decision-making including profiling, with meaningful information about the logic, significance and envisaged consequences
- All the individual's data subject rights
- The right to complain to the ICO

See [[uk-gdpr-right-to-be-informed]] for the full Art. 13/14 content framework.

### Format and Delivery

| Scenario | Format requirement |
|---------|------------------|
| Request made electronically | Respond in a commonly used electronic format (unless individual requests otherwise) |
| Request made non-electronically | Any appropriate format — establish individual's preference before fulfilling |
| Individual cannot access a particular format | Consider alternative formats or remote access with download |
| Individual requests verbal response | Permissible if identity confirmed by other means — must log date, respondent, and content |

**Remote access option:** As an alternative to providing a copy, the individual may be permitted to access their data remotely and download it in an appropriate format.

**Security:** The controller is responsible for ensuring secure delivery. Data must not be transmitted in a way that exposes it to unauthorised access.

**Record-keeping for verbal responses:** Date of request; date of response; identity of person who provided the information; description of information provided.

## Reasonable Search

The controller must make reasonable efforts to find and retrieve the requested information. This is not an unlimited obligation — searches that would be unreasonable or disproportionate to the importance of providing access are not required. The reasonableness standard takes account of the nature of the request, the complexity of the controller's information environment, and the importance of the data to the individual.

## Third-Party Data in SAR Responses

Where complying with a SAR would require disclosing data about other individuals:

**Step 1:** Consider whether compliance is possible without disclosing information that identifies the third party (e.g., by redacting third-party names and identifiers).

**Step 2:** If compliance without disclosure is not possible, the controller is **not required to comply** — unless:
- The third party **consents** to the disclosure; or
- It is **reasonable** to comply without the third party's consent (taking into account: the duty of confidentiality owed to the third party; any steps taken to seek consent; whether the third party is capable of consenting; and whether the individual making the SAR is the person who provided the data)

**Always respond:** The controller must respond to the requester regardless of whether third-party data is disclosed. If declining to disclose third-party data, explain this to the requester.

**Document the decision:** Record what was decided, why, and on what basis — whether disclosing or withholding third-party information.

## Refusal — DPA 2018 Schedules 2 and 3 Exemptions

SARs have their own dedicated exemption regime under DPA 2018 Schedules 2–3. These are **distinct from the general UK GDPR Art. 17 exemptions** and apply specifically to subject access. An exemption permits the controller to withhold all or part of the requested information.

Exemptions must be assessed individually for each request and each category of data — no blanket application.

### Exemptions Most Relevant to Financial Services

| Exemption | When it applies | Notes |
|-----------|----------------|-------|
| **Crime and taxation: general** | Where disclosure would prejudice crime prevention/detection, apprehension/prosecution of offenders, or assessment/collection of taxes | Directly applicable to fraud investigations, AML/CTF internal records, tax assessments |
| **Crime and taxation: risk assessment** | Systems used to assess risk of crime or tax evasion | Relevant to fraud scoring and AML risk assessment models |
| **Legal professional privilege** | Legally privileged communications — solicitor advice, litigation correspondence | Protects legal advice sought/received by the controller |
| **Regulatory functions: other** | Data processed in connection with the exercise of regulatory functions | Potentially relevant to FCA-regulated firms' supervisory correspondence |
| **Management information** | Management forecasts, plans, intentions, or information about negotiations, where disclosure would or would be likely to prejudice the conduct of the controller's business | Protects business planning and commercial decision-making data |
| **Negotiations with the requester** | Information that relates to the controller's intentions in relation to negotiations with the requester | Protects negotiating position in active disputes or settlement discussions |
| **Confidential references** | References given or received in confidence (employment, educational, professional) | Protects employment references |

### Full Schedule 2/3 Exemption List

Crime and taxation (general); crime and taxation (risk assessment); legal professional privilege; functions designed to protect the public; regulatory functions relating to legal services/health/children's services; other regulatory functions; judicial appointments/independence/proceedings; journalism/academia/art/literature; research and statistics; archiving in the public interest; health, education and social work data; child abuse data; management information; negotiations with the requester; confidential references; exam scripts and exam marks; other exemptions.

### Refusal Procedure

Where refusing wholly or partly, the controller must notify the individual without undue delay and within 1 month:
- The reasons for not taking action
- The right to complain to the ICO or another supervisory authority
- The right to seek judicial enforcement

## Enforced SAR — Criminal Offence

An **enforced SAR** occurs when someone **requires an individual to make a SAR** in order to obtain certain information about them, then uses that information for their own purposes. Examples:
- Requiring a job applicant to SAR their criminal record or convictions as a condition of being considered for employment
- Requiring a prospective customer to SAR their health data before granting insurance cover
- Requiring an individual to SAR any other data as a condition of entering into a contract

**Forcing an individual to make a SAR in such circumstances is a criminal offence under the DPA 2018.**

The correct route to obtaining such information is via the individual's informed, freely given consent and a legitimate formal disclosure mechanism — not via an enforced SAR.

**Why this matters in financial services:**
- Pre-employment screening: insurers and financial firms that conduct criminal record checks cannot require candidates to self-SAR their records. The Disclosure and Barring Service (DBS) framework is the correct route.
- Underwriting: requiring health or other personal data via enforced SAR is unlawful. Underwriting questions must be answered by the individual directly as part of the application process.
- Credit: requiring a credit SAR as a condition of a loan application is similarly prohibited.

## Special Cases

Different rules apply in specific contexts:
- **Unstructured manual records:** Different search and retrieval obligations
- **Credit files:** Specific provisions under DPA 2018 — credit reference agencies have separate rules
- **Health data:** May require clinical professional involvement before disclosure; additional safeguards
- **Educational data:** School record provisions
- **Social work data:** Specific exemptions to protect vulnerable individuals

Detailed guidance for each special case is in ICO sub-page guidance (not yet ingested).

## Enforcement

**ICO enforcement:** Where a controller fails to comply with a SAR, the ICO may take regulatory action in accordance with its Regulatory Action Policy — including enforcement notices and fines.

**Court enforcement:** The requester may apply to court for an order requiring the controller to comply.

**Compensation:** The requester may seek compensation for damage suffered as a result of non-compliance.

## Key Points for Agents

- A SAR is valid verbally (including by phone), in writing, or via social media — no legal language, specific form, or named contact required. Any employee may receive a valid SAR. **(UK GDPR Art. 15)**
- **Response deadline: one calendar month** from receipt of the request. **(UK GDPR Art. 12(3))** The ICO recommends targeting 28 days to allow time for review. Extension to three months is available for complex or numerous requests, but the individual must be informed within the first month.
- **Third-party SARs:** The third party must provide evidence of their authority — the burden is on them. Do not respond before verifying authority.
- **Child SARs:** Competence-based — respond directly to the child if they understand their rights. Parent can only act with child's authorisation or where evidently in the child's best interests.
- **Clarification pause:** Where large volumes of data make the scope unclear, the clock can be paused (not merely extended) pending clarification. Supplementary information must still be provided within 1 month.
- **SAR response = copy + supplementary information.** The supplementary information is equivalent to Art. 13/14 privacy notice mandatory content.
- **Enforced SAR is a criminal offence** — requiring an individual to SAR their criminal record, health data, or any personal data as a condition of employment or contract is unlawful under DPA 2018.
- **DPA 2018 Schedule 2/3 exemptions** are SAR-specific. Most financially relevant: crime/taxation (fraud/AML), legal professional privilege, regulatory functions, management information, negotiations, confidential references.
- Third-party data: withhold if necessary to protect third-party privacy, but always respond to the requester and document the decision.
- Detailed sub-page guidance for each exemption, identity verification, search mechanics, delivery security, and special cases has not been ingested — consult ICO sub-page guidance for operational detail.

# SARs — Suspicion, the DAML Consent Regime, and Tipping Off

## Overview

Chapter 6 of JMLSG Part I covers the operational framework for identifying and reporting suspicious activity. It provides legal definitions of the suspicion threshold not found in FCG 3, explains the DAML consent procedure that applies when suspicion arises *before* a transaction, and sets out the two criminal offences of tipping off together with their permitted disclosure carve-outs.

These obligations sit alongside — not in place of — the SAR obligations covered in [[aml-ctf-framework]]. They provide the operational and legal detail that nominated officers and MLROs need to discharge those obligations without committing further offences.

## The Reporting Obligation — Who and When

Persons in the regulated sector must report to their nominated officer where they know, suspect, or have **reasonable grounds for knowing or suspecting** that a person is engaged in or attempting money laundering or terrorist financing. These three conditions are collectively the "grounds for knowledge or suspicion." **(POCA s.330; JMLSG §6.1)**

The nominated officer must evaluate each internal report, and — where grounds for knowledge or suspicion are established — must report to the NCA as soon as is reasonably practicable. **(POCA s.331; JMLSG §6.3)**

## Legal Definitions of "Knowledge" and "Suspicion"

### Knowledge (Subjective)
Having knowledge means **actually knowing something to be true**. In criminal proceedings, it must be proved that the individual in fact knew a person was engaged in money laundering. Knowledge can, however, be **inferred from surrounding circumstances**: failure to ask obvious questions may be relied upon by a jury to imply knowledge. The knowledge must have come to the person in the course of business. **(JMLSG §6.10)**

### Suspicion (Subjective)
Suspicion is more subjective and **falls short of proof based on firm evidence**. The courts have defined it as:

> "A degree of satisfaction and not necessarily amounting to belief but at least extending beyond speculation as to whether an event has occurred or not."

> "Although the creation of suspicion requires a lesser factual basis than the creation of a belief, it must nonetheless be built upon some foundation."

A transaction that appears unusual is not automatically suspicious. An unusual transaction is a basis for further enquiry, which may or may not result in a judgment that it is suspicious. A transaction may not be suspicious at the time but give rise to an obligation to report later if suspicions develop. **(JMLSG §6.11–6.12)**

In **K v NatWest [2006] EWCA Civ 1039**, the Court of Appeal confirmed: "The existence of suspicion is a subjective fact. There is no legal requirement that there should be reasonable grounds for the suspicion. The relevant bank employee either suspects or he does not. If he does suspect, he must (either himself or through the Bank's nominated officer) inform the authorities." **(JMLSG §6.69; K v NatWest [2006])**

A staff member reporting a suspicion is not required to know the exact nature of any underlying offence, or that funds are definitively criminal. **(JMLSG §6.13)**

### Reasonable Grounds to Know or Suspect (Objective)
In addition to the subjective knowledge and suspicion test, POCA and the Terrorism Act impose criminal liability for failing to disclose information where **reasonable grounds exist for knowing or suspecting** ML/TF — even if the individual did not personally suspect. This introduces an **objective test**. **(POCA s.330(2)(b); JMLSG §6.15)**

Relevant factors for the objective test include: the nature and origin of the transaction; how funds were discovered; amounts involved; intended destination; how the customer came into possession of funds; and any links to criminals, terrorist groups, or their sympathisers. To defend against a failure-to-report charge, staff must demonstrate they took reasonable steps to know the customer and understand the transaction rationale. **(JMLSG §6.15–6.16)**

## Internal Reporting — Staff Obligations

All relevant employees must report suspicions to the nominated officer (or their alternate) as soon as is reasonably practicable. The legal obligation lies with the individual staff member — they cannot allow colleagues to decide for them whether to report. Once reported to the nominated officer, the staff member's statutory obligation is fully satisfied. **(JMLSG §6.18–6.23)**

Firms may permit staff to consult a line manager before escalating to the nominated officer, but such systems must not prevent reports reaching the nominated officer when a staff member has stated they have knowledge or suspicion. Multiple staff members becoming aware of the same suspicion should result in only one report reaching the nominated officer. **(JMLSG §6.19–6.21)**

## The DAML Consent Regime

### When Consent Is Required

Reporting *before* and reporting *after* a transaction are not equivalent options. Where suspicion arises **before** a transaction, the nominated officer must not simply file a SAR — they must request **consent (a "defence against money laundering" — DAML)** from the NCA before proceeding. **(POCA s.336; JMLSG §6.46)**

The consent regime applies equally to terrorist financing under the Terrorism Act, with parallel time periods and procedures. **(Terrorism Act ss.21ZA–21ZB; JMLSG §6.51–6.55)**

### The 7-Day and 31-Day Periods

| Stage | Time period | What happens |
|-------|------------|--------------|
| DAML request submitted | Working day after submission | The 7-day notice period begins |
| **NCA must refuse within 7 working days** | Days 1–7 | If NCA does not refuse, the firm may proceed |
| NCA refuses | Day of refusal | **31-day moratorium begins** |
| Moratorium period | 31 calendar days | Authorities must obtain a restraint order within this period to prevent the transaction |
| Moratorium extension | Up to 186 additional days | Extendable in 31-day increments on application by authorities |

If the NCA does not refuse within 7 working days of the day after disclosure, the firm may process the transaction. If refused, the transaction cannot proceed unless a restraint order is not obtained within the moratorium period (including any extensions). **(POCA ss.335–336A; JMLSG §6.49–6.50)**

### How to Submit a DAML Request

Consent requests must be submitted electronically via SAR Online (ticking the consent request box) or, where electronic methods are unavailable, by fax — **never by post** due to timing constraints. Speed is critical: the firm should submit as soon as the suspicion is identified. **(JMLSG §6.41, 6.48)**

The UKFIU Consent Desk will conduct enquiries and contact the appropriate law enforcement agency for a recommendation. The firm will be informed of the decision by telephone and given a reference number; a formal letter follows. **(JMLSG §6.48)**

### After the Transaction Has Occurred

The consent mechanism is only available **prior to** the transaction or activity. Where the transaction has already occurred, the SAR is treated as an ordinary standard SAR — no consent is possible. The firm is free to operate the customer's account under normal commercial considerations until law enforcement determines otherwise through its investigation. **(JMLSG §6.56)**

### Automated Systems — When Consent May Not Be Possible

Where a suspicious transaction is already within an automated clearing or settlement system and delay would breach a contractual obligation or market settlement/clearing rules, the nominated officer may need to let it proceed and report afterwards. POCA provides a defence from making an advance report where there is a reasonable excuse — but this defence is untested by case law and must be considered case by case. **(JMLSG §6.47)**

### Deposit-Taker Threshold Following Disclosure

For **deposit-taking institutions only**: after a SAR has been filed on a customer or account, any subsequent transactions involving that customer or account may only proceed if the transaction is **£3,000 or less**. Where regular payments exceed £3,000, the deposit-taker may apply to the NCA for a **Threshold Variation** specifying the approved lifestyle payments, accounts, and amounts. **(POCA s.339A; JMLSG §6.73–6.74)**

## Tipping Off

### Two Criminal Offences

POCA and the Terrorism Act each contain **two separate tipping-off offences**. Both apply where the information came to the person in the course of a business in the regulated sector: **(POCA ss.333A–333D; Terrorism Act ss.21D–21G; JMLSG §6.60–6.62)**

**Offence 1 — Disclosing that a SAR has been made:**
Once an internal or external suspicion report has been made, it is a criminal offence for anyone to disclose information about that report if the disclosure is **likely to prejudice an investigation** that might follow. An offence is not committed if the person does not know or suspect that the disclosure is likely to prejudice the investigation. **(POCA s.333A(1); JMLSG §6.61)**

**Offence 2 — Disclosing that an investigation is underway:**
Where a money laundering investigation is being contemplated or carried out, it is a criminal offence to disclose this if the disclosure is **likely to prejudice that investigation**. **(POCA s.333A(3); JMLSG §6.62)**

### Practical Consequences

A firm subject to the tipping-off rules: **(JMLSG §6.68)**
- **Cannot** tell a customer that their transaction is being delayed because a DAML request is awaiting NCA consent
- **Cannot** later tell the customer that a transaction was delayed because a report had been made — unless law enforcement/NCA agrees or a court order permits disclosure
- **Cannot** tell the customer that law enforcement is investigating

This can create acute customer service difficulties. Where the customer complains to the FOS, the FOS has specialist AML-trained teams and a dedicated secure email for firms to communicate the SAR position confidentially. **(JMLSG §6.70–6.71)**

### Permitted Disclosures — Exceptions to Tipping Off

The following disclosures are expressly permitted and do not constitute a tipping-off offence: **(POCA ss.333B–333D; JMLSG §6.63–6.66)**

| Type | Conditions |
|------|-----------|
| **Disclosure to supervisor/regulator** | Made to the FCA (or other relevant supervisor) for detection, investigation or prosecution of a criminal offence, POCA investigation, or enforcement of a court order **(POCA s.333D(1))** |
| **Intra-firm disclosure** | Disclosure by an employee, officer or partner to another employee, officer or partner of the same firm **(POCA s.333B(1))** |
| **Intra-group disclosure** | Disclosure within the same group to a credit institution or financial institution situated in an EEA State or country imposing equivalent AML requirements **(POCA s.333B(2))** |
| **Inter-institution disclosure** | Disclosure from one credit institution or financial institution to another, where: (i) relates to a common customer/former customer, or common transaction/service; (ii) purpose is solely preventing a POCA Part 7 or Terrorism Act Part III offence; (iii) recipient is in an EEA state or equivalent country; (iv) both are subject to equivalent data protection duties **(POCA s.333C)** |

Note: reasonable enquiries of a customer regarding the background to an unusual transaction, conducted tactfully, form part of CDD and **should not give rise to the tipping-off offence**. **(JMLSG §6.61)**

## Nominated Officer — Evaluation Duty and Documentation

The nominated officer must evaluate each internal report and document:
- The decision on whether to make a SAR — including, where no SAR is made, the reasons for not doing so **(JMLSG §6.32)**
- The rationale for any conscious decision not to report **(JMLSG §6.58)**

The firm must permit the nominated officer access to all information in the firm's possession (including KYC information) that could be relevant. The nominated officer may seek further information from the customer or an intermediary, but any approach should be made sensitively and not by the nominated officer themselves, to minimise tipping-off risk. **(JMLSG §6.29)**

## Key Points for Agents

- "Suspicion" is a subjective test — if the employee suspects, they must report. There is no legal requirement for reasonable grounds to support a subjective suspicion (K v NatWest [2006]).
- "Reasonable grounds to know or suspect" introduces a separate objective test. Staff cannot escape criminal liability simply by not personally forming a suspicion if objective circumstances warranted one.
- When suspicion arises **before** a transaction, the nominated officer must file a DAML consent request — not just a SAR. NCA has 7 working days to refuse. If not refused, the transaction may proceed. If refused, a 31-day moratorium applies (extendable to 186 days).
- Filing a SAR **after** the transaction provides no consent protection — consent is only available pre-event.
- Tipping off has two variants: disclosing that a SAR was made; disclosing that an investigation is underway. Both are criminal if likely to prejudice an investigation.
- Permitted disclosures (which are not tipping off): within the same firm; within the same group to EEA/equivalent institutions; between two institutions sharing a common customer or transaction.
- Deposit-takers face a £3,000 per-transaction threshold on all subsequent activity in a customer account where a SAR has been filed, unless a Threshold Variation is obtained from the NCA.

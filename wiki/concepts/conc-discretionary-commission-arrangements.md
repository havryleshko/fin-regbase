# CONC — Discretionary Commission Arrangements (DCA Prohibition)

## What This Covers

CONC 4.5 governs commission arrangements between lenders and credit brokers. It imposes disclosure obligations on credit brokers and contains a hard prohibition — CONC 4.5.6R — on **discretionary commission arrangements (DCAs)**. The prohibition is of direct relevance to the FCA's motor finance commission review and to any regulated credit market in which brokers have historically had discretion to set interest rates.

---

## Commission Disclosure Obligations

### CONC 4.5.3R — Prominent Disclosure Where Commission Could Affect Impartiality

A credit broker must **prominently disclose** to the customer, in good time before a credit or consumer hire agreement is entered into, the existence and nature of any commission or fee or other remuneration payable to the broker by the lender, owner, or a third party, where the existence or amount of that commission could **actually or potentially**: **(CONC 4.5.3R)**

1. Affect the impartiality of the broker in recommending the credit or hire agreement; or
2. If made known to the customer, have a **material impact** on the customer's transactional decision to enter into the agreement.

### CONC 4.5.3AR — How Commission Affects Customer Costs

Where disclosure under CONC 4.5.3R is required, the firm must simultaneously and with **equal prominence** also disclose **how the existence and nature of the commission may affect the amounts payable by the customer** under the credit or hire agreement. **(CONC 4.5.3AR)**

This is not an obligation to provide an individually tailored illustration — general disclosure of the mechanism by which the commission affects cost is sufficient, provided it enables the customer to reasonably appreciate the effect. **(CONC 4.5.3B G(3)–(4))**

Where commission varies depending on which agreement the customer enters into (e.g. the broker has arrangements with multiple lenders and different commission rates apply to different products), the broker should disclose in relation to each of those arrangements. **(CONC 4.5.3BG(2))**

### CONC 4.5.4R — On-Request Amount Disclosure

At the **customer's request**, a credit broker must disclose, in good time before the agreement is entered into, the amount (or if the precise amount is not known, the likely amount) of any commission, fee or other remuneration payable by the lender, owner, or a third party. **(CONC 4.5.4R)**

---

## CONC 4.5.6R — The DCA Prohibition

**Hard rule:** A lender or credit broker **must not**:
1. Enter into a discretionary commission arrangement; or
2. Seek to exercise, enforce or rely on rights or obligations under a discretionary commission arrangement — including any rights to receive or tender payment of commission, fee or other financial consideration. **(CONC 4.5.6R)**

This is a prohibition on both sides: the lender must not offer DCAs and the broker must not hold or exercise rights under them.

---

## What Is a Discretionary Commission Arrangement?

A discretionary commission arrangement is an arrangement under which the commission payable by the lender to the credit broker **varies with the interest rate charged to the customer under a regulated credit agreement** entered into by the lender — and the broker has authority to **decide or negotiate** that interest rate. **(CONC 4.5.5G)**

CONC 4.5.7G names three types:

| Type | Structure | Common name |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Type 1** | Lender sets a **minimum** rate. Commission = difference between the rate the broker negotiates and that minimum. Broker earns more commission by setting higher rates. | "Increasing difference in charges" / "interest rate upward adjustment" (IUA) |
| **Type 2** | Lender sets a **maximum** rate. Commission = difference between the maximum rate and the rate negotiated by the broker. Broker earns more commission by setting rates closer to the maximum. | "Decreasing difference in charges" / "interest rate downward adjustment" (IDA) |
| **Type 3** | Commission varies (within set parameters) according to the interest rate negotiated by the broker. | "Scaled models" |

**The harm mechanism** in all three types is the same: the broker's own financial interest (higher commission) is aligned with charging the customer a higher rate — directly compromising the broker's impartiality and creating a structural incentive for customer detriment.

---

## Accrued Commissions — CONC 4.5.8G

The prohibition in CONC 4.5.6R does not apply retrospectively to commissions that **accrued before** the date on which CONC 4.5.6R came into force. **(CONC 4.5.8G(1))**

However, CONC 4.5.6R **does** apply to commissions that **became due on or after** the date the rule came into force — irrespective of whether the underlying discretionary commission arrangement was entered into before or after that date. **(CONC 4.5.8G(1)–(3))**

In practice:
- DCA arrangements entered into before the prohibition date, relating to regulated credit agreements also entered into before that date: commissions accruing from those agreements are not affected.
- DCA arrangements entered into before the prohibition date, relating to regulated credit agreements entered into **on or after** the prohibition date: commissions accruing from those post-date agreements **are** affected by CONC 4.5.6R.

---

## Motor Finance Context

CONC 4.5.5G–4.5.8G explicitly state that the purpose of the prohibition is to prevent credit brokers and lenders from making or relying on arrangements under which credit brokers are given authority to decide or negotiate the prices of credit agreements to finance motor vehicle acquisition, where the commission the brokers earn is affected by those prices. **(CONC 4.5.5G)**

The FCA's motor finance review (initiated 2024) focuses on the historical use of DCA-type arrangements in the motor finance market — principally IUA and IDA structures under which car dealers acting as credit brokers had discretion to set rates above a minimum "buy rate," earning additional commission from the spread. CONC 4.5.6R is the sourcebook basis for the regulatory position that such arrangements were impermissible and may have caused customer harm.

---

## Key Points for Agents

- CONC 4.5.6R is a hard prohibition — lenders must not offer DCAs and brokers must not hold or exercise rights under them.
- Three named DCA types: interest rate upward adjustment (IUA), interest rate downward adjustment (IDA), and scaled models. All share the same harm mechanism: broker commission varies with the rate charged to the customer, and the broker has discretion to set that rate.
- Commission that accrued before CONC 4.5.6R came into force is not retroactively caught. But commissions accruing on or after the effective date are caught, even under arrangements entered into before the prohibition.
- CONC 4.5.3R requires prominent disclosure of commission where it could affect broker impartiality or have material impact on the customer's decision. CONC 4.5.3AR requires simultaneous disclosure of how that commission affects amounts payable by the customer.
- On request (CONC 4.5.4R), the broker must disclose the amount or likely amount of commission.

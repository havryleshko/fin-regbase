# CONC — Creditworthiness Assessment

## What This Covers

The creditworthiness assessment obligation is the central responsible lending requirement in CONC. It applies to consumer credit lenders (CONC 5.2A) and P2P platform operators (CONC 5.5A), with a lighter suitability-like obligation on credit brokers (CONC 5.4). This page covers all three, with a dedicated P2P section.

## When Assessment Is Required — Three Hard Triggers

A firm must assess creditworthiness before **(CONC 5.2A.4R)**:

| Trigger | Notes |
|---|---|
| Entering into a regulated credit agreement | Applies to every new agreement in scope |
| Significantly increasing the amount of credit | Applies mid-agreement |
| Significantly increasing a credit limit (running-account) | Applies to credit cards, overdrafts, revolving facilities |

CONC 5.2A.5R is a hard gate: the firm **cannot proceed** unless it can demonstrate it has completed the assessment and had proper regard to its outcome on affordability risk. This is a pre-condition, not a best-efforts standard.

**Cumulative increase trap (CONC 5.2A.6G):** Individual credit limit increases that are each below the "significant" threshold may aggregate — since the last assessment — into a total that is significant. When that threshold is crossed cumulatively, a fresh assessment is required. A pattern of small monthly limit increases can trigger this.

## The Two-Track Assessment

The assessment must address two legally distinct risks **(CONC 5.2A.10R)**:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  CREDITWORTHINESS ASSESSMENT                        │
├──────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────┤
│  Credit risk         │  Affordability risk           │
│  Will the customer   │  Can the customer repay       │
│  miss payments?      │  without harm to themselves?  │
│  (CONC 5.2A.10R(1)) │  (CONC 5.2A.10R(2))          │
└──────────────────────┴──────────────────────────────┘
```

Passing credit risk scoring does not satisfy affordability risk. Both tracks are mandatory and must be assessed independently.

A single missed repayment may be relevant to creditworthiness even if not conclusive **(CONC 5.2A.11G)**.

## The Affordability Test — Five Conditions

The firm must consider the customer's ability to make repayments **(CONC 5.2A.12R)**:

1. As they fall due **over the life of the agreement** (or reasonable period for open-end agreements)
2. Out of income or savings/assets the customer has clearly indicated they will use
3. **Without having to borrow** to meet the repayments
4. **Without failing** to meet other contractual or statutory obligations
5. **Without significant adverse impact** on the customer's financial situation

All five conditions must be met. A customer who can only meet repayments by taking on more debt, or by defaulting on utility bills or other credit agreements, fails the affordability test even if they have a good credit score.

**Savings and assets (CONC 5.2A.13R):** If the customer intends to repay from savings or assets, the firm must additionally assess: the purpose those assets are held for, the likelihood of their availability, and the impact on the customer's financial position of using them.

## Security Cannot Offset Affordability Risk

**CONC 5.2A.14R** is a hard prohibition: when assessing affordability risk for the borrower, the firm must not take into account the existence of any guarantee, indemnity, or other form of security.

A guarantor arrangement does not reduce the borrower affordability assessment. The guarantor is assessed separately (see Guarantors section below). This prevents a firm from approving an unaffordable loan to a borrower on the basis that a guarantor exists.

## Income Assessment

Unless repayment is obviously achievable (making income determination disproportionate) or the customer is repaying solely from savings/assets, the firm must **(CONC 5.2A.15R)**:

- Determine or estimate the customer's **current income**
- Where a foreseeable income reduction could materially affect affordability, **estimate that reduction**
- Only account for expected future income increases where there is appropriate evidence they are likely to occur

**Self-declaration is not sufficient (CONC 5.2A.16G(3)):** Relying solely on a customer's statement of income without independent corroboration is not generally acceptable. Independent evidence includes credit reference agency data or third-party documentation. The "obvious repayment" exception exists but the burden to demonstrate it falls on the firm.

Income sources are broad — salary, wages, benefits, savings income, jointly held income where reasonably expected to be available. Estimates can use ranges or minimums if the assumptions are reasonable **(CONC 5.2A.16G(2), (5))**.

## Expenditure Assessment

The firm must determine or estimate the customer's **non-discretionary expenditure** **(CONC 5.2A.17R)**, unless:
- The obvious repayment exception applies (burden on firm to demonstrate), or
- Non-discretionary expenditure is unlikely to have a material impact on affordability risk

Non-discretionary expenditure includes **(CONC 5.2A.18G)**:
- Priority debt payments (rent, mortgage, council tax)
- Essential living expenses (utilities, food, basic quality-of-life costs)
- Contractual or statutory obligations (other credit agreements, statutory payments)
- Financial obligations of others whose financial obligations the customer meets

**Statistical data (CONC 5.2A.19G):** Statistical expenditure benchmarks may be used unless the firm knows or has reasonable cause to suspect the customer's situation differs significantly — for example, different household size, number of dependants, or existing debt level.

## Proportionality — Assessment Scope Scaled to Risk

The depth of the assessment is proportionate to the individual circumstances **(CONC 5.2A.20R)**. The firm must determine:
- What types of information to use
- Content and level of detail required
- Whether information already held is sufficient
- Whether to obtain additional information from the customer or a credit reference agency
- Whether and how to verify accuracy
- The degree of evaluation and analysis required

**Factors that drive required rigour (CONC 5.2A.20R(3)):** credit type, amount, credit limit, duration, repayment frequency and amounts, total amount payable, total charge for credit, APR, fixed vs. variable rates, and potential adverse consequences of default.

Higher credit amounts, longer durations, and higher total costs require more rigorous assessment. Lower-cost, lower-value credit may require less — but the framework must still be applied **(CONC 5.2A.24G)**.

**High affordability risk indicators (CONC 5.2A.25G):** outstanding debt high relative to income; high likelihood of missed payments. A single high-risk payment being likely missed may itself indicate high affordability risk.

The firm should also consider **(CONC 5.2A.22G)**:
- Whether CONC 1.3 financial difficulty indicators are present — see [[conc-financial-difficulty-indicators]]
- Whether vulnerability indicators are present — mental health, mental capacity limitations (CONC 2.10, CONC 7.2) — see [[vulnerable-customers]]

Previous dealings with the customer may inform the assessment but the firm must consider whether the passage of time has affected their validity **(CONC 5.2A.23G)**.

## Running-Account Credit — Mandatory Worst-Case Assumption

For credit cards, overdrafts, and revolving facilities, the firm cannot assess on expected or average usage. It must apply a mandatory worst-case assumption **(CONC 5.2A.27R)**:

- **New agreement:** Assume the customer draws the entire credit limit at the earliest opportunity and repays by equal instalments over a reasonable period.
- **Credit limit increase:** Assume the customer draws the entire available balance up to the new limit at the earliest opportunity and repays by equal instalments.

**Benchmark for "reasonable period" (CONC 5.2A.28G):** The typical repayment period for a fixed-sum unsecured personal loan of the same amount — taking into account loan terms likely available to that customer. This means a credit card limit of £5,000 should be stress-tested against the repayment period a £5,000 personal loan would require.

If individual customer circumstances justify a different assumption, the firm may depart from the benchmark — but must document the reasoning.

## Open-End Agreements

For open-end agreements, the firm must make a reasonable assumption about likely duration **(CONC 5.2A.26R)**, taking into account: agreement terms, pre-contractual disclosures, and the customer's known intentions.

## Guarantors

Where a guarantee or indemnity is provided in connection with a regulated credit agreement **(CONC 5.2A.31R)**:
- The firm must assess the guarantor's ability to meet their potential obligations under the guarantee
- This is required before entering into the agreement or significantly increasing credit/limit
- The assessment is based on sufficient information — from the guarantor directly, the borrower on the guarantor's behalf, or a credit reference agency

The guarantor assessment does not need to be identical to the borrower assessment but must be sufficient in depth and scope given the potential obligations **(CONC 5.2A.32G)**. The same cumulative increase rule applies to guarantors.

**The guarantee does not remove the borrower affordability obligation.** CONC 5.2A.14R (security exclusion) means the guarantor's existence cannot be used to justify lending to a borrower who fails affordability.

## Joint Borrowers

The firm should consider whether to assess each borrower separately as well as jointly, having regard to the risk to each borrower if they became solely liable **(CONC 5.2A.30G)**. Where a borrower is a partnership or unincorporated association acting through agents, the members may be treated as a single customer.

## Policies, Procedures and Governance

A firm must **(CONC 5.2A.33R)**:

| Obligation | Detail |
|---|---|
| Written policies | Establish and maintain clear written creditworthiness assessment policies and procedures |
| Governing body approval | Have policies approved by the governing body or senior personnel (not sole traders) |
| Periodic review | Assess and periodically review effectiveness and compliance |
| Remediation | Take appropriate measures to address deficiencies found |
| Transaction records | Maintain a record of every qualifying transaction sufficient to demonstrate assessment was carried out and was reasonable |
| Governance | Establish robust governance and internal controls (not sole traders) |

Record-keeping obligations align with SYSC 9.1.4G and 9.1.5G. The transaction record requirement is an FCA audit target — it must be sufficient to enable the FCA to monitor compliance.

This is a governing-body-level obligation. Under SM&CR, accountability for creditworthiness policy sits with the relevant Senior Manager **(see [[smcr-senior-managers-regime]])**.

## Unfair Business Practices

Two hard prohibitions **(CONC 5.2A.35R–5.2A.36R)**:
- A firm must not complete application sections intended for the customer without consent or without the customer having had the opportunity to check
- A firm must not accept a credit application where it knows or has reasonable cause to suspect the customer has been untruthful about information relevant to the creditworthiness assessment

A clear inconsistency between customer-declared income/employment and other information the firm holds constitutes reasonable cause to suspect untruthfulness **(CONC 5.2A.37G)**.

## Credit Brokers — Suitability-Like Obligation (CONC 5.4)

Credit brokers do not carry out a full creditworthiness assessment (that is the lender's obligation) but have a conduct obligation **(CONC 5.4.2R)**:
- Pay due regard to the **customer's needs and circumstances** when giving explanations, advice, or making recommendations
- Pay due regard to whether the credit product is **affordable** and whether any factors the firm knows or ought to know would make the product **unsuitable** for that customer

A broker that represents it will search the market (or part of it) must carry out that search to the stated extent **(CONC 5.4.3R)**.

This is an intermediate standard — less than a full assessment obligation, more than mere product disclosure.

## P2P Platforms — Dual Assessment Obligation (CONC 5.5A)

### Structure

CONC 5.5A applies to P2P platform operators. CONC 5.2A applies to the individual lender under the P2P agreement. **Both have separate, independent creditworthiness assessment obligations (CONC 5.5A.3G).** Neither discharges the other's obligation. SYSC 8 outsourcing rules apply but the outsourcing firm remains fully responsible.

### Material Difference from CONC 5.2A

The assessment framework in CONC 5.5A mirrors 5.2A in almost every respect. The one material structural difference is in unfair business practices **(CONC 5.5A.37R)**:

- Under CONC 5.2A.36R: a lender must **not accept** an application where it knows or suspects untruthfulness.
- Under CONC 5.5A.37R: a platform operator must **inform the prospective lender** where it knows or suspects the borrower has been untruthful about information relevant to the creditworthiness assessment.

This is an active disclosure obligation to the lender — the platform cannot simply decline the application without notification. The lender then determines whether to proceed.

### P2P Running-Account Credit

The worst-case drawdown assumption applies to P2P running-account credit **(CONC 5.5A.28R)**: assume full drawdown at earliest opportunity, repaid over a period equivalent to a personal loan of the same amount. For credit limit increases, the platform must take reasonable steps to procure the lender to set the credit limit in line with the assumptions.

## Key Points for Agents

- Assessment is required at three hard gates and cannot be bypassed. Cumulative credit limit increases can trigger a reassessment even if no single increase was significant.
- Two tracks must be assessed: credit risk and affordability risk. Passing one does not satisfy the other.
- The affordability test has five conditions — the customer must be able to repay without borrowing, without defaulting on other obligations, and without significant financial harm.
- Self-declared income is not sufficient. Independent corroboration is required unless repayment is obviously achievable, and the burden of demonstrating that exception falls on the firm.
- Security, guarantees, and indemnities cannot offset affordability risk in the borrower assessment. Guarantor assessment is additional, not a substitute.
- For running-account credit (credit cards, overdrafts), the firm must assume full drawdown repaid over a personal-loan-equivalent period — not typical or expected usage.
- Creditworthiness policies must be in writing, governing-body approved, reviewed periodically, and every qualifying transaction must be recorded.
- Under SM&CR, the Senior Manager allocated responsibility for creditworthiness in the firm's Statement of Responsibilities is personally accountable for policy compliance. The Duty of Responsibility (FSMA s.66A) applies — the SM must demonstrate reasonable steps to avoid personal liability for policy failures (CONC 5.2A.33R).
- In P2P: both platform operator and lender have separate obligations. The platform must affirmatively disclose suspected borrower untruthfulness to the prospective lender.

# SM&CR — Conduct Rules

## What Conduct Rules Are

The Conduct Rules (set out in COCON) are rules of professional conduct that apply to individuals working in FCA-regulated firms, not just to the firm as an entity. They are enforceable by the FCA directly against individuals. Breach of a Conduct Rule can result in personal disciplinary action by the FCA, regardless of whether the firm itself is also sanctioned.

Two tiers exist:
- **Individual Conduct Rules (1–5 + 6):** Apply to all employees of the firm except ancillary staff
- **Senior Manager Conduct Rules (SC1–SC4):** Additional rules applying only to Senior Managers (SMF holders)

---

## Who Is Subject to Conduct Rules

**In scope:**
- All **employees** of the firm who are not ancillary staff
- This includes: Senior Managers, Certification Function holders, and all other employees (including those whose roles fall entirely outside the Certification Regime)
- Employees seconded to the firm from group entities
- The term "employee" under FSMA includes persons who personally provide services under an arrangement where they are subject to the firm's supervision, direction or control

**Out of scope:**
- **Ancillary staff** — employees whose work is unrelated to financial services activity. Examples: cleaners, caterers, security guards, receptionists performing purely administrative roles unconnected to the firm's regulated activities.

## Individual Conduct Rules (Rules 1–5)

| Rule | Obligation |
|------|-----------|
| **Rule 1** | You must act with integrity |
| **Rule 2** | You must act with due care, skill and diligence |
| **Rule 3** | You must be open and cooperative with the FCA, the PRA and other regulators |
| **Rule 4** | You must pay due regard to the interests of customers and treat them fairly |
| **Rule 5** | You must observe proper standards of market conduct |

These rules reflect baseline standards of conduct — they are purposely high-level to allow the FCA to apply them across the diverse range of roles in financial services.

### Individual Conduct Rule 6 — Consumer Duty Addition

**Rule 6** was added following the FCA's Consumer Duty (PS22/9, July 2022, §§13.16–13.22), after publication of the 2019 guide:

> You must act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers

Rule 6 applies where an individual carries out activities within the scope of the Consumer Duty. It makes Consumer Duty compliance a **personal obligation on individuals**, not merely a firm-level obligation. Senior managers are accountable under Rule 6 for good consumer outcomes in their areas of responsibility.

## Senior Manager Conduct Rules (SC1–SC4)

Senior Managers are subject to Individual Conduct Rules 1–6 **plus** four additional Senior Manager Conduct Rules:

| Rule | Obligation |
|------|-----------|
| **SC1** | You must take reasonable steps to ensure that the business of the firm for which you are responsible is organised so that it can be controlled and monitored effectively |
| **SC2** | You must take reasonable steps to ensure that the business of the firm for which you are responsible complies with the relevant requirements and standards of the regulatory system |
| **SC3** | You must take reasonable steps to ensure that any delegation of your responsibilities is to an appropriate person and that you oversee the discharge of the delegated responsibility effectively |
| **SC4** | You must disclose appropriately any information of which the FCA or PRA would reasonably expect notice |

### What the Senior Manager Rules Require in Practice

**SC1** focuses on organisational structure — a Senior Manager cannot discharge their accountability by pointing to an organisational structure that makes effective monitoring impossible. Controls and oversight must be designed to function in practice, not just on paper.

**SC2** requires the SM to take active steps to ensure their area complies — not merely to have policies in place. A failure of compliance in their area, without evidence of reasonable preventative steps, may be both a breach of SC2 and trigger the Duty of Responsibility (FSMA s.66A).

**SC3** governs delegation specifically. Delegation does not transfer accountability. An SM who delegates to an unsuitable person, or who fails to maintain adequate oversight of delegated responsibilities, may breach SC3 even if the delegate carries out the function in good faith.

**SC4** is a proactive disclosure obligation. An SM who becomes aware of information the FCA or PRA would reasonably expect to be told about — and fails to disclose it — breaches SC4. This includes information about their own conduct, firm conduct in their area, and material adverse developments the regulators would want to know about.

## Conduct Rule Breach Reporting

The reporting regime for conduct rule breaches differs depending on whether the breach involves a Senior Manager.

| Breach by | Reporting obligation | Deadline |
|-----------|---------------------|----------|
| Senior Manager (SMF holder) | Notify the FCA directly | **Within 7 business days** |
| Any other employee (Certification staff, other staff in scope) | Annual report to the FCA via REP008 on GABRIEL | At the end of the annual reporting period |

### The 7-Day SM Reporting Obligation

Where a firm concludes that a Senior Manager has breached a Conduct Rule, it must notify the FCA within 7 business days of that conclusion. The obligation is triggered by the **firm's conclusion** — not the discovery of the underlying conduct. Firms should be careful not to delay reaching a conclusion where evidence is clear.

### REP008 Annual Reporting

For all other conduct rule breaches (Certification Function holders and all other employees in scope), firms report annually using REP008 submitted via GABRIEL. The report covers:
- The number of conduct rule breach findings in the period
- The nature of the breaches
- Actions taken

This aggregated annual reporting model means that individual non-SM conduct rule breaches do not trigger per-event FCA notification — but patterns may be scrutinised during supervision.

## Interaction with Other Obligations

### Fitness and Propriety

A conduct rule breach is a material input into any F&P reassessment. For Certification Function holders, it will be considered at annual recertification. For Senior Managers, it may trigger an FCA fitness review and potential withdrawal of approval.

### Duty of Responsibility

A Senior Manager's breach of SC1 or SC2 will frequently be considered alongside the Duty of Responsibility (FSMA s.66A) if the same conduct contributed to a firm-level contravention. The two frameworks operate in parallel and are cumulative.

### Consumer Duty (Rule 6)

Rule 6 makes Consumer Duty compliance a personal obligation on individuals, in addition to the firm-level obligations under PRIN 2A. Firms should ensure that Conduct Rules training explicitly covers Rule 6 and its scope.

### AML/CTF

PR(b-1) under the Core PRs requires a named Senior Manager to take responsibility for conduct rule training and notifications. Training must cover the Conduct Rules in full — including Rule 6 where the firm is within scope of the Consumer Duty.

## Key Points for Agents

- Conduct Rules apply to all employees **except ancillary staff** (cleaners, caterers, security, non-financial administrative roles). The cut-off is whether the role relates to financial services activity.
- Two tiers: Individual Rules 1–6 (all in-scope employees) + Senior Manager Rules SC1–SC4 (SMF holders only).
- Individual Rule 6 (act to deliver good outcomes for retail customers) was added by the Consumer Duty. It makes Consumer Duty a personal obligation on individuals, not just firms.
- SC3 makes clear that **delegation does not transfer accountability**. An SM who delegates to an unsuitable person, or fails to oversee effectively, may breach SC3.
- SC4 is a proactive disclosure obligation to the FCA/PRA — the SM must disclose information the regulators would reasonably expect to be told about, without waiting to be asked.
- **Breach reporting is asymmetric:** SM breaches must be notified to the FCA within 7 business days. All other in-scope staff breaches are reported annually via REP008 on GABRIEL.
- The 7-day clock for SM breaches starts when the **firm concludes** a breach occurred, not when it first identifies the concern. Firms should avoid prolonging the conclusion phase where evidence is clear.

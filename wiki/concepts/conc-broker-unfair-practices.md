# CONC — Credit Broker Unfair Practices

## What This Covers

CONC 2.5 sets out the conduct requirements for credit brokers and contains a detailed catalogue of prohibited unfair practices at CONC 2.5.8R. This page covers both the positive obligations on credit brokers (CONC 2.5.3R) and the specific unfair practices prohibition catalogue. Related prohibitions in CONC 4.4 (broker fee disclosure) and CONC 4.5 (commission disclosure and DCA prohibition) are cross-referenced but treated in full on their respective pages.

**Credit broker** in this context means a firm carrying on the regulated activity of credit broking — typically arranging or facilitating introductions between customers and lenders, or acting as an intermediary.

---

## Positive Conduct Obligations — CONC 2.5.3R

A credit broker must **(CONC 2.5.3R)**:
- Act honestly, fairly, and professionally in accordance with the best interests of customers
- Not exert undue pressure on or exercise undue influence over a customer
- Explain the nature of its credit broking services to customers and the basis on which it operates (e.g. whether it operates from the whole market or from a panel)
- Have appropriate regard to the customer's financial circumstances when considering what products or services to introduce them to
- Take reasonable steps to ensure that any credit product it introduces a customer to is appropriate for the customer's needs and circumstances

## Unfair Business Practices Catalogue — CONC 2.5.8R

CONC 2.5.8R prohibits credit brokers from engaging in a wide range of unfair practices. The named prohibitions include:

### Contact and Communication Prohibitions

- **TPS and automated calls:** Contacting a customer who is registered with the Telephone Preference Service, or making unsolicited automated marketing calls, without the customer having consented to receive such contact.
- **Inconvenient contact:** Contacting a customer at unreasonable times or in a way that causes unnecessary distress or embarrassment.
- **Premium rate complaint numbers:** Using or directing customers to premium rate telephone numbers for the purpose of dealing with complaints or queries, or conducting such calls in a way that causes the call to exceed 15 minutes on a premium rate line.

### Conflicts of Interest — Securing More Disadvantageous Terms for Own Gain

- **Securing more credit than needed:** Securing a higher amount of credit for a customer than the customer needs or has requested, where the primary motivation is the broker's own financial gain from the higher amount.
- **Securing a worse interest rate:** Arranging credit on a rate of interest that is less favourable to the customer than the customer could obtain, where the broker does so to generate more commission — the core harm mechanism of discretionary commission arrangements (see **[[conc-discretionary-commission-arrangements]]**).
- **Preferred lender for broker's benefit:** Introducing a customer to a particular lender — or promoting that lender's products — primarily because of the financial benefit to the broker rather than because the lender's product is suitable for the customer.

### Add-On and Product Pressuring

- **Add-on insurance:** Pressurising customers into taking add-on insurance or other ancillary products that the customer has not requested and may not need.
- **Against-interest consolidation:** Encouraging a customer to consolidate existing debts in circumstances where consolidation is not in the customer's financial interests — for example, where consolidation would result in higher overall cost or extend the repayment period significantly to the customer's detriment.

### Harmful Credit Extension

- **Unsustainable refinancing:** Arranging or facilitating the refinancing of existing credit agreements where the refinancing is not in the customer's interests and may trap the customer in a cycle of debt.
- **Harmful credit extension:** Extending, or arranging the extension of, credit in a way that is harmful to the customer — including where the customer is clearly in financial difficulty or the credit will make their situation worse.

### Fee and Payment Account Conduct

- **Wrong-type introduction fee:** Charging a customer a fee for a service that is characterised as an introduction or credit broking fee, when the actual service provided is debt adjusting, without the customer's informed consent to that characterisation and fee.
- **Payment account access without authorisation:** Accessing a customer's payment account to take a fee or charge without having first obtained the customer's express authority to do so.

### Data and Referral Conduct

- **Passing data to unauthorised firms:** Passing customer personal data — including contact details and financial information provided in the course of a credit application — to third parties who are not authorised or entitled to receive that data, or who will use it for purposes the customer has not consented to.

## HCSTC Price Comparison Websites — CONC 2.5A

Firms operating price comparison websites for high-cost short-term credit (HCSTC) are subject to specific conduct rules designed to prevent commercial conflicts of interest distorting search results:

| Obligation | Rule |
|---|---|
| Rank by total amount payable — not by commercial interests | CONC 2.5A.2R |
| Enable search by credit amount and duration | CONC 2.5A.3R |
| List all covered lenders (or clearly disclose basis of coverage) | CONC 2.5A.4R |
| Present search results and financial promotions in clearly separated sections | CONC 2.5A.5R |

These rules prevent commercial arrangements (e.g. lenders paying for prominence) from corrupting the ranked results that customers rely on to compare products fairly.

## Related CONC 4 Broker Obligations

Two additional CONC 4 obligations apply specifically to credit brokers:

**Broker fee disclosure (CONC 4.4.2R–4.4.3R):** Before a customer is committed to any credit broking arrangement, the broker must provide a written information notice explaining the fee it charges (or will charge) and the basis on which it operates. The customer must confirm, in writing, that they have received that information. The customer must be able to access the broker's payment page only after providing that confirmation. Each broker in a chain must issue its own notice.

**Commission disclosure (CONC 4.5.3R–4.5.3AR):** Where commission or remuneration payable to the broker could affect its impartiality or have a material impact on the customer's transactional decision, the broker must prominently disclose the existence and nature of that commission — and simultaneously disclose how it may affect the amounts payable by the customer. See **[[conc-discretionary-commission-arrangements]]**.

## Key Points for Agents

- The CONC 2.5.8R unfair practices catalogue contains hard prohibitions — not aspirational standards. Breach is a rule breach, not merely poor practice.
- The most operationally significant prohibitions for credit brokers: (1) securing worse rates or more credit than needed for commission benefit; (2) accessing payment accounts without express authorisation; (3) passing data to unauthorised third parties.
- HCSTC PCW operators may not rank by commercial interests. Lenders cannot buy their way into the ranked results section. Separation of results and promotions is mandatory.
- Pressurising customers into add-on insurance or consolidation loans against their interests are both named unfair practices. Firms should document why any recommendation is in the customer's interest.
- Credit brokers face both CONC 2.5 (conduct) and CONC 4.4/4.5 (fee and commission disclosure) obligations — these are additive, not alternatives.

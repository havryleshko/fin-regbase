# FCA Consumer Duty — Sludge and Positive Friction

## Overview

The Consumer Duty requires firms to distinguish between friction in customer journeys that harms customers and friction that protects them. FG22/5 introduces specific terminology for this distinction: **sludge** (harmful friction) and **positive friction** (beneficial friction). Neither term appears in PS22/9 or the rules themselves — they are operational guidance concepts. **(FG22/5 §9.20)**

## Sludge — Harmful Friction

**Sludge** refers to friction points deliberately or negligently embedded into customer journeys that deter customers from taking actions in their own interests — such as cancelling a product, making a complaint, switching provider, or accessing a benefit they are entitled to.

FG22/5 defines sludge through the consumer support outcome: firms must design and deliver consumer support in a way that does not create unreasonable barriers to consumers realising the benefits of products and services or acting in their interests. This includes avoiding sludge in the design of consumer journeys. **(FG22/5 §5.46)**

Sludge can arise from deliberate commercial incentive (e.g. a firm profits if customers fail to claim a rate increase) or from neglect (a firm simply fails to give adequate attention to processes where customers seek to act in ways that do not benefit the firm). Both are inconsistent with the Duty. **(FG22/5 §9.25)**

### Named Sludge Examples from FG22/5

**1. Savings rate radio button (§9.27)**
A firm increases the interest rate on a savings product. To receive the higher rate, customers must log in, navigate to an account page, and find a discreet radio button to opt in. The firm has designed this using its own behavioural analysis, which showed customers would not take these steps — so it avoids paying additional interest. A firm acting in line with the Duty would use that same behavioural evidence to design a simpler process. **(FG22/5 §9.27)**

**2. Retention phone process (§9.27)**
A firm requires customers who want to switch provider to contact it by phone. Once on the phone, customers are subjected to a lengthy retention process that discourages switching. FG22/5 specifically adds: where a product is taken out online, a visit to a branch should not normally be required to close it. Closing should generally be possible via the same channel used to open the product. **(FG22/5 §9.27)**

**3. Complex insurance claims process (§9.27)**
An insurance firm requires hard copies of all evidence for claims and refuses to waive this requirement. While firms may have legitimate claims requirements, the means of making a claim must be easy to find and the firm must not impose unreasonably restrictive, rigid or arbitrary administrative requirements that create barriers to customers making a claim. **(FG22/5 §9.27)**

**4. COVID forbearance digital sludge (§9.27)**
During forbearance implementation under the Coronavirus Tailored Support Guidance, the FCA found firms using digital tools that added friction: customers had to register and log in to more than one platform; multiple click-throughs were required to reveal relevant information; customers waited a day or more for confirmation of payment plans. These were identified as sludge practices that may prevent customers from pursuing their financial objectives. **(FG22/5 §9.27)**

## Positive Friction — Beneficial Friction

**Positive friction** refers to deliberate additional steps in a customer journey designed to protect customers from harm — by slowing decisions, ensuring adequate information is absorbed, or confirming the customer understands consequences. This does not amount to an unreasonable barrier under the consumer support outcome. **(FG22/5 §4.17)**

The commercial incentive runs against positive friction: marketing and sales teams tend to optimise for minimal clicks, fast conversion, and low drop-out rates. But streamlined sales processes without appropriate friction risk customers purchasing products they do not understand or that are not right for them. **(FG22/5 §9.23)**

### Named Positive Friction Examples from FG22/5

**1. Investment risk video (§9.24)**
A firm selling a high-risk investment product online on an execution-only basis requires customers to watch an educational video on investment risks, diversification benefits, and regulatory protections before they can purchase. While some customers may see this as unnecessary, it is designed to support informed decisions and reduce foreseeable harm. This is good practice. **(FG22/5 §9.24)**

**2. Confirmation of Payee warnings (§9.24)**
A payments firm embeds warnings and Confirmation of Payee name-checking messages into its payment processes. This is positive friction designed to prevent authorised push payment scams and misdirected payments — a consumer harm prevention measure. **(FG22/5 §9.24)**

**3. Cancellation consequence alerts (§9.24)**
Steps in a customer journey designed to ensure customers are aware of the consequences of cancelling a contract benefit customers. These are not unreasonable barriers. **(FG22/5 §9.24)**

## The "At Least as Easy to Exit as Enter" Rule

Firms must make it at least as easy for customers to switch product, leave their service, or make a change, as it is to buy in the first place. **(FG22/5 §9.26)**

This rule does not prohibit retention prompts or incentives — a firm may prompt a customer not to switch — but retention activities must not unreasonably impact the ease with which a customer could switch or exit. **(FG22/5 §9.27)**

The principle applies channel-by-channel: a product opened online should generally be closeable online. A visit to a branch should not normally be required to close a product opened digitally. Closing by phone is also permissible provided it does not involve a process designed to deter customers from acting in their own interests. **(FG22/5 §9.27)**

FG22/5 §9.55 states explicitly that the outcome rules do not require firms to guarantee processes never experience issues or delay — but where individual customers do not get the support they need due to system strain, firms must deal with this promptly and fairly and provide redress where appropriate. **(FG22/5 §9.55)**

## Applying the Distinction: How Firms Should Judge

Firms must be able to:
1. **Identify** all friction points in customer journeys.
2. **Justify and evidence the customer benefit** of each additional step — friction that cannot be justified by customer benefit is likely sludge. **(FG22/5 §9.21)**
3. **Monitor** how friction points are working in practice, using outcome data to detect whether barriers are deterring customers from acting in their interests. **(FG22/5 §9.21)**

FG22/5 is explicit that the judgement required is an objective one: what a prudent firm would consider appropriate in the circumstances, with reference to the product risk, customer characteristics, and purpose of the friction. **(FG22/5 §9.20)**

## What the Rules Do Not Require

The consumer support outcome rules do not require: **(FG22/5 §9.55)**

- Firms to guarantee their processes will never experience issues or delays; reasonable contingency processes are sufficient.
- Firms to streamline customer journeys to such an extent that customer-protecting steps are removed. Firms must be able to justify friction, but justified friction is permitted.
- Multi-channel support — firms must meet the needs of their customer base but are not required to provide all channels.

## Key Points for Agents

- Sludge is friction that deters customers from acting in their interests; positive friction is friction that prevents customers from harming themselves. The same mechanism (e.g. a required step before a transaction) can be either, depending on its purpose and design.
- The "at least as easy to exit as enter" principle is a direct, testable obligation: firms must be able to demonstrate this, and the FCA will ask about it in supervision.
- Behavioural data is double-edged: using customer behaviour analysis to design barriers (as in the savings rate example) is sludge; using it to design simpler processes is what the Duty requires.
- Retention prompts are permitted; retention processes that unreasonably impede switching are not.

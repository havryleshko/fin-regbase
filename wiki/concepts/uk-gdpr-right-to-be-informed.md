# UK GDPR — Right to Be Informed (Articles 13–14)

## Overview

The right to be informed is the foundation of the UK GDPR's transparency regime. It requires controllers to provide individuals with prescribed information about how their personal data will be used — at the point of collection, or within defined timeframes for indirectly obtained data. This obligation is typically discharged through a privacy notice.

Articles 13 and 14 set out two overlapping but distinct regimes depending on how the data was obtained:

| Regime | When it applies | Timing |
|--------|----------------|--------|
| **Article 13** | Data collected directly from the individual | At the point of collection |
| **Article 14** | Data obtained from a source other than the individual | Within 1 month of obtaining; or before first communication; or at first disclosure to a third party — whichever is earliest |

## Mandatory Content — Article 13 (Direct Collection)

When personal data is collected directly from the individual, all of the following must be provided at the time of collection:

| # | Item | Notes |
|---|------|-------|
| 1 | Controller identity and contact details | Name and address of the data controller |
| 2 | DPO contact details | Where a DPO has been appointed |
| 3 | Purposes of processing | For each specific processing activity |
| 4 | Lawful basis for each purpose | The specific Art. 6 basis (and Art. 9/10 condition if special category or criminal offence data) |
| 5 | Legitimate interests relied upon | **Required only where Art. 6(1)(f) is the basis** — must explain what the specific legitimate interests are, not merely name the basis |
| 6 | Recipients or categories of recipients | Who data will be shared with (specific names or categories acceptable) |
| 7 | International transfers | Whether data will be transferred outside the UK; the safeguard mechanisms relied upon |
| 8 | Retention period | The specific period or — where not possible — the criteria used to determine it |
| 9 | All eight data subject rights | Rights to access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, object, and automated decision-making |
| 10 | Right to withdraw consent | Required where processing is based on consent; must explain how and the consequences of withdrawal |
| 11 | Right to lodge a complaint | Right to complain to the ICO |
| 12 | Statutory or contractual requirement | Whether providing the data is a statutory or contractual requirement; consequences of not providing it |
| 13 | Automated decision-making | Existence of any automated decision-making including profiling — with **meaningful information** about the logic involved, significance, and envisaged consequences |

## Additional Items — Article 14 (Indirect Collection)

Where data is obtained from a source other than the individual, all 13 Art. 13 items apply **plus**:

| Additional item | Requirement |
|----------------|------------|
| Categories of personal data | The categories of personal data obtained |
| Source of the data | Where the data originated; specifically whether it came from publicly accessible sources |

## Timing Rules — Article 14

The Art. 14 information must be provided by the **earliest** of the following:

1. Within **1 month** of obtaining the personal data
2. At the **first communication** with the individual (if applicable)
3. At the point of **first disclosure** to a third party (if applicable)

**Financial services example:** A firm obtains credit reference data about a prospective customer from a CRA (indirect collection). The Art. 14 obligation requires the firm to inform the customer within 1 month — or before the first letter, call, or email to that customer, whichever comes first.

## The Automated Decision-Making Transparency Requirement

Item 13 in the mandatory content table (existence of automated decision-making including profiling) is a standalone transparency obligation that applies wherever a controller uses automated decision-making or profiling in any form — it is **not limited to Art. 22 processing**.

The information must be **meaningful**, which the ICO defines as:
- **Logic:** What data inputs are used; how they are processed; what the output or result is
- **Significance:** Why the automated processing matters in the context of the individual's relationship with the controller
- **Envisaged consequences:** What decision or outcome the individual can expect as a direct result of the automated processing

A generic statement such as "we use automated systems to make decisions" does not satisfy this requirement.

**For Art. 22 processing specifically:** The privacy notice must additionally describe the human review mechanism available to the individual — i.e., how they can request human intervention, express a view, and challenge an automated decision. See [[uk-gdpr-automated-decision-making]].

## Exceptions to the Right to Be Informed

### Article 13

- Where the individual already possesses all of the required information (no obligation to provide it again)

### Article 14

- **Disproportionate effort:** Where providing the information to the individual would involve a disproportionate effort (in which case public disclosure — e.g., a published privacy notice — may substitute for individual notice)
- **UK law:** Where obtaining or disclosing the data is expressly laid down in UK law
- **Professional confidentiality:** Where the personal data must remain confidential subject to a professional secrecy obligation regulated by UK law

Where an exception applies, the controller must document the reason and be able to demonstrate it to the ICO if challenged.

## Delivery Methods

The UK GDPR requires information to be provided in a "concise, transparent, intelligible and easily accessible form, using clear and plain language." There is no mandated format. Methods that the ICO treats as effective include:

| Method | Description |
|--------|------------|
| **Layered notices** | A short "headline" layer covering the most important information, linked to a full layer with complete detail. Reduces information overload without sacrificing completeness. |
| **Just-in-time notices** | Contextual disclosures at the specific moment a data collection occurs (e.g., a pop-up at checkout, a notice at login). Highly effective for delivering relevant information when it is most meaningful to the individual. |
| **Dashboards** | Persistent privacy dashboards where individuals can view, review, and manage their data and preferences at any time. |
| **Icons** | Standardised machine-readable icons can supplement text-based notices. No UK-standard icon set has been defined, but organisations may develop their own. |
| **Mobile-optimised formats** | For mobile-first products: short lead text with expandable sections. Avoids presenting a wall of text on a small screen. |

No single method satisfies all scenarios. A combination of approaches is typically required to meet the transparency standard across all touchpoints.

## Key Points for Agents

- Art. 13 (direct collection): disclosure must be provided **at the point of collection** — not retrospectively.
- Art. 14 (indirect collection): must disclose within **1 month**, or before first communication, or before first third-party disclosure — whichever is earliest.
- Art. 13 has 13 mandatory items; Art. 14 adds 2 more (categories of data obtained; source of data).
- The legitimate interests item is mandatory **only where Art. 6(1)(f) is the basis** — the controller must explain the specific legitimate interests, not just name the basis.
- Automated decision-making transparency is mandatory in all privacy notices where any automated decision-making or profiling occurs — it is not limited to Art. 22 processing. Must describe logic, significance, and envisaged consequences meaningfully.
- For Art. 22 processing, the privacy notice must additionally describe the human review and challenge mechanism.
- Art. 14 exceptions (disproportionate effort, UK law, professional confidentiality) must be documented — they are not self-applying.
- Layered notices and just-in-time disclosure are the recommended mechanisms for digital products.

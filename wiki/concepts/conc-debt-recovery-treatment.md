# CONC — Debt Recovery Treatment Standards

## What This Covers

This page covers the substantive treatment standards that govern how a firm must behave toward a customer who is in arrears, in default, or at risk of default. It draws principally from CONC 7.2 (policy obligations), CONC 7.3 (forbearance standard), CONC 7.7 (charges), CONC 7.10 (mental capacity), CONC 7.12 (lender responsibility), CONC 7.13 (data accuracy), CONC 7.14 (disputed debt), and CONC 7.15 (statute barred debt).

For CPA-specific rules, see **[[conc-cpa-rules]]**. For firm scope and jurisdiction, see **[[conc-scope-and-application]]**.

---

## Written Arrears and Vulnerability Policies — Mandatory Governance Obligation

A firm **must** establish and implement **clear, effective and appropriate** written policies and procedures for **(CONC 7.2.1R)**:
1. Dealing with customers in or approaching arrears or in default.
2. The fair and appropriate treatment of customers who the firm understands or reasonably suspects to be vulnerable.

The firm **must** review the effectiveness of these policies at appropriate intervals **(CONC 7.2.4R)**. The review must look at the full extent of support provided to customers — not just individual interactions in isolation **(CONC 7.2.5G)**.

This is a governance-level obligation. Under SM&CR, accountability for arrears and vulnerable customer treatment policy sits with the relevant Senior Manager. See **[[smcr-senior-managers-regime]]**.

Reference guidance: FCA FG21/1 (fair treatment of vulnerable customers); MALG "Good Practice Awareness Guidelines for Consumers with Mental Health Problems and Debt" **(CONC 7.2.2A G, 7.2.3G)**.

## Forbearance Standard

### The Core Obligation

**CONC 7.3.4R** is a hard rule: a firm **must** treat customers in or approaching arrears or in default with **forbearance and due consideration**.

When determining appropriate forbearance, the firm **must** take into account the **individual circumstances** of the customer of which the firm is or should be aware **(CONC 7.3.4B R)**. Generically applied policies are insufficient.

A customer is approaching arrears as soon as they **indicate they are at risk of not meeting one or more repayments** when due **(CONC 7.3.4A G)**. The firm should not wait for an actual missed payment.

### What Forbearance Looks Like

Examples of forbearance in CONC 7.3.5G (guidance, not exhaustive):
1. Suspending, reducing, waiving or cancelling further interest or charges where the customer provides evidence of financial difficulties and cannot meet repayments or can only make token payments.
2. Allowing deferment of payment of arrears where immediate payment would push repayments to an unsustainable level.
3. Accepting no payments, reduced payments, or token payments for a reasonable period from a customer who demonstrates that meeting their debts would mean failing to meet priority debts or essential living expenses.
4. Agreeing a repayment arrangement that allows a reasonable period to repay the full debt.
5. Transferring debt to an alternative credit agreement (refinancing) to reduce it over a reasonable period without adversely affecting the customer's financial situation.

**Priority debts and essential living expenses** include, but are not limited to: mortgage, rent, council tax, food, and utility bills **(CONC 7.3.5C G)**.

## Interest and Charges Freeze — Mandatory Rule During Active Arrangements

**CONC 7.3.5G R** is a hard rule distinct from the guidance examples above:

Where **(a)** a firm has put in place a repayment arrangement as a forbearance measure, **and** **(b)** the customer is **meeting the terms** of that arrangement — the firm **must** reduce, waive or cancel any further interest or charges **to the extent necessary to ensure the level of debt does not rise** for the period of that arrangement.

**Why this matters:** A customer meeting a forbearance arrangement in good faith cannot have the total debt grow through continued interest accrual. The obligation applies automatically once both conditions are met. The firm cannot require a separate request from the customer.

The required extent of the waiver may vary as circumstances change — if the customer's capacity improves and they can pay larger amounts, the firm need not waive as much **(CONC 7.3.5H G)**.

The firm must take reasonable steps to ensure forbearance provided **remains appropriate** over time **(CONC 7.3.5I R)**.

### Income and Expenditure Assessment During Forbearance

Any assessment of income and expenditure must be conducted **objectively** and should be informed by sufficiently detailed information **(CONC 7.3.5D R, 7.3.5E G)**. The Standard Financial Statement may be used as a reference for spending guidelines. The firm should make the income and expenditure record available to the customer so they can share it with other creditors **(CONC 7.3.7A G(4))**.

## Charges

| Rule | Obligation |
|---|---|
| CONC 7.7.2R | Must not claim costs of recovering a debt if there is no contractual right to do so |
| CONC 7.7.3R | Must not cause a customer to believe they are legally liable for costs where no such obligation exists |
| CONC 7.7.5R | Must not impose charges on customers in default or arrears difficulties unless the charges are no higher than necessary to cover the firm's reasonable costs |

These rules operate separately from the interest freeze obligation in CONC 7.3.5G R. CONC 7.7 addresses **what charges may lawfully be levied at all**; CONC 7.3.5G R addresses **whether accruing interest must be frozen during an active arrangement**.

## Mental Capacity — Mandatory Suspension Obligation

**CONC 7.10.1R** is a hard rule: a firm **must suspend** active pursuit of debt recovery when:
1. The firm **has been notified** that the customer might not have the mental capacity to make relevant financial decisions about the management of their debt or to engage in the debt recovery process at the time, **or**
2. The firm **understands or ought reasonably to be aware** that the customer lacks such mental capacity.

The constructive awareness standard in (2) means the firm cannot simply take a passive position — if signs of incapacity are evident, suspension is mandatory.

The firm should allow a reasonable period for the customer or someone acting on their behalf to provide evidence of the likely impact of any mental capacity limitation **(CONC 7.10.2G)**.

**Pursuit via third party:** CONC 7.10.1R does not prevent the firm from pursuing the debt through a **responsible third party acting on behalf of the customer**, where the customer has given prior consent — for example, pursuant to a registered lasting power of attorney **(CONC 7.10.3G)**.

**Policy requirement:** Firms should note CONC 7.2.1R which requires written policies for the appropriate treatment of vulnerable customers. Customers with mental health difficulties or mental capacity limitations may fall into the category of vulnerable customers **(CONC 7.2.2G, 7.10.4G)**. See **[[vulnerable-customers]]**.

## Post-Assignment Lender Responsibility

**CONC 7.12.1R** confines this section to consumer credit lenders (and firms that would be lenders but for article 60C(4A) RAO). It is not a general obligation on debt collectors.

**CONC 7.12.2R** imposes hard prohibitions on lenders. A firm **must not**:
1. Refuse to deal with a not-for-profit debt advice body, debt counsellor, debt adjuster, or another person acting on behalf of a customer, unless there is an objectively justifiable reason.
2. Refuse to accept a payment tendered by the customer or a person acting on their behalf (unless the credit agreement specifically requires payment to a third party).
3. Refuse to deal with a customer developing a repayment plan, or a third party developing a debt management plan, without justification.
4. Directly contact a customer when a person is acting on the customer's behalf, without the customer's consent, unless objectively justified.
5. Operate a policy of only freezing interest where the lender has an existing arrangement with a specific intermediary.
6. Return or refuse a repayment merely because it is tendered by a debt management firm.

**CONC 1.2.2R applies to debt collectors acting as agents:** The lender remains responsible under CONC 1.2.2R for ensuring employees and agents comply with CONC, and for taking reasonable steps to ensure other persons acting on the firm's behalf comply. Where a debt collector acts as agent or on behalf of a lender, the lender is responsible for the collector's conduct under CONC **(CONC 7.12.3G(1))**.

**Implication:** Assignment of a debt to a third-party collector does not transfer the original lender's regulatory responsibility for how that debt is collected. The original lender must take ongoing steps to ensure the collector complies with CONC.

## Data Accuracy in Debt Recovery

Before pursuing a customer, a firm **must** take reasonable steps to verify the **accuracy and adequacy** of available data to ensure the true customer is pursued for the correct amount **(CONC 7.13.4R)**. The firm must maintain accurate and adequate records of debt and repayment history **(CONC 7.13.2R)**.

Information the firm holds about a customer that must be passed to persons involved in recovery on its behalf includes **(CONC 7.13.5G)**:
- Whether the customer is in financial difficulties.
- Whether they are particularly vulnerable.
- Whether they are disputing the debt.
- Whether a repayment plan or forbearance arrangement is in place.
- Whether a representative is acting on their behalf.

Where the firm has established that an individual being pursued is not the true borrower, it **must** update its records and the data supplied to credit reference agencies **(CONC 7.13.7R)**.

Third parties engaged to recover debt or trace customers must be selected with due care, and the firm must take steps to avoid multiple simultaneous approaches to the customer by different parties **(CONC 7.13.9G, 7.13.10G)**. Third parties must have the necessary Part 4A permissions to engage in regulated activities **(CONC 7.13.13R)**.

## Disputed Debt — Mandatory Suspension

**CONC 7.14.1R:** A firm **must suspend** any steps it or its agent takes to recover a debt where the customer disputes the debt on valid grounds or what may be valid grounds.

**Valid grounds (CONC 7.14.2G):**
1. The individual being pursued is not the true borrower or hirer.
2. The debt does not exist.
3. The amount being pursued is incorrect.

**Burden of proof is on the firm (CONC 7.14.4R):** Where there is a dispute as to identity or amount, it is for the firm — not the customer — to establish that the customer is the correct person and the amount is the correct amount owed.

Where the firm investigating the dispute is a debt collector (not the lender), it must either pass the customer's information to the lender/owner, or if it has authority, investigate and notify the lender/owner of the outcome **(CONC 7.14.6R)**.

The firm **must give due consideration** to a reasonable offer of repayment **(CONC 7.14.8R)**. If rejected, a clear explanation of the reason for rejection must be given **(CONC 7.14.9R)**. Rejecting a repayment offer by intimidating the customer into increasing the offer is prohibited **(CONC 7.14.10R)**.

## Statute Barred Debts

### Limitation Periods

**CONC 7.15.1G** explains the applicable periods:
- **England, Wales, Northern Ireland:** Generally 6 years (Limitation Act 1980).
- **Scotland:** 5 years (Prescription and Limitation (Scotland) Act 1973).

**Key jurisdictional distinction (CONC 7.15.2G–7.15.3G):**
- In England, Wales and Northern Ireland, a statute barred debt **still exists** and technically remains recoverable (though subject to the restrictions below).
- In Scotland, once the prescriptive period expires and no relevant claim has been made and the debt has not been acknowledged during the period, the debt **ceases to exist and is no longer recoverable**.

### Hard Rules on Pursuit

| Rule | Obligation |
|---|---|
| CONC 7.15.4R | Must not attempt to recover a statute barred debt in England, Wales or Northern Ireland if the lender or owner has **not been in contact** with the customer during the limitation period |
| CONC 7.15.5G | If the lender/owner **has been in regular contact** during the limitation period, the firm may continue to attempt recovery |
| CONC 7.15.6R | Must not mislead the customer as to their rights and obligations in relation to the debt |
| CONC 7.15.7G | Misleading to suggest court action may be taken where the firm knows or ought to know the limitation period has expired |
| CONC 7.15.8R | Must not continue demanding payment after the customer states they will not pay because the debt is statute barred |
| CONC 7.15.9R | Must identify statute barred debts for prospective purchasers of those debts — to avoid a purchaser taking inappropriate action |
| CONC 7.15.10R | Must not initiate legal proceedings in relation to a regulated credit agreement where the lender is aware the customer has submitted a valid complaint or what appears to be a valid complaint being considered by the Financial Ombudsman Service |

**Practical implication:** A firm acquiring a debt by purchase must obtain and assess data sufficient to determine whether the limitation period has expired and whether there was prior contact during the period. A debt purchaser that pursues a statute barred debt without this diligence is in breach of CONC 7.15.4R and CONC 7.13.4R.

## Key Points for Agents

- **DMP as financial difficulty indicator and CPA restriction:** A formal debt management plan (DMP) agreement is **indicator 6** of the seven CONC 1.3.1G financial difficulty indicators. **(CONC 1.3.1G)** Once the firm knows a customer is in a DMP or otherwise in financial difficulties and cannot afford to repay, it **must not exercise CPA rights** — this is a hard rule, not guidance. **(CONC 7.6.7R)** The 30-day debt pursuit suspension (CONC 7.3.11R) applies while a debt counsellor is developing a plan; if the DMP is already signed that window has passed — the ongoing obligations are CPA suspension (CONC 7.6.7R), forbearance, and interest freeze. **(CONC 7.3; CONC 7.6.7R)**
- Forbearance is a **hard rule obligation** (CONC 7.3.4R), not discretionary. The firm must actively consider and apply appropriate forbearance based on the individual customer's circumstances. Generic policies applied uniformly are insufficient.
- Once a forbearance repayment arrangement is in place and the customer is meeting it, the firm **must** freeze interest/charges so the debt does not rise (CONC 7.3.5G R). There is no customer request requirement — the obligation is automatic.
- Mental capacity triggers a **mandatory suspension** of debt pursuit (CONC 7.10.1R). The constructive awareness standard means the firm must act on signs of incapacity, not only formal notification.
- Disputed debts trigger a **mandatory suspension** of pursuit (CONC 7.14.1R). The burden of proof — identity and amount — sits with the firm, not the customer (CONC 7.14.4R).
- A lender **remains responsible** for the conduct of debt collectors acting as its agents (CONC 7.12.3G / CONC 1.2.2R). Assigning or outsourcing the debt does not transfer the original lender's regulatory obligations.
- Statute barred debts in England/Wales/NI cannot be pursued if the lender had no contact during the limitation period (CONC 7.15.4R). In Scotland, the debt itself ceases to exist (CONC 7.15.3G). Ongoing demands after a customer invokes the statute bar are prohibited (CONC 7.15.8R).
- Before pursuing any customer, the firm must verify that the right person is being pursued for the correct amount (CONC 7.13.4R) and must share known information about vulnerability, financial difficulty, representation, or forbearance with agents conducting recovery (CONC 7.13.5G).

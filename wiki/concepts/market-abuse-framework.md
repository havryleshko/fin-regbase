# UK Market Abuse Framework — Overview

## Legislative Structure

The UK market abuse regime has two layers:

1. **UK MAR** (the UK Market Abuse Regulation, being EU Regulation 596/2014 as retained in UK law) — creates the direct prohibitions and applies to any person, not just authorised firms
2. **FCA MAR Sourcebook (MAR 1–9)** — FCA guidance and supplementary rules explaining how UK MAR operates in practice and setting out additional FCA-specific obligations (e.g., MAR 2 for price stabilisation)

UK MAR is **directly applicable** to all persons in all UK markets — it is not limited to FCA-authorised firms. An individual, a corporate, or a firm can all commit market abuse.

---

## The Five Market Abuse Behaviours

UK MAR Art. 12–15 prohibits five categories of behaviour:

| Behaviour | UK MAR Article | FCA Guidance |
|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| **Insider dealing** — using inside information to deal | Art. 8 | MAR 1.3 |
| **Unlawful disclosure** — disclosing inside information without legitimate purpose | Art. 10 | MAR 1.4 |
| **Manipulating transactions** — transactions/orders giving false impression of supply/demand/price | Art. 12(1)(a) | MAR 1.6 |
| **Manipulating devices** — fictitious devices or deception to give false impression of price/value | Art. 12(1)(b) | MAR 1.7 |
| **Dissemination** — spreading false or misleading information about instruments | Art. 12(1)(c)–(d) | MAR 1.8–1.9 |

---

## Core Principle: Intent Not Required

The single most important structural feature of UK MAR is that **intent to commit market abuse is not a required element of any of the prohibited behaviours (MAR 1.2.3G)**.

A person can commit insider dealing by accidentally trading on information they did not realise was inside information. A person can commit unlawful disclosure by sharing information they did not know was confidential. The test is objective — did the behaviour fall within the prohibited category and produce the prohibited effect?

This has direct compliance implications: systems calibrated only to catch deliberate misconduct will miss negligent and accidental violations. Policies, training, and surveillance must address the objective standard.

---

## Inside Information: The Core Definition

Inside information has four elements **(UK MAR Art. 7(1))**:
1. **Precise** — specific enough to draw a conclusion about the effect on prices
2. **Not made public** — see the "made public" test below
3. **Relates to one or more issuers or financial instruments** (or their underlying commodity)
4. **Would be likely to have a significant effect on price** if made public (the "reasonable investor" test)

### The "Made Public" Test

Information is made public if it is **(MAR 1.2.12G)**:
- Published via a Regulatory Information Service (RIS) or equivalent official channel
- Contained in publicly inspectable records
- Generally available — including via subscription, derived from public sources, or obtainable by direct observation

Two specific extensions apply **(MAR 1.2.14G–1.2.15G)**:
- Information available **only outside the UK** is still made public
- Information that requires **above-average expertise** to derive or interpret from public sources is still made public — specialist analysis of public data does not make the result non-public

## The Three Statutory Safe Harbours

Three behaviours that would otherwise constitute market abuse are permitted by UK MAR:

| Safe harbour | Basis | Key conditions |
|-------------|-------|----------------|
| **Buy-back programmes** | UK MAR Art. 5 | Pre-announced; volume/price limits; reported to FCA |
| **Price stabilisation** | UK MAR Art. 5; FCA MAR 2 | Stabilisation window; disclosure; overallotment restrictions |
| **Accepted market practices (AMPs)** | UK MAR Art. 13 | FCA-published list; conformance required for each AMP |

These are complete defences. Additionally, the **takeover/acquisition safe harbour** in UK MAR Art. 9 provides that where a person acquires inside information as a natural consequence of carrying out a takeover or merger, dealing in the target on the basis of that information may not constitute insider dealing.

---

## The Legitimate Reasons + AMP Double Test for Manipulation

For manipulating transactions (Art. 12(1)(a)), a person can rebut the allegation by demonstrating that **(MAR 1.6.5G)**:
1. The transactions were carried out for **legitimate reasons** (genuine investment activity, hedging, portfolio management); AND
2. They **conform with an accepted market practice (AMP)**

Both elements are required. Legitimate reasons alone — without AMP conformance — may not provide a complete defence. The relevant AMP must be one the FCA has formally recognised.

## Firm-Specific Obligations Under the Market Abuse Regime

Authorised firms have regulatory obligations under SYSC in addition to the UK MAR prohibitions:

- **Conflicts of interest (SYSC 10)**: Firms must use Chinese walls and other arrangements to prevent information flows that could give rise to market abuse — particularly insider dealing based on inside information held in one part of the firm
- **Systems and controls**: Firms must have arrangements to detect, prevent, and report suspicious transactions and order reports (STORs) under UK MAR Art. 16
- **SM&CR accountability**: Senior managers are accountable for the adequacy of market abuse prevention systems; failure to prevent avoidable market abuse can constitute a breach of SM&CR conduct rules

## Key Points for Agents

- **Intent is not required for any UK MAR prohibited behaviour (MAR 1.2.3G).** Compliance systems must address the objective standard, not just deliberate misconduct.
- **"Made public" is broadly defined — expertise does not make public information private (MAR 1.2.15G).** Information derivable by a market professional from public sources is public.
- **All five prohibited behaviours apply to any person, not just FCA-authorised firms.** UK MAR is directly applicable to individuals, corporates, and unregulated entities.
- **The legitimate reasons + AMP double test applies to manipulating transactions (MAR 1.6.5G).** Both elements are required for the defence; legitimate reasons alone are insufficient without formal AMP conformance.
- **Chinese walls are a relevant mitigant for dissemination (MAR 1.8.5G) and are a condition of SYSC 10 (SYSC 10.2.2R).** Chinese walls also constitute a statutory defence under FSA 2012 ss.89–91 and s.138D for SYSC 10 conflicts of interest purposes.

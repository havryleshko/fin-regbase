# Proliferation Financing — MLR Reg 18A Risk Assessment and Trade Controls

## What Proliferation Financing Is

Proliferation financing (PF) is the provision of funds or financial services used wholly or partially for the development, production, maintenance, acquisition, possession, or proliferation of **weapons of mass destruction (WMD)** — nuclear, radiological, chemical, or biological weapons, and their delivery systems. **(FCG 7.1.6; FCG 7.2.5)**

PF sits alongside financial sanctions and AML/CTF as a distinct financial crime risk. For most FCA-supervised firms, the PF risk level will be **very low** — but all firms must assess and document it. **(FCG 7.1.2)**

---

## The MLR Regulation 18A Obligation

**Money Laundering Regulations 2017, Regulation 18A** requires all MLR-regulated firms to carry out a **proliferation financing risk assessment**. This is a standalone obligation — separate from the AML/CTF risk assessment required under MLR Reg 18, though it can be produced as a single integrated document. **(FCG 7.2.5)**

The PF risk assessment must:
- Assess the firm's exposure to PF risk across its customer base, products, delivery channels, and geographies
- Be proportionate to the nature, scale, and complexity of the firm's activities
- Be kept current — updated when the firm enters new jurisdictions, introduces new products, or when new PF risk events emerge
- Demonstrably inform the design of controls (as with the AML risk assessment)

**For most firms:** A brief, documented assessment concluding that PF risk is low — with the rationale for that conclusion — will be sufficient. Absence of any assessment is the compliance failure.

## Trade Controls and Dual-Use Goods

Alongside financial sanctions, the UK government imposes controls on trade in goods and services that could be used in WMD programmes. The export of goods and services for use in nuclear, radiological, chemical or biological weapons programmes is subject to strict export licensing controls. **(FCG 7.1.6)**

**Aiding proliferators is a criminal offence** under the **Anti-Terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001**.

**Dual-use goods** are commercial goods that can also be used for weapons development. The **Export Control Organisation (ECO)** maintains lists of goods requiring an export licence — both military items and dual-use items.

### Trade Finance and PF Risk

For firms with trade finance activities, PF risk is a specific concern: **(FCG 7.2.5)**

- Finance of trade with high-risk countries may involve goods subject to export restrictions or end-users of concern
- Enhanced due diligence is required on counterparties and goods where trade involves high-risk jurisdictions
- Where doubt exists about the legitimacy of a trade, firms should seek evidence from exporters that the trade is lawful and that appropriate assurances have been obtained from relevant government authorities

FCTR 15 (FCA thematic review of banks' management of financial crime risk in trade finance) contains additional guidance on sanctions procedures and dual-use goods in a trade finance context. **(FCG 7.3.1)**

## Counter-Terrorism Act 2008 — Treasury Direction Powers

HM Treasury can use powers under the **Counter-Terrorism Act 2008** to **direct financial firms to cease business** with specific customers involved in proliferation activity or terrorism financing. This is a direct legislative mechanism distinct from financial sanctions — it can be triggered without the customer being on the Consolidated List. **(FCG 7.1.6)**

Good practice includes firms having considered in advance how they would respond if such a direction were issued against one of their customers. **(FCG 7.2.5)**

---

## Good and Poor Practice (FCG 7.2.5)

| Good practice | Poor practice |
|---------------|--------------|
| Bank identifies customers exporting goods to high-risk jurisdictions and subjects transactions to enhanced scrutiny (checking whether goods may be export-restricted or end-users may be of concern) | Firm assumes customers selling goods to countries of concern will have verified the exports are legitimate; does not ask for evidence |
| Where doubt exists, bank asks customer to demonstrate appropriate assurances from relevant government authorities | Firm knows customers deal with individuals from high-risk jurisdictions but does not communicate with them about relevant regulations |
| Firm has considered how to respond if HM Treasury acts under Counter-Terrorism Act 2008 against a customer | — |

## Key External Sources for PF Guidance

FCG 7 designates the following as the primary sources for operational PF guidance: **(FCG 7.4.2)**

| Source | What it covers |
|--------|---------------|
| **JMLSG Part III** (chapter on proliferation financing) | The FCA's designated chief operational source for PF guidance for financial services firms |
| **FATF Typologies Report on Proliferation Financing** | Red flag indicators and typologies |
| **FATF PF Risk Assessment and Mitigation Guidance** | Methodology for firm-level PF risk assessment |
| **Export Control Organisation (ECO)** | Lists of goods requiring export licences (military and dual-use) |
| **OTSI (Office of Trade Sanctions Implementation)** | Trade sanctions guidance (distinct from financial sanctions; OTSI sits within DBT) |
| **NCA** | Guidance on reporting proliferation financing suspicions via SARs |
| **HM Government National Risk Assessment of Proliferation Financing** | National-level PF risk landscape |

## Relationship to AML/CTF Framework

PF is referenced in the AML/CTF framework at **MLR Reg 18A** (standalone PF risk assessment) and implicitly in the general risk assessment requirement at MLR Reg 18. The key distinctions: **(aml-ctf-framework.md; FCG 7.2.5)**

- **MLR Reg 18** — AML/CTF risk assessment: covers money laundering and terrorist financing risk
- **MLR Reg 18A** — PF risk assessment: covers proliferation financing risk; a separate document or an integrated section

Both must be documented. The PF risk assessment requirement was introduced as part of the UK's implementation of FATF Recommendation 1 and the FATF's 2020 guidance on PF risk.

---

## Key Points for Agents

- Proliferation financing risk assessment is a **standalone MLR Reg 18A obligation** — not a subset of the AML risk assessment, though they can be integrated in one document.
- **All MLR-regulated firms** must carry out a PF risk assessment. For most, risk will be low, but absence of any documented assessment is the compliance failure.
- Aiding proliferators (assisting with WMD programmes through trade) is a **criminal offence** under the Anti-Terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001.
- HM Treasury can **direct firms to cease business** with specific customers under the Counter-Terrorism Act 2008 — this is a direct power distinct from financial sanctions designations.
- **JMLSG Part III** is the FCA's designated primary operational source for PF guidance — not FCG 7 itself.
- Trade finance firms face heightened PF risk: dual-use goods, high-risk jurisdiction counterparties, and end-user verification are key concerns.

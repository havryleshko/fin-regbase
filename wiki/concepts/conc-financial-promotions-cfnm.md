# CONC — Financial Promotions: Clear, Fair and Not Misleading Standard

## What This Covers

CONC 3.3.1R establishes the overarching content standard for all CONC-regulated financial promotions: the clear, fair and not misleading (CFNM) test. This standard applies in addition to — not instead of — the specific mandatory content rules elsewhere in CONC 3 (representative example, APR, risk warnings). A promotion can satisfy every specific rule and still breach CONC 3.3.1R if it is overall misleading.

---

## The Five-Element Standard (CONC 3.3.1R)

A financial promotion must be:

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| **(a) Identifiable** | Clearly identifiable as a financial promotion — a customer must be able to recognise they are being marketed to |
| **(b) Accurate** | Accurate — not emphasising potential benefits without also giving a fair and prominent indication of the relevant risks and consequences |
| **(c) Balanced** | Balanced — risks and consequences are fairly and prominently shown alongside any benefit or feature being promoted |
| **(d) Sufficient** | Sufficient for the target audience — the information must enable the typical customer in the target audience to understand the key features and risks of the product |
| **(e) Not concealing** | Not designed or likely to disguise, diminish, or conceal material information that a customer would need to make an informed decision |

All five elements must be satisfied. A promotion is not saved because it scores well on four of them.

---

## Comparisons (CONC 3.3.1R(1B))

Where a financial promotion includes a comparison with another product, provider, or rate, the comparison must separately be:
- **Fair:** not constructed to be artificially favourable to the promoted product
- **Balanced:** presenting both sides of the comparison honestly
- **Meaningful:** comparing like with like on a consistent basis

A comparison between a promotional introductory rate and a competitor's standard rate, without disclosing the promotional nature of the rate, is an example of a comparison that is accurate in isolation but unfair and misleading as presented.

---

## No Credit Regardless — CONC 3.3.3R

A financial promotion must not state or imply that credit is available **regardless of financial status, income, or other commitments**. This prohibition applies even where the literal words used are softened — for example, claims like "loans for everyone", "all credit histories considered", or "no questions asked" are likely to contravene CONC 3.3.3R if they suggest credit availability is unconditional.

---

## BNPL and Promotional Rates — CONC 3.3.11AR

A financial promotion for a credit agreement that features a promotional interest rate — including buy-now-pay-later with an interest-free period — **must not omit material risk information**. Specifically:

- The promotion must make clear that the promotional rate is time-limited
- The promotion must make clear that interest will be charged on the outstanding balance if it is not cleared before the promotional period ends
- The rate at which interest will be charged after the promotional period must be disclosed or signposted

A promotion that presents only the "0% for 12 months" element without any indication of the post-promotional rate or repayment obligation breaches CONC 3.3.11AR.

---

## Overdraft and Non-Business Credit — Must Use "Borrowing" (CONC 3.3.12R)

A financial promotion for an overdraft — or any non-business credit product that is available to customers — must use the word **"borrowing"** wherever the promotion refers to money being available, accessible, or usable. Terms like "funds available", "headroom", or "buffer" used in place of "borrowing" without the word itself appearing contravene CONC 3.3.12R.

---

## Nine Unfair Practice Examples (CONC 3.3.10G)

The following are named examples of promotions likely to contravene CONC 3.3.1R. They are guidance (G), not a closed list:

1. **Speed without risk context:** Emphasising how quickly credit can be obtained without giving appropriate prominence to the cost and risk of that credit.
2. **Ease of application without cost disclosure:** Promoting how simple it is to apply without disclosing material costs or the representative APR.
3. **False urgency:** Creating a false impression of scarcity or urgency ("offer ends today", "limited places") where no genuine limit exists.
4. **Exploiting vulnerability:** Targeting a promotion in a way that exploits the circumstances of customers likely to be vulnerable.
5. **Concealing repayment obligations:** Presenting repayments in isolation from total cost — for example, showing only weekly amounts without the total cost of credit.
6. **Disguising the true nature of the product:** Presenting a high-cost product using language or imagery more consistent with a lower-cost product category.
7. **Small print key terms:** Placing material information — such as the total cost of credit, the APR, or a mandatory condition — in small print or in a location where a customer is unlikely to see it.
8. **Selective omission in digital formats:** Using interactive or truncated formats to conceal the unfavourable aspects of a product while the favourable aspects are given screen prominence.
9. **Implication of endorsement:** Using language or imagery that implies regulatory or third-party endorsement that does not exist.

---

## "Prominent" — The Standard (CONC 3.2.1G)

Throughout CONC 3, requirements for information to be "prominent" are assessed against the standard: **would a requirement draw the attention of an average customer?** This is objective — the average customer for the target audience of that specific promotion, not a universal benchmark. For a promotion directed at financially distressed customers, the benchmark is calibrated to that group.

Prominence is assessed holistically — size, position, contrast, and sequence all matter. A risk warning buried at the end of a lengthy digital page in small text is unlikely to be prominent even if it is technically present.

---

## Approver Responsibility

A firm that approves a financial promotion under s.21 FSMA / s.55NA FCA permission accepts responsibility for the promotion meeting CONC 3.3.1R — and all other applicable CONC 3 requirements. The approver cannot disclaim responsibility by pointing to the originating firm's content choices. Approver firms must implement a process sufficient to satisfy themselves of compliance with CONC 3 before approving **(CONC 3.2.3G)**.

---

## Key Points for Agents

- CONC 3.3.1R five-element CFNM test: identifiable; accurate; balanced/risks fairly shown; sufficient for target audience; not concealing material information. All five must be satisfied.
- The test is a floor — specific rules (APR, representative example, risk warnings) stack on top. Meeting specific rules does not guarantee CFNM compliance.
- Comparisons (3.3.1R(1B)) must be fair, balanced, and meaningful independently of the general CFNM test.
- "No credit regardless" (3.3.3R): any implication that credit is unconditionally available breaches this rule — including soft language like "all histories considered."
- BNPL / promotional rates (3.3.11AR): must disclose the post-promotional rate and repayment consequence of not clearing the balance — promotion of the benefit alone is a breach.
- "Borrowing" (3.3.12R): overdraft and non-business credit promotions must use this word when referencing available funds — substitutes do not satisfy the rule.
- "Prominent" is assessed against the average customer in the target audience, not universally — a distressed-customer audience sets a more demanding prominence standard.

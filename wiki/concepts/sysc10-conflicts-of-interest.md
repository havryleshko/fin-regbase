# SYSC 10 — Conflicts of Interest

## What This Document Covers

SYSC 10 is the FCA Handbook chapter that sets out obligations to identify, manage, and — only as a last resort — disclose conflicts of interest. It applies to all authorised firms providing services to clients. The chapter is organised around a strict three-stage sequence: identification is mandatory, management is the primary remedy, and disclosure is permitted only when management is insufficient. Departing from this sequence — for example, by issuing a conflicts notice without first attempting management — is itself a breach.

---

## SYSC 10.1.1R — Scope

SYSC 10 applies to all common platform firms providing investment services or ancillary services to clients, and to equivalent obligations for non-common platform firms under analogous sourcebooks (e.g. COBS). For firms subject to MiFID II, SYSC 10 gives effect to the MiFID II conflicts of interest framework. The chapter applies regardless of client category — retail, professional, or eligible counterparty — in all cases where a service is being provided. **(SYSC 10.1.1R)**

---

## SYSC 10.1.3R — The Identification Duty

The duty to identify conflicts is **absolute and prospective** — it is not triggered by a conflict having occurred. Firms must take all reasonable steps to identify conflicts of interest that arise or may arise in the course of providing investment services or ancillary services, between: **(SYSC 10.1.3R)**

- The **firm** (including managers, employees, tied agents, and connected persons) **and a client**
- **One client and another client**

The duty applies at the level of the firm as an enterprise, not merely at the transactional or client-relationship level. A firm cannot argue it was unaware of a conflict that could have been identified through reasonable review of its activities and organisational structure.

---

## SYSC 10.1.4R — The Six Conflict Types

SYSC 10.1.4R lists six specific types of situation that must be assessed when identifying whether a conflict exists or may exist. These are not exhaustive — they are the minimum assessment framework: **(SYSC 10.1.4R)**

| # | Conflict type |
|---|---------------|
| 1 | The firm or a connected person is likely to make a **financial gain or avoid a financial loss at the expense of the client** |
| 2 | The firm or a connected person has an **interest in the outcome** of a service or transaction on behalf of the client that is distinct from the client's own interest |
| 3 | The firm or a connected person has a **financial or other incentive to favour** the interest of another client or group of clients over the interests of the client |
| 4 | The firm or a connected person **carries on the same business** as the client (proprietary activities running alongside client-service activities) |
| 5 | The firm or a connected person **receives an inducement** in relation to a service provided to the client — in the form of money, goods, or services — other than the standard commission or fee for that service |
| 6 | Any other situation where the firm's or connected person's interests conflict or may conflict with those of the client |

Type 5 (inducements) is the SYSC 10 basis for the FCA's PFOF prohibition framework — see [[payment-for-order-flow]]. Type 4 captures proprietary trading conflicts in investment banking contexts.

### SYSC 10.1.5G — Conflict = Duty Conflict, Not Mere Benefit

A conflict of interest does not arise simply because the firm or a connected person benefits from a transaction. The relevant question is whether the firm's interests are **in conflict with a duty owed to the client**, or whether a duty owed to one client conflicts with a duty owed to another client. The mere fact that the firm earns a fee or commission does not, without more, constitute a conflict. **(SYSC 10.1.5G)**

## The Mandatory Governance Infrastructure

### SYSC 10.1.6R — Conflicts Register

Firms must maintain and regularly update a **written record of all types of conflicts** that arise or may arise from the provision of investment or ancillary services. This conflicts register is not a static document — it must reflect the firm's current activities and be updated when new products, services, client segments, or organisational changes introduce new conflict potential. **(SYSC 10.1.6R)**

### SYSC 10.1.6AAR — Annual Senior Management Report

Separately from the conflicts register, firms must produce **at least an annual written report to senior management** covering: (i) conflicts identified during the period; (ii) how they were managed; and (iii) disclosures made to clients. **(SYSC 10.1.6AAR)**

This is a discrete governance obligation — a firm that maintains an up-to-date register but fails to surface conflicts to senior management in a structured annual report breaches SYSC 10.1.6AAR. The report is the mechanism by which senior management exercises oversight, and its absence is a specific FCA supervisory focus.

---

## The Identify → Manage → Disclose Sequence

### SYSC 10.1.7R — Manage First

Where a conflict is identified, the firm's primary obligation is to **manage it** — that is, to take organisational or procedural steps to prevent the conflict from adversely affecting the client's interest. Management may take the form of: **(SYSC 10.1.7R)**

- Declining to act for a particular client
- Separating functions (physical, informational, or organisational separation)
- Removing financial incentives that create the conflict
- Escalating to an independent oversight function
- Independent supervision or compliance oversight of affected activities

### SYSC 10.1.8R and SYSC 10.1.9AR — Disclosure Is the Last Resort

Disclosure to the client is permitted only where the **management arrangements are not sufficient** to ensure with reasonable confidence that the client's interests will not be adversely affected. **(SYSC 10.1.8R)**

SYSC 10.1.9AR makes this explicit: disclosure is the **last resort**, not an alternative to management. A firm cannot skip the management step and proceed directly to disclosing a conflict. Issuing a conflicts disclosure notice while having failed to implement (or even consider) management arrangements is a standalone breach of SYSC 10.1.9AR, irrespective of whether the disclosure itself is accurate.

The FCA's supervisory position is that disclosure-only frameworks — where firms rely on a generic conflicts notice in their terms of business without a supporting management programme — are non-compliant. **(FCA PFOF Supervisory Report 2019)**

## SYSC 10.1.10R — Mandatory Written Conflicts Policy

All firms must establish, implement, and maintain a **written conflicts of interest policy** appropriate to their size, organisation, and activities. **(SYSC 10.1.10R)**

The policy must be implemented in practice — unwritten conventions that function effectively in practice do not satisfy the written policy requirement. The FCA found in the PFOF supervisory review that protocols governing how firms managed PFOF structures were not captured in their written conflicts policies, even where the arrangements operated in practice. See [[fca-pfof-supervisory-report-2019]].

### SYSC 10.1.11R — Six Named Policy Measures

The written policy must address, at minimum, six measures to manage the types of conflicts identified: **(SYSC 10.1.11R)**

1. **Information barriers** — preventing the exchange of information between persons whose interests may conflict
2. **Separate supervision** — supervised by different managers where persons have different interests at stake
3. **Removal of direct financial linkage** — eliminating incentive structures that reward one person or business unit at the expense of a client
4. **Prevention of inappropriate influence** — over the way a person carries out services where a conflict would result
5. **Prohibition on simultaneous or sequential participation** — in separate investment or ancillary services where the participation could give rise to the identified conflict
6. **Any other measure** reasonably expected to prevent or manage identified conflicts

### SYSC 10.1.11AAR — Annual Policy Review

The conflicts policy must be reviewed **at least annually** and whenever a material change occurs that could affect its adequacy. The annual review must be documented, and findings must be addressed. **(SYSC 10.1.11AAR)**

## SYSC 10.2.2R — Chinese Walls: Statutory Defence

Chinese walls established under SYSC 10.2 — that is, arrangements that restrict the flow of information between different parts of a firm where that flow would give rise to a conflict of interest — constitute a **formal statutory defence**. **(SYSC 10.2.2R)**

The statutory effect is twofold:

1. **Market abuse offences (FSA 2012 ss.89(2)/90(1)/91(1)):** An individual on the restricted side of the wall is not treated as having possessed information withheld from them. This means the wall provides a complete defence against allegations of market abuse based on the firm possessing inside information, provided the wall is properly established and maintained.

2. **FCA rules (s.138D FSMA):** The same protection applies for the purposes of FCA rules — individuals restricted by the wall are not treated as in breach of rules that would require them to act on, or disclose, the withheld information.

This is the only mechanism in SYSC 10 that provides a **statutory** (as opposed to regulatory) defence. The practical implication is that firms with proprietary trading desks, investment banking, and asset management under the same roof — where inside information routinely exists on one side — must establish Chinese walls under SYSC 10.2 to protect both the firm and individuals against market abuse liability.

A Chinese wall is not effective simply by declaration. It requires:
- Physical or logical separation of information flows
- Documented procedures for restricted persons
- Trained personnel who understand what information is restricted
- Clear escalation channels for inadvertent disclosure
- Audit trail confirming the wall was operational at the relevant time

## Key Points for Agents

- SYSC 10 imposes a **strict sequencing rule**: identify conflicts (SYSC 10.1.3R — absolute, prospective) → manage them (SYSC 10.1.7R — primary obligation) → disclose only if management is insufficient (SYSC 10.1.9AR — last resort). Disclosure is not an alternative to management.
- The conflicts register (SYSC 10.1.6R) and the annual senior management report (SYSC 10.1.6AAR) are **two separate obligations**: the register tracks current conflicts; the report surfaces them to senior management on at least an annual cycle. Having one does not satisfy the other. A **verbal briefing to senior management does not satisfy SYSC 10.1.6AAR** — the obligation requires a written report, at least annually. An 18-month gap without register review or update is likely a breach regardless of whether new conflicts have arisen, because the firm cannot demonstrate active monitoring for changes.
- **Conflicts policy annual review (SYSC 10.1.11AAR):** The written conflicts policy must be reviewed **at least annually** and whenever a material change occurs. A firm that has not reviewed its conflicts register or conflicts policy in 18 months may be in breach of both SYSC 10.1.6AAR (annual written report) and SYSC 10.1.11AAR (annual policy review) simultaneously — these are discrete obligations. **(SYSC 10.1.6AAR; SYSC 10.1.11AAR)**
- The written conflicts policy (SYSC 10.1.10R) must capture management arrangements **in writing**. Effective but unwritten protocols do not satisfy the requirement. The FCA specifically found this deficiency in PFOF arrangements.
- The six conflict types in SYSC 10.1.4R include inducements from third parties (Type 5) — the SYSC 10 basis for the FCA's PFOF prohibition framework.
- Chinese walls under SYSC 10.2.2R give a **formal statutory defence** under FSA 2012 ss.89–91 and s.138D — individuals on the restricted side are not treated as possessing withheld information. This is the only SYSC 10 mechanism with statutory (not just regulatory) protection.

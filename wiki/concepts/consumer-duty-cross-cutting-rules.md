# FCA Consumer Duty — Cross-Cutting Rules

## What They Are

The three cross-cutting rules sit between Principle 12 and the four outcome rules. They define the **standard of conduct** firms must meet across all activity within scope of the Duty. They apply at both target market and individual customer level, depending on the situation. **(PS22/9 §5.1, §5.15)**

The three rules are: **(PS22/9 §5.1)**

1. Act in good faith towards retail customers
2. Avoid causing foreseeable harm to retail customers
3. Enable and support retail customers to pursue their financial objectives

## Rule 1: Act in Good Faith

**Definition:** A standard of conduct characterised by **honesty, fair and open dealing, and acting consistently with the reasonable expectations of retail customers**. **(PS22/9 §5.5)**

This requires firms to consider what customers would reasonably expect given the nature and quality of the product or service, and the firm's previous conduct or interactions with consumers.

**What it covers:** Applies at every stage of the customer relationship — product design, marketing, sale, and ongoing management. Includes an obligation to proactively consider whether remedial action (including redress) is appropriate where customers have suffered harm through the firm's action or inaction. **(PS22/9 §5.8)**

### Bad-Faith Patterns Identified by FG22/5

FG22/5 provides specific examples of conduct that constitutes a failure to act in good faith. **(FG22/5 §5.12–§5.14)**

**At product design stage:**
- Designing features to exploit customers' behavioural biases (e.g. tendency to overvalue immediate benefits and discount future costs like renewal or exit fees) in order to create demand for a product. **(FG22/5 §5.9, §5.12)**
- Using algorithms, machine learning or AI within products in ways that embed or amplify bias, leading to systematically worse outcomes for some groups of customers — unless the differences can be objectively justified. **(FG22/5 §5.12)**
- Adding product variations specifically to make products harder to compare with competitors. **(FG22/5 §5.12)**
- Designing products in which pricing and charges are not presented in a way that makes total cost easy for consumers to understand. **(FG22/5 §5.12)**

**In communications:**
- Promoting products in a way that misleads customers about benefits or risks — including burying key terms in documents or web pages the firm knows customers are unlikely to read. **(FG22/5 §5.13)**
- Inappropriately exploiting consumers' biases to create demand or take advantage of customers in difficult or stressful situations. **(FG22/5 §5.13)**
- Presenting customers with incomplete, distorted or skewed information. **(FG22/5 §5.13)**

**In consumer support:**
- Operating systems or processes that the firm knows frustrate or prevent customers from enjoying use of their products — including designing websites or apps to manipulate or subvert consumer choices through harmful leading questions. **(FG22/5 §5.14)**

**On culture:**
- Using staff incentives, performance management or remuneration structures that are likely to cause customer detriment. A firm is unlikely to act in good faith if its incentive structures push staff conduct towards detriment. **(FG22/5 §5.10)**

**Practical test:** Would a reasonable customer, knowing what this firm knows, regard this conduct as honest and consistent with their expectations?

## Rule 2: Avoid Causing Foreseeable Harm

**Definition:** Firms must avoid causing foreseeable harm to retail customers — through **action or inaction**, and through their **role in the distribution chain**. **(PS22/9 §5.9–§5.11)**

Key points from the FCA:

- Firms are **not required** to protect customers from risks they understood and accepted (e.g. investment risk, explicitly disclosed). **(PS22/9 §5.11)**
- A firm can cause foreseeable harm through what it does directly, or through its position in the chain — but it is not responsible for the actions of other firms in the chain. **(PS22/9 §5.9)**
- "Foreseeable" is **dynamic** — firms must stay current with new or emerging harm through complaints, MI, press, and FCA supervisory communications. **(PS22/9 §5.11)**
- Ineffective disclosure does not discharge this obligation. Simply telling customers about a risk is not the same as ensuring they understood and accepted it. **(PS22/9 §5.11)**

### FG22/5 Foreseeable Harm Examples

FG22/5 §5.23 provides a non-exhaustive list of examples of foreseeable harm:

- Consumers being unable to cancel a product or service that is no longer right for them because the firm's processes are unclear or difficult to navigate. **(FG22/5 §5.23)**
- Products and services performing poorly because they were not tested in a range of market scenarios to understand how consumers would be affected. **(FG22/5 §5.23)**
- Products distributed widely to customers for whom they were not designed — i.e. an inappropriate distribution strategy causing harm. **(FG22/5 §5.23)**
- Consumers incurring overly high charges because they do not understand the charging structure or how it impacts the value of the product. **(FG22/5 §5.23)**
- Consumers incurring total investment costs that are likely to outweigh expected above-cash returns. **(FG22/5 §5.23)**
- Consumers with characteristics of vulnerability being unable to access or use a product or service properly because customer support is inaccessible to them. **(FG22/5 §5.23)**
- Consumers becoming victims of scams due to inadequate systems to detect or prevent them, or poorly designed scam warning messages. **(FG22/5 §5.23)**
- Consumers finding it too difficult to switch to a better product or different provider because the process is too onerous or unclear. **(FG22/5 §5.23)**

Firms must also proactively consider how consumers' behavioural biases — such as inertia — might lead their products or services to cause foreseeable harm. **(FG22/5 §5.25)**

**Practical test:** Given what this firm knows today, could it foresee that its conduct (or inaction) may cause harm to retail customers? If so, the firm must act.

## Rule 3: Enable and Support Customers to Pursue Financial Objectives

**Definition:** Firms must take responsibility for creating an environment in which consumers can act in their own interests. Customers remain ultimately responsible for their own decisions. **(PS22/9 §5.14)**

Key points:

- The extent of a firm's obligation depends on its role and knowledge of the customer. An **advisory firm** must understand individual customer objectives and act on that knowledge; a **non-advisory firm** must equip customers with information to make effective decisions without crossing the advice boundary. **(PS22/9 §5.13)**
- This rule applies proportionately to what is within the firm's control — firms are not required to carry out activities beyond what could reasonably be expected of them in their role. **(PS22/9 §5.14)**
- Firms must understand and account for **behavioural biases** and the impact of vulnerability characteristics on customer decisions. **(PS22/9 §5.14)**

### Signposting for Declined Customers

Where a firm declines to provide a product or service, it should still consider whether there is information or support it could provide to help the customer pursue their financial objectives elsewhere. For example, a firm that declines credit could signpost the customer to a reliable third-party resource such as the MoneyHelper guide. **(FG22/5 §5.40)**

Travel insurance firms are already required to signpost customers with pre-existing medical conditions to a directory of specialist insurers. FG22/5 states that firms in other markets should consider a similar approach when declining customers — signposting to a reliable source of information that can help customers achieve their financial objectives is consistent with the Duty. **(FG22/5 §5.41)**

**Practical test:** Is this firm creating conditions in which its customers can understand their options, make effective choices, and act in their own financial interests — within the scope of the firm's role?

## How the Cross-Cutting Rules Interact with the Four Outcomes

The cross-cutting rules are **not separate tests** — they define the conduct standard that applies when assessing whether each of the four outcomes is being met. A firm that breaches a cross-cutting rule will typically be in breach of one or more outcome rules simultaneously.

## Application at Target Market vs Individual Level

The cross-cutting rules apply at both levels: **(PS22/9 §5.15)**

- **Target market level:** Product design, marketing, pricing decisions — a firm avoids causing foreseeable harm to its target market by designing products appropriately.
- **Individual customer level:** One-to-one interactions, bespoke services, individual circumstances including vulnerability — a firm supports an individual customer to pursue their financial objectives by tailoring its response.

## Supervisory Findings — Culture and Proactivity (February 2024 Implementation Review)

The February 2024 implementation review identified two named good-faith and governance failures that bear directly on the cross-cutting rules.

### "Wait and See" Behaviour

Firms waiting to see if the FCA will intervene before addressing a known issue rather than tackling it themselves. The FCA states this is likely to cause greater complexity in the long run, particularly where consumer redress becomes due. The Duty requires firms to **proactively** identify and address risks of harm — passivity in the face of a known issue is a breach of the good faith and foreseeable harm rules. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**

### Duty Ownership Siloed in Compliance

Where the Duty is driven primarily by programme or compliance teams and not discussed at board level, the FCA treats this as evidence that culture, leadership and governance drivers are not functioning. Firms must ensure responsibility for good customer outcomes is understood across the whole business — from product design through to post-sale support — and that it is discussed at senior leadership and board level. **(FCA implementation review, February 2024)**

## Key Points for Agents

- **Three cross-cutting rules contained in PRIN 2A (PS22/9):** (1) act in good faith towards retail customers; (2) avoid causing foreseeable harm to retail customers; (3) enable and support retail customers to pursue their financial objectives. All three apply simultaneously. **(PRIN 2A; PS22/9 §5.1)**
- Three cross-cutting rules: act in good faith; avoid causing foreseeable harm; enable customers to pursue financial objectives. All three apply simultaneously.
- "Foreseeable harm" includes harm caused through inaction and through a firm's role in the distribution chain — not only direct, active harm.
- Foreseeable harm is dynamic — firms must monitor complaints, MI and FCA communications to stay current on emerging harms.
- Disclosure alone does not discharge the foreseeable harm rule — ineffective disclosure that customers did not understand does not constitute acceptance of risk.
- The cross-cutting rules apply at both target market and individual customer level depending on the context.
- Specific bad-faith patterns named by FG22/5 include: exploiting behavioural biases, using algorithms that amplify bias, adding product complexity to hinder comparison, burying key terms, and designing sludge into support journeys.
- Where a firm declines a customer, it should consider signposting them to reliable information or third-party resources.
- "Wait and see" behaviour — knowing of an issue and waiting for the FCA to intervene — is a named breach of the proactivity obligation under the cross-cutting rules.
